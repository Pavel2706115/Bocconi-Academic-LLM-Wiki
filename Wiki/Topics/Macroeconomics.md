# Macroeconomics

## Overview

Macroeconomics (30066) studies the behaviour of the aggregate economy — output, unemployment, inflation, interest rates — and the policies that affect them. Based on Blanchard, Amighini & Giavazzi. The course covers short-run (IS-LM), medium-run (Phillips Curve, labor market), and extends to expectations and open economy.

## Core Concepts

- [[Wiki/Concepts/Gross-Domestic-Product|Gross Domestic Product (GDP)]] — Measuring aggregate output and income
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Aggregate-Supply-and-Demand|Aggregate Supply and Demand]] — The goods market and multiplier
- [[Wiki/Concepts/IS-LM-Model|IS-LM Model]] — Joint equilibrium in goods and money markets
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Phillips-Curve|Phillips Curve]] — Inflation-unemployment tradeoff
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Labor-Market-Macro|Labor Market]] — Wage setting, price setting, and natural unemployment
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Fiscal-Policy|Fiscal Policy]] — Government spending and taxation effects
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Monetary-Policy-Macro|Monetary Policy]] — Central bank interest rate policy and money supply
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Expectations-in-Macro|Expectations in Macroeconomics]] — How expectations affect bonds, stocks, consumption, and investment
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Open-Economy-Macro|Open Economy]] — Exchange rates, trade, and international capital flows

## Key Takeaways

- GDP, inflation, and unemployment are the three core macro indicators
- The IS-LM model shows how fiscal and monetary policy affect output and interest rates simultaneously
- The Phillips Curve reveals the short-run tradeoff between inflation and unemployment
- Expectations play a crucial role in financial markets, consumption, and investment decisions
- In an open economy, exchange rates and trade balance add additional policy dimensions
