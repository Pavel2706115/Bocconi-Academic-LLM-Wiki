# Microeconomics

## Overview

Microeconomics (30065) studies how individual agents — consumers, firms, and governments — make decisions about resource allocation. The course covers competitive markets, consumer and producer theory, market structures (competition to monopoly), game theory, and market failures.

## Core Concepts

- [[Wiki/Concepts/Supply-and-Demand|Supply and Demand]] — Market demand/supply curves, shifts, and equilibrium
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Elasticity|Elasticity]] — Price, income, and cross-price elasticity of demand and supply
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Consumer-Theory|Consumer Theory]] — Utility maximization, budget constraints, and indifference curves
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Producer-Theory|Producer Theory]] — Production functions, cost curves, and profit maximization
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Perfect-Competition|Perfect Competition]] — Price-taking firms and long-run equilibrium
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Monopoly|Monopoly]] — Single-seller markets, price discrimination, and deadweight loss
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Oligopoly-and-Game-Theory|Oligopoly and Game Theory]] — Strategic interaction, Nash equilibrium, and Cournot/Bertrand models
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Externalities-and-Market-Failures|Externalities and Market Failures]] — When markets fail to achieve efficiency
- [[Wiki/Concepts/Market-Equilibrium|Market Equilibrium]] — Price determination and comparative statics

## Key Takeaways

- Markets allocate resources efficiently under perfect competition, but market failures justify intervention
- Consumer and producer surplus measure welfare gains from trade
- Game theory models strategic interactions where one agent's payoff depends on others' actions
- Elasticity determines how responsive quantity is to changes in price, income, or related goods
