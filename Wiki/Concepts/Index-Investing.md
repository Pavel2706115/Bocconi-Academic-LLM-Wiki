# Index Investing

**Index Investing** (or **Passive Investing**) is a buy-and-hold investment strategy that aims to replicate the performance of a broad market index (such as the S&P 500 or MSCI World). Rather than attempting to "beat the market" through active security selection or market timing, index investing accepts the market return, minimizing costs and maximizing long-term compounding efficiency.

---

## 1. The Rationale Behind Passive Indexing

The academic and practical success of index investing rests on four core pillars:

### Cost Efficiency
Active mutual funds charge high management fees (expense ratios typically ranging from $0.75\%$ to $1.50\%$) and incur substantial transaction costs due to high portfolio turnover. Index funds operate passively, resulting in extremely low expense ratios (often below $0.05\%$) and negligible transaction costs.

### Broad Diversification
By holding every security in an index, the passive investor eliminates **idiosyncratic (unsystematic) risk**—the risk associated with individual companies. The investor is left with only **systematic (market) risk**, which cannot be diversified away.

### Tax Efficiency
Because passive index funds buy and hold securities, they trigger fewer taxable events. Active funds frequently realize and distribute capital gains to shareholders, compounding the drag of taxes on long-term returns.

### The Arithmetic of Active Management
William F. Sharpe (1991) mathematically demonstrated the necessity of passive outperformance:
1.  Before costs, the return of the average actively managed dollar must equal the return of the average passively managed dollar (since both groups combined make up the entire market).
2.  Because active management involves higher research, trading, and administrative costs, **after costs, the average actively managed dollar must underperform the average passively managed dollar.**

## 2. The Grossman-Stiglitz Paradox of Indexation

As index investing has grown to represent a massive share of total stock market assets, academic economists have analyzed a fundamental microeconomic contradiction known as the **Grossman-Stiglitz Paradox** (Grossman & Stiglitz, 1980):

### The Concept
An efficient market requires active investors to gather and analyze information to determine fair values and allocate capital. 
*   **The Cost of Information**: Information acquisition is expensive (requiring analysts, databases, and technology).
*   **The Free-Rider Problem**: Passive investors do not pay for information; they simply purchase the index at prevailing market prices, essentially "free-riding" on the price discovery performed by active managers.

### The Paradox
> **If markets are perfectly efficient, no investor can earn abnormal returns. If no investor can earn abnormal returns, no one will pay the cost to acquire information. But if no one acquires information, markets cannot be efficient.**

```
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │         All Investors Go Passive             │
   └──────────────────────┬───────────────────────┘
                          │ (No info acquired)
                          ▼
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │         Price Discovery Collapses            │
   └──────────────────────┬───────────────────────┘
                          │ (Severe mispricings)
                          ▼
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │     Active Returns Become Highly Lucrative   │
   └──────────────────────┬───────────────────────┘
                          │ (Active managers enter)
                          ▼
   ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
   │     Market Returns to Equilibrium            │
   └──────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### The Active-Passive Equilibrium
Consequently, there must exist an equilibrium point where the market is *sufficiently* inefficient that the abnormal returns earned by active managers exactly offset the costs they incur to gather information. If the proportion of passive indexation exceeds this equilibrium:
*   Price discovery fails, and capital is misallocated.
*   The returns to active management surge, prompting capital to flow back into active strategies.
