## Definition

Stock markets are venues where equity securities (shares of ownership in corporations) are issued and traded. They provide capital for companies and investment opportunities for individuals.

## Market Types

- **Primary market**: new shares issued (IPOs, seasoned equity offerings)
- **Secondary market**: existing shares traded (NYSE, NASDAQ)
- **Exchanges** vs **dealer/OTC markets**

## Instruments

- **Common stock**: voting rights + residual cash flow claims
- **Preferred stock**: priority dividends, usually no voting
- **ADRs**: foreign stocks trading domestically

## Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)

The pricing behavior of stock markets is theoretically described by the [[Wiki/Concepts/Efficient-Market-Hypothesis|Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)]]. It posits that because information is processed rapidly by millions of participants, prices always fully reflect all available information. The hypothesis is divided into:
- **Weak form**: Prices reflect all past trading and price data.
- **Semi-strong form**: Prices reflect all publicly available information.
- **Strong form**: Prices reflect all information, including private insider info.

## Active vs. Passive Investing

Stock market participation generally falls into two competing paradigms:
- **Active Management**: Attempting to outperform the market index by identifying undervalued securities using fundamental analysis (as seen in [[Wiki/Concepts/Value-Investing|Value Investing]]) or timing market swings.
- **Passive Management**: Accepting market returns by buying and holding a diversified index, minimizing operating costs and taxes (as seen in [[Wiki/Concepts/Index-Investing|Index Investing]]).

The debate between these paradigms is shaped by the *Grossman-Stiglitz Paradox*, which establishes that a microeconomic equilibrium of both active and passive participants is required for healthy market function.
