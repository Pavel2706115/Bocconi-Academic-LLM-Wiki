# Value Investing

**Value Investing** is an active investment strategy based on identifying and purchasing securities whose market price ($P$) is significantly lower than their intrinsic economic value ($V$). Developed by Benjamin Graham and David Dodd at Columbia Business School and popularized by Warren Buffett, the strategy assumes that markets are subject to emotional swings and cognitive biases, creating temporary pricing discrepancies that can be exploited by patient, rational investors.

---

## 1. Core Principles of Graham's Philosophy

### Investment versus Speculation
Graham draws a sharp distinction between these two behaviors based on three quantitative and qualitative parameters:
*   **Thorough Analysis**: Systematic examination of the target company's financial health, asset backing, capital structure, and earnings stability before committing capital.
*   **Safety of Principal**: Focus on minimizing downside risk under all reasonable operational and macroeconomic scenarios.
*   **Adequate Return**: Target a reasonable, realistic return, prioritising capital preservation over maximum return.

> *"An investment operation is one which, upon thorough analysis, promises safety of principal and an adequate return. Operations not meeting these requirements are speculative."* (Graham, 1949)

### The Margin of Safety
The cornerstone of value investing is the **Margin of Safety**—the discount at which a stock trades relative to its calculated intrinsic value:
$$\text{Margin of Safety} = V - P$$

*   **Risk Reduction**: A wider margin of safety provides a buffer against errors in the valuation process, operational downturns, and market volatility.
*   **Asymmetric Risk-Return**: By buying at a deep discount, the investor limits their downside while capturing significant upside when the market price converges to the intrinsic value.

### Mr. Market Metaphor
Graham uses the allegory of a business partner named **Mr. Market** to explain market volatility:
*   Every day, Mr. Market offers to buy or sell his share of the business at a specific price.
*   Some days he is highly optimistic (demanding a premium price); other days he is deeply depressed (offering to sell at a steep discount).
*   **The Rational Investor's Role**: The value investor does not let Mr. Market's mood swings dictate their opinion of the company's value. Instead, they ignore him when his prices are unreasonable, sell to him when he is wildly euphoric, and buy from him when he is deeply pessimistic.

## 2. Quantitative Valuation Criteria

### Benjamin Graham's Net Current Asset Value (NCAV) Model
Graham's most conservative valuation method is the "Net-Net" model, which evaluates companies trading at a discount to their liquidation value. 

*   **NCAV Calculation**:
    $$\text{NCAV} = \text{Current Assets} - \text{Total Liabilities} - \text{Preferred Stock}$$
*   **Filter Criteria**: A stock is considered a strong value buy if its price satisfies:
    $$P < \frac{2}{3} \times \text{NCAV}$$
    *Note: By buying at this discount, the investor is essentially purchasing the current assets for free while getting all long-term fixed assets (property, plant, equipment) and future cash flows entirely as a bonus.*

### Graham's Growth Formula
To value companies with growth prospects, Graham proposed a simplified valuation model, later updated to reflect rising interest rates:
$$V = \text{EPS} \times (8.5 + 2g)$$

Where:
*   $V$ = Intrinsic value
*   $\text{EPS}$ = Expected trailing 12-month Earnings Per Share
*   $8.5$ = The base P/E ratio for a company with $0\%$ annual growth
*   $g$ = Expected annual growth rate over the next 7 to 10 years (expressed as a percentage)
