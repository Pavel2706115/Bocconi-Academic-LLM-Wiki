# Education Economics

## Overview & Trends

Education represents one of the largest public investments across the globe, functioning as a primary mechanism to boost long-term productivity and intergenerational social mobility.

### Spending and Outcomes in OECD Countries
- **Macro Allocations**: OECD countries dedicate approximately $5\%\text{--}6\%$ of their GDP to education, which comprises over $10\%$ of total public expenditures. About $80\%$ of educational spending within the OECD is public.
- **The Case of Italy**: Italy falls below the OECD spending average, allocating only $7.3\%$ of its public budget to education (Eurostat data, compared to the EU average of $9.6\%$). The system exhibits low tertiary attainment and significant North-South regional disparities.
- **Resource Efficiency**: Data from PISA scores reveals that higher public spending does not guarantee superior outcomes. Countries like Finland and Estonia achieve top reading/math scores with moderate budgets, whereas Italy shows performance inefficiencies relative to its resource inputs.

## Rationales for Government Involvement

A competitive private market fails to provide the socially optimal level of education due to several structural factors:

1. **Productivity**: Education increases aggregate productivity, elevating standards of living that benefit graduates, coworkers, and future generations.
2. **Citizenship and Social Externalities**: Better-educated individuals exhibit higher civic and political participation, lower crime rates, and better health outcomes.
3. **Credit Market Failure**: The private loan market struggles to finance basic education because human capital cannot be collateralized. Governments correct this by directly providing free schools.
4. **Family Utility (Principal-Agent Problem)**: Parents may fail to maximize their children's educational utility over their own short-run consumption.
5. **Equity and Redistribution**: Education is a normal good. Free public provision levels the playing field, promoting intergenerational mobility. In Italy, social mobility is low: only $7\%$ of individuals whose parents did not complete secondary education go on to finish tertiary education.

## Mechanisms of Government Provision and the Crowd-Out Problem

### Free Public Provision
The primary instrument of government intervention is free public schooling. While intuitive, direct provision introduces **crowd-out**:
* **Mechanism**: Free public schools lower the price of a fixed quantity of education to zero. 
* **Implication**: Middle- and high-income parents may shift their children out of high-expenditure private schools to consume the free public option. If crowd-out is high, public spending merely substitutes for private spending, acting as a transfer to the wealthy without increasing total education consumption.

### Voucher Systems
Vouchers provide parents with a tuition credit usable at any public or private school, aiming to leverage market forces.
* **Benefits**: 
  * Boosts consumer sovereignty by matching individual family preferences.
  * Promotes school competition, forcing efficiency improvements.
  * Reduces crowd-out, encouraging wealthy families to add private funds on top of the voucher.
* **Limitations**: 
  * Can lead to excessive specialization, bypassing core curricular elements.
  * Reintroduces sorting/segregation by race, income, or ability.
  * Results in public subsidies for wealthy families who would have paid privately anyway.
  * Tends toward natural monopolies in rural/remote areas.
  * Induces private schools to avoid high-cost students (e.g., special education).

## Measuring the Returns to Education

Calculating the private return to education—defined as the wage premium per additional year of schooling—is expressed as:

$$\text{Measuring the Return to Education} = r \approx (\ln{Y_U} - \ln{Y_{HS}}) \times \frac{1}{\Delta s}$$

Where:
* $Y_U$ is the earnings of university graduates.
* $Y_{HS}$ is the earnings of high school graduates.
* $\Delta s$ is the difference in years of schooling.

### Human Capital vs. Screening Theory
* **Human Capital Theory**: Education directly increases an individual's skills, knowledge, and productivity.
* **Screening Theory**: Education merely serves as a credential or signal of pre-existing high ability to employers.
* **Policy Relevance**: If education is purely a screening tool, private returns are positive but social returns are zero, making public financing inefficient. Empirical evidence indicates a hybrid model, with **Human Capital Theory** being the dominant driver.

## Empirical Evidence on Quality and Inputs

To identify which inputs yield the highest marginal product in the education production function, economists utilize randomized natural experiments to resolve reverse-causality biases (e.g., struggling schools receiving compensatory funding).

### 1. Class Size (The Tennessee STAR Experiment)
- **Method**: Students were randomly assigned to small (13–17 students) or regular (22–25 students) classes.
- **Findings**: Smaller class sizes yielded significantly higher test scores (particularly for minority and low-income students) and long-term benefits (higher college enrollment, larger adult earnings).
- **Policy Cost-Effectiveness**: Universal class-size reduction is extremely expensive. Targeted reductions in early grades and disadvantaged schools are far more cost-effective.

### 2. Teacher Quality (Teacher Value-Added)
- **Method**: Chetty, Friedman, and Rockoff measured teacher quality using historical student performance gains ("value-added").
- **Findings**: Replacing a bottom-$5\%$ teacher with an average teacher increases the collective lifetime earnings of a single classroom by $\$250,000$. Teacher quality is the single most important school-based determinant of student success.
- **Rigidity Constraints**: Rigid, seniority-based pay systems (such as in Italy) hinder teacher performance by failing to incentivize or reward excellence.

### 3. Early Childhood Intervention (The Heckman Curve)
- **Dynamic Complementarity**: Skills acquired early in life make subsequent learning more efficient. Heckman (2006) proved that early childhood interventions for disadvantaged kids have the highest rate of return of any educational policy.
