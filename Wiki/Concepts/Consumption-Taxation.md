# Consumption Taxation

## Economic Rationales: Income vs. Consumption Base

A core debate in public finance is whether to base taxation on **Income** or **Consumption**. 

### 1. The Savings Neutrality Argument (Why Consumption is a Better Base)
- **Double Taxation of Savings**: Under an income tax, savings are distorted because they are taxed twice: first when the income is originally earned, and second on the interest generated by those savings. This drives a wedge in intertemporal choice, reducing saving efficiency.
- **Intertemporal Neutrality**: A consumption tax does not tax the returns to savings. Because future consumption is not penalized relative to current consumption, it removes the intertemporal distortion, promoting higher aggregate capital accumulation and economic growth.
- **Lifetime Equity**: Two individuals with identical lifetime earnings but different consumption paths (one saves early, one consumes immediately) face highly unequal tax burdens under an income tax. A consumption tax treats them equally over their lifetimes.

### 2. Equity Concerns (Why Consumption is a Worse Base)
- **Regressivity**: Lower-income households consume a larger share of their income than wealthy households (who save a large portion). Therefore, a flat consumption tax (like VAT) is regressive, taking a larger percentage of income from the poor.
- **Mitigation**: To restore progressivity, governments either exempt essential goods (food, healthcare) or pair the consumption tax with targeted cash transfers or a progressive personal expenditure tax.

## Designing Consumption Taxes

Governments implement consumption taxation through both indirect and direct mechanisms.

### 1. Value-Added Tax (VAT)
A VAT is an indirect tax levied at each stage of the production and distribution chain, rather than just at the final retail point.
* **Invoice-Credit Method (Standard)**: 
  * Firms pay tax on their total sales (output tax) but receive a tax credit for the VAT paid on their inputs (input tax), documented by invoices.
  * This creates a **self-enforcing audit trail** because firms have an incentive to report transactions accurately to claim input tax credits, reducing tax evasion.
* **VAT in Italy (IVA - Imposta sul Valore Aggiunto)**:
  * Established as a major revenue generator.
  * Italian standard rate is **$22\%$**, with reduced rates of **$4\%$** (essential goods like books, bread), **$5\%$** (certain social services), and **$10\%$** (tourism, electricity, specific foods).

### 2. Retail Sales Tax (RST)
* RST is collected only at the final stage of sale to the consumer.
* **Disadvantage**: Lack of intermediate checks makes it highly vulnerable to evasion by retailers who underreport sales.

### 3. Personal Expenditure Tax
* A direct, progressive tax where individuals report their income and subtract their net savings. The difference (consumption) is taxed using a progressive rate structure, combining savings neutrality with progressive equity.

### 4. Cash-Flow Taxation
* Under a cash-flow corporate tax, capital investments are instantly expensed ($100\%$ depreciation in year one) rather than amortized over time. This neutralizes the tax on business investment.

## Tax Compliance, Evasion, and Enforcement

Fundamental tax reforms seek to broaden the tax base and simplify code compliance, particularly to address tax evasion.

### 1. Theory of Tax Evasion (Allingham-Sandmo Model)
Tax evasion is modeled as a decision under uncertainty. An individual chooses how much income $X$ to declare out of true income $W$ to maximize expected utility:

$$\text{Expected Utility} = (1-p) \times U(W - \tau X) + p \times U(W - \tau X - \theta \tau (W - X))$$

Where:
* $p$ is the probability of being audited.
* $\tau$ is the tax rate.
* $\theta$ is the penalty rate applied to undeclared taxes.
* $W - X$ is the amount of income hidden.

**Takeaway**: Evasion decreases when the audit probability ($p$) increases or the penalty rate ($\theta$) increases. The effect of the tax rate ($\tau$) is theoretically ambiguous due to countervailing wealth effects, but empirically higher tax rates correlate with increased evasion.

### 2. Empirical Determinants of Compliance
- **Third-Party Reporting**: Evasion is near $0\%$ when income is third-party reported (e.g., W-2 wages reported by employers). Evasion rises above $50\%$ for self-reported income (e.g., cash businesses, self-employed).
- **Italy's Compliance Disparities**: Italy suffers from substantial tax evasion, particularly in self-employed sectors and southern regions, leading to a large informal shadow economy. Reforms focus on electronic invoicing (Fatturazione Elettronica) to digitize invoice-credit records.
