# Asymmetric Information

## Definition

Asymmetric information occurs when one party to a transaction possesses more or superior information compared to the other party. This disparity in information creates market inefficiencies, often leading to market failures where mutually beneficial transactions do not occur.

## The Two Core Problems

### 1. Adverse Selection (Before the Transaction)
Adverse selection occurs *before* a transaction takes place, where the party with less information cannot distinguish between high-quality ("good") and low-quality ("bad") options, leading to a disproportionate selection of low-quality options.

*   **Akerlof's Market for Lemons**: George Akerlof demonstrated this using the used-car market. Buyers cannot tell if a used car is a high-quality "peach" or a low-quality "lemon" and are only willing to pay an average price. Owners of "peaches" withdraw their cars from the market because the average price is too low, leaving only "lemons" in the market. Ultimately, the market can collapse entirely.
*   **Other Examples**:
    *   *Insurance*: People with high health risks are the most eager to buy health insurance, driving up premiums and forcing healthy individuals to opt out.
    *   *Credit Markets*: Risky borrowers are the most willing to accept high interest rates because they are less likely to repay, making banks cautious about lending.

### 2. Moral Hazard (After the Transaction)
Moral hazard occurs *after* a transaction is completed, where the informed party changes their behavior in a way that harms the uninformed party because they do not bear the full consequences of their actions.

*   **Examples**:
    *   *Insurance*: An individual with fire insurance may become less careful about locking doors or installing smoke alarms, or in extreme cases, commit arson to collect the payout.
    *   *Employment*: An employee may shirk duties if their effort is not easily monitored by the employer.
    *   *Banking*: Financial institutions might take excessive risks if they expect a government bailout (the "too big to fail" moral hazard).

## Mitigation Tools and Market Solutions

To overcome asymmetric information and prevent market collapse, several mechanisms have developed:

| Tool | Focus | Description |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Signalling** | Adverse Selection | The informed party takes a costly action to verify their quality. Examples: obtaining a university degree to signal high productivity, or a firm offering a long product warranty. |
| **Screening** | Adverse Selection | The uninformed party structures choices to reveal the informed party's private information. Example: an insurance company offering a choice between high-premium/low-deductible and low-premium/high-deductible plans. |
| **Monitoring** | Moral Hazard | Direct auditing, supervision, or tracking of behavior. Example: employers monitoring employee activities or lenders requiring periodic financial statements. |
| **Collateral & Covenants** | Both | Restrictive clauses and asset backing. Lenders require collateral (to reduce losses and incentivize repayment) and covenants (to prevent borrowers from taking on excessive risk). |

## Cross-Course Connections

*   **Financial Markets & Institutions**: Explains why financial intermediaries (like banks) exist. Intermediaries specialize in screening and monitoring borrowers, reducing information costs more efficiently than individual savers.
*   **Microeconomics**: Analyzes asymmetric information as a fundamental source of market failure, justifying government regulatory interventions.
*   **Public Finance**: Guides the design of social insurance systems (like mandatory unemployment or health insurance) to prevent adverse selection death spirals.
*   **Corporate Finance**: Examines the principal-agent problem between shareholders (principals) and corporate managers (agents).
