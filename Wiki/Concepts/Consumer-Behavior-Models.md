# Consumer Behavior Models

## Definition

**Consumer Behavior** is the study of how individuals, groups, and organizations select, buy, use, and dispose of goods, services, ideas, or experiences to satisfy their needs and wants. Understanding consumer behavior allows marketers to structure customer journeys, identify purchasing motives, and design effective decision-making models.

---

## 1. The Stages of the Purchasing Process

Consumers navigate five core stages during a purchase decision:
1.  **Need Perception**: Recognizing a discrepancy between the actual state and a desired state (driven by internal or external stimuli).
2.  **Information Search**: Searching for options. Sources include *internal* (memory) and *external* (interpersonal, commercial, public, empirical/experiential).
3.  **Pre-Purchase Evaluation**: Assessing alternatives based on attributes and choice heuristics.
4.  **Moment of Purchase**: The transaction itself (highly influenced by in-store shopper marketing and commercial service parameters).
5.  **Post-Purchase Evaluation**: Comparing performance with expectations, leading to satisfaction or cognitive dissonance.

---

## 2. Customer Journey and Touchpoint Analysis

The **Customer Journey** is the sequence of direct and indirect interactions (touchpoints) a consumer has with a brand before, during, and after a purchase.
*   **Customer Journey Map**: Tracks the customer's emotions, actions, and pain points across three periods: **Pre-Service**, **Service**, and **Post-Service**.
*   **Touchpoints**: Points of contact between the customer and the company, categorized into *physical*, *digital*, and *human* touchpoints.

---

## 3. Psychological Involvement

**Psychological Involvement** is the consumer's level of interest, motivation, and attention directed toward a product category or purchase decision. 
*   **High Involvement**: High risk (financial, functional, physical, psychosocial) and high self-identification. Leads to extended problem-solving.
*   **Low Involvement**: Low risk, routine purchase behavior. Marks habit-driven decision-making.

## 4. Evaluation Models (Alternative Assessment)

Marketers classify consumer evaluation models into two main paradigms:

### A. Compensatory Models (Expected Value Models)
A consumer evaluates products by weighing all attributes. A high score on one attribute can *compensate* for a low score on another.

#### 1. Fishbein Multi-Attribute Attitude Model
The overall attitude ($A_i$) toward brand $i$ is the weighted sum of beliefs about its attributes:
$$A_i = \sum_{j=1}^{M} w_j \times b_{ij}$$

Where:
*   $w_j$ = Importance weight of attribute $j$
*   $b_{ij}$ = Consumer's rating of brand $i$ on attribute $j$
*   $M$ = Total number of attributes evaluated

#### 2. Ideal Profile Model
Measures the distance of a brand's performance from the consumer's ideal level:
$$A_i = \sum_{j=1}^{M} w_j \times |I_j - b_{ij}|$$
*   *Ideal level* ($I_j$) is the perfect rating on attribute $j$. The brand with the **lowest** score is closest to the ideal and is preferred.

### B. Non-Compensatory Models
A low score on a key attribute cannot be compensated by other attributes. Choice is made using strict heuristics:
*   **Conjunctive Model**: The consumer establishes minimum acceptable cutoff levels for *every* attribute. A brand is rejected if it fails to meet even a single cutoff.
*   **Disjunctive Model**: The consumer establishes a high cutoff level for a single key attribute (or a few). A brand is accepted if it exceeds this high standard, regardless of others.
*   **Lexicographic Model**: The consumer ranks attributes in order of importance. The brand that scores highest on the *most important* attribute is chosen. If there is a tie, the second most important attribute is compared.

---

## 5. Choice-Set Models

Consumers do not evaluate all active brands in the market. The cognitive screening process is modeled as:
$$\text{Total Set} \rightarrow \text{Awareness Set} \rightarrow \text{Consideration Set} \rightarrow \text{Choice Set} \rightarrow \text{Decision}$$

1.  **Total Set**: All existing brands in the product category.
2.  **Awareness Set**: The subset of brands the consumer knows.
3.  **Consideration Set**: Brands that meet initial purchase criteria and are seriously evaluated.
4.  **Choice Set**: The final candidates after initial attribute screening.

---

## 6. Purchasing Roles

In many purchase decisions (especially families or B2B), multiple individuals take on different roles:
1.  **Initiator**: The person who first suggests or thinks of the idea of buying the product.
2.  **Influencer**: Someone whose views or advice influence the final decision.
3.  **Decider**: The individual who ultimately determines any part of the buying decision (whether, what, how, or where to buy).
4.  **Buyer**: The person who makes the actual physical purchase.
5.  **User**: The individual who consumes or uses the product or service.
