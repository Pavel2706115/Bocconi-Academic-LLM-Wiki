# Gross Domestic Product

## Definition

GDP is the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period. It is the primary measure of aggregate economic activity and national output.

## Measurement Approaches

GDP can be calculated using three equivalent methods:

1. **Expenditure Approach**: Sum of all spending on final goods and services:
   $$GDP = C + I + G + NX$$
   - **$C$ (Consumption)**: Household spending on durable and non-durable goods and services.
   - **$I$ (Investment)**: Spending on capital equipment, structures, and changes in business inventories. (Note: It counts when capital is *produced*, not necessarily sold).
   - **$G$ (Government Spending)**: Public expenditure on final goods and services (excludes transfer payments like welfare or pensions, as they are not payments for current goods/services).
   - **$NX$ (Net Exports)**: Exports minus Imports ($X - M$). Imports are subtracted because they are already included in $C, I, G$ but were not produced domestically.
2. **Income Approach**: Sum of all incomes earned within the economy:
   $$GDP = \text{Wages} + \text{Profits} + \text{Rent} + \text{Interest}$$
3. **Production (Value Added) Approach**: Sum of the value added at each stage of production:
   $$\text{Value Added} = \text{Value of Output} - \text{Value of Intermediate Inputs}$$

## Key Concepts and Distinctions

- **Avoiding Double-Counting**: Only *final* goods and services are counted in GDP. Intermediate goods (e.g., steel used to make a car) are excluded because their value is already embedded in the price of the final product.
- **Nominal vs. Real GDP**: 
  - **Nominal GDP**: Output valued at current-year prices. It can rise due to inflation rather than an increase in physical output.
  - **Real GDP**: Output valued at constant-year prices (using a base year). It isolates physical production changes by stripping away inflation.
- **GDP per Capita**: $GDP / \text{Population}$ — serves as a proxy for the average standard of living.
- **Trade Deficits and Assets**: A trade deficit ($NX < 0$) means a nation imports more than it exports. In macroeconomics, this is balanced by the capital account: the nation is importing goods but exporting financial assets (debt, equity, or property) to foreigners, meaning they are receiving capital inflows that can fund domestic investment.

## Limitations

- **Informal Sector & Non-Market Activities**: Excludes household chores, volunteer work, and the underground economy.
- **Welfare & Distribution**: Ignores income inequality and wealth distribution.
- **Negative Externalities**: Does not subtract the costs of environmental pollution or resource degradation.
- **Quality of Life**: Does not directly measure health, happiness, or leisure time.
