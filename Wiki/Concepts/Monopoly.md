# Monopoly

## Definition

A monopoly is a market structure characterized by a single seller facing the entire market demand curve. Because there are no close substitutes and entry is blocked, the monopolist is a **price-maker** with significant **market power** — the ability to raise price above marginal cost without losing all customers.

## Key Features and Mechanisms

### 1. Marginal Revenue lies below Demand ($MR < P$)
To sell an additional unit of output, a monopolist must lower the price on *all* units sold (assuming no price discrimination). This creates two opposing forces on total revenue ($TR = P \times Q$):
- **Quantity Effect**: Revenue increases because more units are sold ($+P$).
- **Price Effect**: Revenue decreases because units are sold at a lower price ($-Q \times \Delta P$).

Therefore, the **Marginal Revenue (MR)** curve is steeper than the demand curve and lies entirely below it:
$$MR = P + Q\frac{\Delta P}{\Delta Q} < P$$

### 2. Profit Maximization ($MR = MC$)
The monopolist maximizes profit by choosing the quantity $Q^*$ where:
$$MR = MC$$
Once $Q^*$ is determined, the monopolist charges the maximum price ($P^*$) consumers are willing to pay for that quantity, found on the market demand curve. Since $P^* > MC(Q^*)$, the monopolist earns positive economic profits (producer surplus) in both the short run and the long run.

### 3. Deadweight Loss and Inefficiency
Compared to a perfectly competitive market where $P = MC$:
- Monopolies restrict output ($Q_M < Q_C$) and charge higher prices ($P_M > P_C$).
- This reduces consumer surplus and increases producer surplus.
- It also creates **Deadweight Loss (DWL)**: a permanent loss of total economic surplus because mutually beneficial trades between $Q_M$ and $Q_C$ are blocked.

### 4. Barriers to Entry
A monopoly can only persist in the long run if there are significant barriers to entry that prevent competitors from entering the market:
*   **Legal/Government Barriers**: Patents, copyrights, and government licenses (e.g., mail delivery franchises).
*   **Natural Barriers (Natural Monopoly)**: Extremely high fixed costs and substantial economies of scale make it cheaper for a single firm to supply the entire market than for multiple firms to compete (e.g., water or electricity grids).
*   **Strategic Barriers**: Control of essential inputs or aggressive pricing strategies to deter entrants.

## Price Discrimination

Monopolists often try to capture more consumer surplus by charging different prices for the same product:

*   **First-Degree (Perfect) Price Discrimination**: Charging every single customer their exact maximum willingness to pay.
    - *Outcome*: Captures 100% of consumer surplus, converts it to producer surplus, and eliminates deadweight loss (output increases to the competitive level $P = MC$).
*   **Second-Degree Price Discrimination**: Charging different prices based on the quantity consumed (e.g., volume discounts, block pricing).
*   **Third-Degree Price Discrimination**: Segmenting the market into distinct groups with different price elasticities of demand (e.g., student/senior discounts, airline tickets booked in advance vs. last minute). Groups with more inelastic demand are charged higher prices.
