# Marketing Segmentation and Positioning

## Definition

The **STP Process** (Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning) is the core strategic framework in marketing. It shifts the company's focus from mass marketing to tailored value propositions:
1.  **Segmentation**: Dividing a diverse market into distinct groups of buyers with different needs or behaviors.
2.  **Targeting**: Evaluating and selecting which market segments to enter.
3.  **Positioning**: Shaping the product's image and value proposition in the target consumers' minds relative to competitors.

$$\text{Segmentation} \rightarrow \text{Targeting} \rightarrow \text{Positioning}$$

---

## 1. Market Segmentation

A company segments the market using relevant descriptors to identify homogeneous clusters of demand.

### A. B2C Segmentation Criteria
B2C markets are segmented using four primary categories:
1.  **Demographic**: Age, gender, family size, income, occupation, education.
2.  **Geographic**: Region, city size, population density, climate.
3.  **Psychographic**: Social class, personality traits, and **lifestyle** (often modeled using standardized tools like the *Eurisko Lifestyle Map*).
4.  **Behavioral**: Purchase occasion, usage frequency (heavy vs. light users), brand loyalty, and *desired benefits* (e.g. toothpaste market segmented into sensory, sociable, worried/prevention, and independent/value segments).

*   **A priori vs. Ex post Segmentation**:
    *   *A priori*: Segmenting using pre-determined demographic or geographic criteria before collecting survey data.
    *   *Ex post*: Using statistical techniques (like cluster analysis) on consumer survey responses to identify segments based on multi-attribute benefits or lifestyles.

### B. B2B Segmentation Criteria
B2B markets use distinct criteria:
*   *Demographics*: Industry sector, company size, geographic location.
*   *Operating Variables*: Technology type, usage intensity, customer capabilities.
*   *Purchasing Approaches*: Power structures, general purchasing policies, relationship orientation.

### C. Requirements for Effective Segmentation
A segment must meet **five** key criteria to be strategically viable:
1.  **Measurability**: The size, purchasing power, and profiles of the segments can be measured.
2.  **Importance / Substantiality**: The segments are large or profitable enough to serve.
3.  **Accessibility**: The segments can be effectively reached and served.
4.  **Differentiability**: The segments react conceptually differently to different marketing mix elements.
5.  **Stability**: The segments remain stable over time to justify long-term investments.

---

## 2. Segment Targeting

Once segments are defined, the company evaluates them based on:
1.  **Segment Attractiveness**: Determined by size, long-term growth rate, price elasticity, and barrier structures.
2.  **Competitive Intensity**: Number of active competitors in the segment, threat of substitutes, and supplier power (Porter forces).
3.  **Strategic Alignment**: The segment matches the company's resources, core capabilities, and brand image.

---

## 3. Product Positioning

**Product Positioning** establishes the unique cognitive space a brand occupies in the consumer's mind. It is visually mapped using **Perceptual Maps**.

### A. Perceptual and Preferences Mapping
*   **Perceptual Map**: A two-dimensional grid plotting how consumers perceive different brands along key evaluation attributes (e.g. price vs. quality, or sportiness vs. comfort).
*   **Preferences Map**: Overlays the consumer's "ideal product" coordinates onto the perceptual map, helping identify **market niches** where customer desires are currently unmet.

### B. Repositioning Strategies
If a brand's current positioning is weak, it can be improved through **six** distinct strategic repositioning vectors:

1.  **Modify the Offer**: Physically changing the product's attributes or features to shift its position on the perceptual map.
2.  **Adjust Attribute Weights**: Launching campaigns to modify the relative importance of attributes (e.g. persuading consumers that safety is more important than speed).
3.  **Psychological Repositioning**: Modifying the consumer's opinion of the brand's performance on existing attributes without changing the physical product.
4.  **Reposition the Ideal Product**: Shifting the perceived coordinates of the ideal product to bring it closer to the company's current position.
5.  **Introduce New Attributes**: Focusing the consumer's evaluation process on a completely new attribute that competitors do not possess.
6.  **Modify Competitor Perception**: Repositioning the competition by highlighting their limitations or weaknesses.
