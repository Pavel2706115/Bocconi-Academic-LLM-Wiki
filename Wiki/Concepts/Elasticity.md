# Elasticity

## Definition

Elasticity measures the responsiveness or sensitivity of one economic variable to changes in another. Unlike the slope of a curve, elasticity is a unit-free measure because it is expressed as a ratio of percentage changes.

---

## Types of Elasticity

### 1. Price Elasticity of Demand ($\epsilon_D$)
Measures how sensitive the quantity demanded of a good is to a change in its price:
$$\epsilon_D = \frac{\%\Delta Q_D}{\%\Delta P} = \frac{\Delta Q_D}{\Delta P} \times \frac{P}{Q_D}$$

- **Slope vs. Elasticity**: On a linear downward-sloping demand curve, the *slope* ($\frac{\Delta P}{\Delta Q}$) is constant, but the *elasticity* changes at every point. Demand is highly elastic at high prices (upper left) and inelastic at low prices (lower right).
- **Extreme Cases**:
  - **Perfecty Inelastic** ($\epsilon_D = 0$): Vertical demand curve. Quantity demanded does not change at all regardless of price (e.g., life-saving insulin).
  - **Perfecty Elastic** ($\epsilon_D = -\infty$): Horizontal demand curve. Consumers will buy any amount at the market price, but zero if price rises even slightly (perfect competition).

### 2. Price Elasticity of Supply ($\epsilon_S$)
Measures how sensitive the quantity supplied of a good is to a change in its price:
$$\epsilon_S = \frac{\%\Delta Q_S}{\%\Delta P} = \frac{\Delta Q_S}{\Delta P} \times \frac{P}{Q_S}$$
Supply is typically more elastic in the long run because firms have more time to expand capacity, build new factories, or enter/leave the industry.

### 3. Income Elasticity of Demand ($\epsilon_Y$)
Measures how demand shifts when consumer income changes:
$$\epsilon_Y = \frac{\%\Delta Q_D}{\%\Delta \text{Income}}$$
- **Normal Goods** ($\epsilon_Y > 0$): Demand increases as income rises.
  - *Necessities* ($0 < \epsilon_Y < 1$): e.g., groceries.
  - *Luxuries* ($\epsilon_Y > 1$): e.g., private jets.
- **Inferior Goods** ($\epsilon_Y < 0$): Demand decreases as income rises (e.g., instant noodles, public transport).

### 4. Cross-Price Elasticity of Demand ($\epsilon_{XY}$)
Measures how the quantity demanded of Good X responds to a change in the price of Good Y:
$$\epsilon_{XY} = \frac{\%\Delta Q_{D, X}}{\%\Delta P_Y}$$
- **Substitutes** ($\epsilon_{XY} > 0$): An increase in the price of iPhone increases the demand for Samsung phones (positive relation).
- **Complements** ($\epsilon_{XY} < 0$): An increase in the price of polaroid film decreases the demand for polaroid cameras (negative relation).

---

## Elasticity and Total Revenue

A firm's Total Revenue ($TR = P \times Q$) is directly tied to the price elasticity of demand. If a firm increases its price, there are two competing effects: the higher price per unit (increases $TR$) and the lower quantity sold (decreases $TR$).

| Category | Range | Revenue Effect of a Price Increase ($P \uparrow$) | Intuition |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Elastic** | $|\epsilon_D| > 1$ | Total Revenue **decreases** ($TR \downarrow$) | The percentage decrease in quantity sold is larger than the percentage increase in price. |
| **Unit Elastic** | $|\epsilon_D| = 1$ | Total Revenue **remains unchanged** | The percentage decrease in quantity exactly offsets the percentage increase in price. |
| **Inelastic** | $|\epsilon_D| < 1$ | Total Revenue **increases** ($TR \uparrow$) | The percentage decrease in quantity sold is smaller than the percentage increase in price. |
