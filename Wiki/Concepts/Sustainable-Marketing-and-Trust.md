# Sustainable Marketing and Trust

## Definition

**Sustainable Marketing** is trust-oriented marketing that aims to establish long-term, mutually beneficial relationships with customers and stakeholders while balancing economic performance with environmental and social responsibility. In sustainable marketing, **trust** is the ultimate key resource of the company, acting as a reducer of consumer perceived risk and market transaction costs.

---

## 1. Trust as a Core Marketing Asset

In modern surplus-of-supply economies, trust stabilizes purchases over time and allows companies to:
1.  **Reduce price sensitivity**: Customers are willing to pay a premium to reliable providers.
2.  **Generate positive word-of-mouth (WOM)**: Promoting organic customer advocacy.
3.  **Lower commercial distance**: Shortening negotiations and transaction times.
4.  **Boost efficiency**: Decreasing monitoring and customer service costs.

---

## 2. Business Orientations

Firms differ in how they orient their operations in relation to the market:

| Orientation | Core Philosophy | Risk / Limitation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Production Orientation** | Maximizing operational efficiency and distribution; assumes consumers favor cheap, widely available products. | Neglects product quality and consumer preference variations. |
| **Product Orientation** | Focusing strictly on product quality, performance, and continuous innovation. | *Marketing Myopia*: Falling in love with the product rather than solving customer needs. |
| **Sales Orientation** | Driven by aggressive personal selling, promotional push, and transaction-driven tactics. | Fails to build long-term relationships or secure repeat purchases. |
| **Marketing Orientation** | Starts by identifying customer needs and wants, delivering value more effectively than competitors. | Can focus too narrowly on individual short-term desires. |
| **Social Marketing Orientation** | Delivers value to customers in a way that maintains or improves the well-being of both the consumer and society (*Triple Bottom Line*). | Requires balancing conflicting goals of profit, customer satisfaction, and social welfare. |

## 3. Customer Equity and Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)

Customer Equity represents the total combined Customer Lifetime Value of all the company's current and potential customers. 

**Customer Lifetime Value (CLV)** quantifies the financial value of an individual customer relationship over time. 

### CLV Formula
For a nondurable product with recurring annual purchases:
$$\text{CLV} = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{(M_t - C_t) \times r_t}{(1 + d)^t}$$

Where:
*   $M_t$ = Gross margin generated by the customer in year $t$
*   $C_t$ = Cost of marketing activities to retain the customer in year $t$
*   $r_t$ = Retention rate (probability that the customer remains active in year $t$)
*   $d$ = Discount rate / cost of capital
*   $T$ = Time horizon of the relationship

## 4. Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) and D&I

Sustainable firms compile a **sustainability report** detailing their environmental, economic, and social impacts. 

### Diversity Brand Index (DBI)
The DBI measures how inclusive a brand is perceived to be by consumers.
*   **D&I and Business Growth**: Genuine commitment to diversity and inclusion accelerates revenue growth, improves market reputation, and increases the **Net Promoter Score (NPS)**.
*   **Net Promoter Score (NPS) Formula**:
    $$\text{NPS} = \% \text{ Promoters} - \% \text{ Detractors}$$
    *   *Promoters*: Customers rating the brand 9-10 (high advocacy).
    *   *Passive*: Customers rating the brand 7-8.
    *   *Detractors*: Customers rating the brand 0-6.
