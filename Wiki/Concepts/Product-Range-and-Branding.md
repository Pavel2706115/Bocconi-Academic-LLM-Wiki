# Product Range and Branding

## Definition

**Product Range Management** and **Branding Strategy** determine how a company designs, organizes, and brands its total market offering. This includes structuring the **Product Mix** (the set of all products offered for sale), defining brand architecture, evaluating product portfolios using strategic models, and managing the **New Product Development (NPD)** process.

---

## 1. Structuring the Product Mix

The product mix (or product assortment) is organized hierarchically:
*   **Individual Item (SKU)**: A distinct unit within a product line, identifiable by size, price, or appearance.
*   **Product Line**: A group of closely related product items that function in a similar manner, are sold to the same customer groups, or fall within given price ranges.
*   **Product Mix**: The complete set of all product lines and items that a company offers to buyers.

### Product Mix Dimensions
The product mix is structurally measured along four dimensions:
1.  **Breadth**: The number of different product lines the company carries.
2.  **Depth**: The number of individual versions/items offered within each product line.
3.  **Length**: The total number of items in the company's product mix (the sum of items across all lines).
4.  **Consistency**: How closely related the various product lines are in end-use, production requirements, or distribution channels.

*   *Product Range Strategy (Industrial vs. Commercial)*:
    *   **Industrial range**: Focuses on technology-driven capabilities and manufacturing consistency.
    *   **Commercial range**: Focuses on market-driven assortments (broad or deep) to satisfy customer needs at the point of sale.

## 2. Branding Strategy & Architecture

Branding architecture defines the relationship between the corporate brand, product lines, and individual products. There are four primary branding models:

1.  **Branded House (Corporate Brand Dominance)**: A single corporate brand is used across all products (e.g. Virgin, FedEx). Builds high brand equity quickly, but exposes all products to corporate reputational risk.
2.  **House of Brands (Individual Product Branding)**: The company markets a range of individual, separate brands with no visible connection to the corporate parent (e.g. Procter & Gamble owning Pampers, Gillette, Tide). Protects brands from mutual reputational contagion, but requires massive individual marketing investments.
3.  **Umbrella Brand**: Using a single brand name to market multiple related products in different categories (e.g. Samsung, Nivea).
4.  **Endorsed Brand**: Individual brands are backed by the credibility of a master corporate brand (e.g. Courtyard *by Marriott*, or PlayStation *by Sony*).

## 3. The BCG Matrix (Portfolio Analysis)

Developed by the Boston Consulting Group, the **BCG Matrix** classifies a company's business units or products along two dimensions to guide cash allocation:

1.  **Relative Market Share** (horizontal axis): A proxy for the firm's competitive strength and ability to generate cash.
2.  **Market Growth Rate** (vertical axis): A proxy for market attractiveness and the level of cash investment required.

| Category | Market Growth Rate | Relative Market Share | Cash Flow & Strategic Action |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Stars** | High | High | High cash generator but also requires high investment to maintain lead. Strategy: *Hold/Build*. |
| **Cash Cows** | Low | High | High cash generator with low investment requirements. Generates the surplus used to fund other projects. Strategy: *Harvest*. |
| **Question Marks** | High | Low | Low market share in a rapidly growing market; requires heavy cash injections to become a Star, or risk turning into a Dog. Strategy: *Build/Divest*. |
| **Dogs** | Low | Low | Low market share in a slow-growth market; rarely generates significant cash. Strategy: *Divest/Liquidate*. |

## 4. The New Product Development (NPD) Process

Product innovation is driven by two competing paradigms:
*   **Technology-Push**: Driven by R&D discoveries, creating products before clear consumer demand exists.
*   **Market-Pull**: Driven by identifying customer needs and designing products to satisfy them.

### Stages of the NPD Process
The standard NPD process follows a structured sequence:
$$\text{Idea Generation} \rightarrow \text{Idea Screening} \rightarrow \text{Concept Development} \rightarrow \text{Business Analysis} \rightarrow \text{Product Development} \rightarrow \text{Market Testing} \rightarrow \text{Commercialization}$$

1.  **Idea Generation**: Systematic search for new product ideas from internal and external sources.
2.  **Screening**: Filtering ideas to spot good ones and drop poor ones as early as possible.
3.  **Concept Development and Testing**: Translating product ideas into detailed consumer concepts and testing them with target groups.
4.  **Business Analysis**: Reviewing sales, cost, and profit projections to see if they satisfy company objectives.
5.  **Product Development**: Converting the concept into a physical prototype.
6.  **Market Testing**: Introducing the product and its marketing program into realistic market settings.
7.  **Commercialization**: Full-scale launch of the product in the market.
