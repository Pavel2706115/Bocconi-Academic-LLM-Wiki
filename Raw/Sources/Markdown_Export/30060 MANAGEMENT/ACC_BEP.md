**OPERATING RISKS** 
OPERATING RISKS 

## **OPERATING RISKS** 

Operating risks is linked to two components of the organization’s economic structure: BEP and degree of the operating leverage. 
OPERATING RISKS 

## **OPERATING LEVERAGE** 

Operating leverage is the size of the differential (gap) between revenues and total costs above and below the break-even point. 
OPERATING RISKS 

Operating risks 
OPERATING RISKS 

## Operating risks 

## Suppose that there are exogenous changes in volume: 

## **FOR A FIRM WITH A VERY RIGID COST STRUCTURE** 

## **FOR A FIRM WITH A FLEXIBLE COST STRUCTURE** 

i.e. high ratio of fixed costs to total costs 

- The firm will react badly to drops in volumes (less room to distribute fixed costs over units of output) 

- The firm will respond positively to increases in volumes 

i.e. low ratio of fixed costs to total costs 

- If volumes drop, the firm can easily reduce costs 

- If volumes increase, the firm experience unavoidable cost increases 
OPERATING RISKS 

## The Elements of Operating risks 

## **THE LEVEL OF BEP** 

(the higher the BEP, the higher the risk) 

## **THE OPERATING ELASTICITY** 

the relationship between total variable costs and fixed costs at the BEP (the higher the elasticity, the lower the risk) 
OPERATING RISKS 

## Operating risks 

1. Operating risk is not necessarily a bad thing: it amplifies losses (as we move in the area left to the BEP), but it amplifies profits (as we move in the area right to the BEP). 

2. The choice of, e.g. two plants with same BEP but different operating risk depends on our estimate of how much volumes sold would exceed the BEP, and on the manager’s degree of risk aversion.

**OPERATING ELASTICITY** 
OPERATING ELASTICITY 

## **OPERATING ELASTICITY** 

Operating elasticity can be measured by calculating the relationship between total variable costs and fixed costs at the BEP. The higher the operating elasticity, the lower the risk. 
OPERATING ELASTICITY 

## Operating elasticity: an application 

Graphically, it reflects into the gap between the lines of total revenues and costs. The wider the wedge between the lines, the higher the risk.
