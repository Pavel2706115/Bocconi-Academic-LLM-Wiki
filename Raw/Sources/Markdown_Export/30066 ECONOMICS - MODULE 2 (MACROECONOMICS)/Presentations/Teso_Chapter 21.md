## **Exchange Rate Crises under Fixed Exchange Rates** 

- Suppose that people believe that there will be soon a devaluation (or, even more extreme, a shift to flexible exchange rates). Why? 

   - large CA deficit and recession 

   - High inflation, leading to real appreciation (and so steady worsening of trade balance) 

   - or simply because markets think so (self-fulfilling prophecy!)… 

Given the (approximate version of the) interest parity condition: 

- With fixed exchange rates, then the second term is zero, and domestic and foreign interest rates are equal 

- If markets expect a devaluation, the the second term is negative, and to maintain parity now the domestic interest rate must be higher than the foreign one 

## **Exchange Rate Crises under Fixed Exchange Rates** 

For example, assume markets have the following expectations: ▪ With a 50% chance, there will be a devaluation of 20% ▪ With a 50% chance, no devaluation. Then, the second term is equal to 0.5 X 0 + 0.5 X (-0.2)= - 0.1= - 10% 

In light of this, if the Central Bank wants to maintain parity, it must increase the domestic interest rate by 10%! 

## **Exchange Rate Crises under Fixed Exchange Rates** 

Faced with the expectations of devaluation, the government has three options: 

1. Try to convince the markets that no devaluation is coming. Of course, this might be hard to do… 

2. Give in and devalue. 

3. Fight and maintain the parity, at the cost of high interest rates and a potential recession. 

   - To do so, what happens is: 

      - since investors, with current expectations, are currently selling domestic currency to buy foreign currency (and invest that in foreign bonds), the exchange rate is decreasing. 

      - To overcome this, the Central Bank must sell part of their reserves in foreign currency and buy domestic currency. 

      - This prevents the exchange rate from falling, increasing interest rate. 

   - More likely to fight and maintain the parity if expected devaluation is small

- Because today’s exchange rate moves with any change in expectations, the **exchange rate will be volatile** (it will move frequently and perhaps by large amounts). 

A country that decides to operate under flexible exchange rates must accept the fact that it will be exposed to substantial exchange rate fluctuations over time. 

## **Exchange rate movements under flexible exchange rates** 

## Major sources of fluctuations 

- News on current account : since investors expect that eventually current account will be balanced (you cannot borrow or lend forever), any news of changes in current account will lead investors to expect movements in future E (which will be needed to restore balance) 

- News of changes in monetary policy (especially if perceived to be persistent rather than temporary). 

Note: what moves Et are news, not changes that were already expected.

## **Common currency areas** 

## **What if countries are tightly integrated and trade a lot?** 

- They could adopt a common currency (extreme version of fixed exchange rates among a group of countries). 

- Big advantages: lower transaction costs and ease to compare prices among countries (great for competition). 

- According to Robert Mundell, an **optimal currency area** needs to satisfy one of the three conditions: 

   - The countries have to experience similar shocks 

      - In this case, they would have followed similar monetary policies anyway even with flexible exchange rates 

   - If the countries experience different shocks, they must have flexible prices and wages 

      - In this case, they could lower prices and wages to stay competitive, if needed. 

   - If the countries experience different shocks, they must have high factor mobility. 

      - In this case, people can move across countries to adjust to shocks 

## **Common currency areas** 

- Are the United States an optimal currency area? 

   - Different states are affected by different shocks 

   - But high factor mobility (workers willing to move across states) 

- There is less agreement on whether Europe constitutes an optimal currency area. ▪ Different shocks across countries 

   - Prices and wages adjust slowly 

   - Little factor mobility 

- Southern Europe suffering from low demand lost the ability to benefit from lower exchange rates relative to their commercial partners 

- (Note: benefits of the Euro are clear: for instance elimination of foreign exchange transactions in the euro area led to a reduction in transaction costs of 0.5% of the combined GDP of these countries.) 

## **Limiting Central Bank’s Ability to Conduct Monetary Policy** 

- What if the CB in a **cannot be trusted to follow a** country **responsible** 

- **monetray policy?** Typical in cases of history of high inflation 

- Possible solution: tie your hands with a fixed exchange rate! 

- Caveat: you need to convince investors that the exchange rate will remain fixed also in the future. 

- Possible ways to do so: 

   - Make the fixed exchange rate be part of a more general macroeconomic package. 

   - Make a **hard peg** so that it is symbolically or technically harder to change the parity. 

      - **Dollarization** : Replace the domestic currency with the dollar (today, for instance: Ecuador, Zimbabwe, El Salvador, Panama). 

      - **Currency board** : A central bank stands ready to exchange foreign currency for domestic currency at the official exchange rate set by the government + they cannot buy and sell government bonds 

## **FOCUS: Lessons from Argentina’s currency board** 

- In 1991, Argentina’s president announced the adoption of a currency board that would stand ready to exchange its pesos for dollars on demand (after years of massive inflation) 

- The currency board appeared to work well for a while as inflation fell from 2300% in 1990 to 4% in 1994. 

- However, the appreciation of the dollar in the second half of the 1990s led to a decrease in demand for Argentina’s goods, leading to declines in output. 

   - Recession led investors to worry about a possible government default, so they started asking for even higher interest rates 

   - Investors started to expect a future return to flexible exchange rates, further driving up interest rates. 

▪ In early 2002, Argentina gave up the currency board and let the peso float.
