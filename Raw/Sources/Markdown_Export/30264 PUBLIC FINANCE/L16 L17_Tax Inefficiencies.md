## Taxation and Economic Efficiency 4 

- The **DWL** measures the inefficiency of taxation: the amount of consumer and producer surplus society loses by imposing a tax 

- DWL is determined by changed in quantities  when a tax is imposed because this change captures the number of socially efficient trades that are not being made 

- The outcome of this efficiency analysis would be the same **if the tax were imposed on consumers** instead of producers 

- The more elastic is demand or supply, the larger the DWL.

## Empirical Evidence: The Window Tax 1 

In 1696, English King William III needed money to finance an ongoing war with France. 

- To raise this revenue, the king imposed a **new tax** based on the **number of windows in a home** , which was an indicator of a home’s value 

- The problem was that individuals could minimize their tax by boarding up or otherwise removing windows. This rendered the tax a less efficient measure of home value and created a **deadweight loss** by making it less pleasant (and therefore of less value to residents) to live in their homes 
## Empirical Evidence: The Window Tax 2 

Oates and Schwab (2015) presented a test for whether this was the case: they looked at **whether the number of windows responded** to “notches” in the tax schedule. 

- From 1747 to 1757: 

   - there was no tax if the house had fewer than ten windows, 

   - a tax of 6 pence per window for 10 to 14 windows; 

   - • a tax of 9 pence per window if there were 15 to 19 windows; 

   - a tax of 1 shilling (12 pence) per window for 20 windows or more. 

- The marginal tax rate jumped enormously on building the 10th, 15th, or 20th window. 
## Empirical Evidence: The Window Tax 3 

Figure 20-3 

There are obvious spikes at 9, 14, and 19 windows, exactly what we would predict from the effects of this tax. 
## Empirical Evidence: The Window Tax 4 

One could argue that perhaps there was some British custom or some other reason for having 9, 14, or 19 windows in a house. 

- Oates and Schwab address this point by using the fact that in 1761, the government added a provision that houses with 8 or 9 windows would pay 1 shilling per window. 

- This means that after 1761, there was suddenly an incentive to keep the number of windows to 7 or fewer. This is exactly what happened. 
## Empirical Evidence: The Window Tax 

Figure 20-4 

In data from 1761 to 1765, the authors find that 27% of houses have 7 windows, while only 5% have 6 windows, and 3% have 8 windows. Clearly, this was a response to the tax. 

## APPLICATION: Tax Avoidance in Practice 

Keynes: “The avoidance of taxes is the only pursuit that still carries any reward.” Some examples of avoidance: 

1. The Papal States taxed salt heavily, so Tuscan bakers stopped using it. Even today, Tuscan bread is saltless. 

2. In the early 1980s, Cyprus’s building tax applied to finished structures. Homeowners put steel bars jutting out from their roofs to avoid the tax. 

3. The U.S. government taxes imported shoes at a much higher rate than imported slippers. Some shoe manufacturers pass their products off as slippers, e.g., by implanting a temporary layer of felt in the soles.

## Progressive Tax Systems Can Be Less Efficient 

- Previous insights about DWL apply not only to taxation of goods, but also to **taxation of income** 

- There can be large efficiency costs to moving from a proportional to a **progressive tax system** 

- **Example:** 

   - Suppose there are two people, one with a wage of $10 per hour and one with a wage of $20 per hour. 

- For both, a 10% rise in wages leads them to supply 10% more labor (elasticity of labor supply = 1). 

- Elasticity of labor demand is also 1.

## Governments Should “Smooth” Tax Rates over Time 

- Government efficiency in taxation over time is maximized by tax smoothing, by having a relatively constant tax rate over time rather than high taxes in some periods and low taxes in others. 

- High and then low tax rates produce a larger DWL than steady medium tax rates.

## Ramsey Taxation: The Theory of Commodity Taxation 

- **Optimal commodity taxation:** Choosing the tax rates across goods to minimize deadweight loss for a given government revenue requirement. 

   - **Ramsey Rule:** To minimize the DWL of a tax system while

## Equity Implications of the Ramsey Model 

- Imagine that the government had only two goods it could tax: cereal and caviar: 

   - The elasticity of demand for caviar is much higher than that for cereal. 

   - The inverse elasticity rule suggests that the government should tax cereal much more highly than caviar. 

- This means taxing more heavily the good consumed by poor people, worsening vertical equality. 
## Optimal Income Taxes 

- Most tax revenue in the United States and other developed countries is from income taxes 

- **Optimal income taxation:** Choosing the tax rates across income groups to maximize social welfare subject to a government revenue requirement 

- Social welfare function guides the trade-off between progressivity and efficiency 
## Conclusion 

- The fundamental issue in designing tax policy is the equityefficiency trade-off. 

- Tax efficiency comes down to two key principles: 

   - The more elastically supplied or demanded the good, the larger the deadweight loss from the tax. 

   - The higher the tax rate, the larger the incremental deadweight loss of taxation.
