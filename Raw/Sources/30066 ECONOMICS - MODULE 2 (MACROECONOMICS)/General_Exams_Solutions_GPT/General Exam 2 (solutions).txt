General Exam 2 (Solutions)

Q1. Yield curve
(a) Expectations hypothesis (no risk premium): i2_t ≈ (i1_t + i1_{t+1}^e)/2 = (2.5% + 4.5%)/2 = 3.5%.
(b) If i1_{t+1}^e rises, i2_t increases and the curve steepens (2Y rises more than 1Y). IS–LM: tighter policy next year shifts next‑year LM up, lowers Y_{t+1}, raising expected short rates; today’s long rate averages current and expected future short rates → slope increases.

Q2. Fixing M
With M fixed, LM is upward sloped in (Y,i). An anticipated tightening at t+1 lowers expected Y_{t+1}. At time t, lower expected future Y depresses I_t and C_t via expectations, shifting today’s IS left. The fall in Y_t lowers money demand today, so along a fixed M, the market clears at a LOWER i1_t. Meanwhile i2_t = avg(i1_t, i1_{t+1}^e) can still rise because i1_{t+1}^e rises; net: the curve becomes steeper (long up, short possibly down).

Q3. Fixed E (credible)
Under credibility, UIP pins i = i*. The IS shifts right because ΔC_on_impact = −c1ΔT̄ + Δc0 = −c1ΔT̄ + 0 (here Δc0 = ΔT̄ > 0 ⇒ ΔC_on_impact = (1 − c1)ΔT̄ > 0), so Y tends to rise. But i cannot change (i = i*), so LM must shift right via higher M to accommodate higher money demand at the fixed i and keep E fixed. Results: Y↑, C↑, I↑ (via Y), NX↓ (via higher Y), i unchanged, E fixed. CB raises M one‑for‑one with money demand.

Q4. ZLB fiscal
(a) With LM flat at i = 0, higher G shifts IS right → Y rises, i stays at 0 (until LM becomes upward sloped). 
(b) No crowding‑out through i; multipliers are larger (crowding‑in) because higher Y raises C and I (via accelerator). If policy is expected persistent, expectations raise future income, further lifting current demand.
(c) Conditions for multipliers >1: ZLB/very flat LM; hand‑to‑mouth/liquidity constraints; high import multipliers avoided (closed or large domestic share); accommodative monetary policy that does not offset fiscal expansion; low initial debt credibility concerns.

Q5. Δc0 = ΔT̄ > 0
Given Y response ΔY = m[(Δc0) − c1ΔT̄] = m[(ΔT̄) − c1ΔT̄] = m(1 − c1)ΔT̄ > 0. 
Private saving S^priv = Y − T − C. On impact, ΔC = c1(ΔY − ΔT̄) + Δc0. Algebra yields ΔS^priv = (1 − c1)ΔY − (1 − c1)ΔT̄ − Δc0. Substituting ΔY and Δc0 = ΔT̄: ΔS^priv = (1 − c1)m(1 − c1)ΔT̄ − (1 − c1)ΔT̄ − ΔT̄. Sign is ambiguous in general; typically S^priv can fall if c0 effect dominates. Public saving ΔS^pub = ΔT̄ (since Ḡ fixed). National saving ΔS^nat = ΔS^priv + ΔS^pub ≈ positive if ΔY is sizable; intuition: higher autonomous demand raises Y and tax revenues, so national saving often rises while private saving can fall due to higher autonomous consumption.
