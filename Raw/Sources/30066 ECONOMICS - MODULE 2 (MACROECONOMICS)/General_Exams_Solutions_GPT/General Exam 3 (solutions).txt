General Exam 3 (Solutions)

Q1. r + x with output stabilized
IS depends on r + x. Shocks: x↑ (tightens credit) and Ḡ↓ (reduces demand). To keep Y unchanged, the CB must cut r. Since both shocks are contractionary, r must fall by more than the rise in x due to the G cut as well. Result: the borrowing rate r + x FALLS (despite x↑) when the CB aggressively cuts r to offset both shocks. Intuition: hold Y constant → restore original IS intersection → r must move to neutralize x and G shifts; net r + x declines.

Q2. Debt steady state
(a) b_{t+1} = (1 + r − g) b_t − pb_t. A constant‑b steady state requires b* = −pb/(g − r) (provided g ≠ r). Signs: if pb < 0 (deficit) and g > r, b* > 0; if r > g, a deficit implies no finite positive b* (debt explodes unless pb becomes ≥ 0).
(b) pb = −0.02, r = 0.03, g = 0.015 ⇒ g − r = −0.015 < 0. Then b* = −(−0.02)/(−0.015) = 0.02/(−0.015) = −1.333… (not meaningful). With r > g and a primary deficit, no finite sustainable b*. To get b* ≤ 0.60, need either pb ≥ (r − g) b* = 0.015*0.60 = 0.009 (i.e., 0.9% primary surplus), or lower r − g via higher g or lower r.
(c) Snowball: when r > g, interest accumulation outpaces growth, so even with zero primary deficit, debt/GDP tends to rise; when r < g, growth dilutes debt.

Q4. ML and J‑curve
(a) Marshall–Lerner: the sum of absolute values of the long‑run price elasticities of export demand and import demand exceeds 1 ⇒ a real depreciation improves the trade balance in the long run. J‑curve: after a depreciation, the trade balance may initially worsen (prices dominate quantities in existing contracts) but improves over time as quantities adjust.
(b) On impact: import prices in domestic currency rise; quantities adjust slowly ⇒ NX can worsen. Over time: quantities respond (exports ↑, imports ↓); with ML satisfied, NX improves.

Q5. Ricardian equivalence
(a) Intertemporal budget for consumer with r = 0: PV income unchanged when ΔT_t = −100, ΔT_{t+1} = +100. Optimal smoothing ⇒ ΔC_t = ΔC_{t+1} = 0; the tax cut is saved today to pay the future hike: ΔS_t = +100.
(b) With liquidity‑constrained consumers, some cannot borrow to smooth; a current tax cut relaxes the constraint, so ΔC_t > 0 for them. Aggregate demand rises today even though Ricardian consumers would save it. Magnitude depends on the share of constrained agents and their MPC out of current income.
