General Exam 1 (Solutions)

Q1. IS–LM coordination
(a) With i fixed, ΔḠ = ΔT̄ > 0 raises autonomous demand A by (1 − c1)ΔḠ − c1ΔT̄ = (1 − c1)ΔḠ − c1ΔḠ = (1 − 2c1)ΔḠ.
But careful: the correct impact on IS comes from ΔC for given Y. For any given Y, ΔC = c1(−ΔT̄) and ΔG = ΔḠ, so ΔZ = ΔC + ΔI + ΔG = −c1ΔT̄ + ΔḠ = (1 − c1)ΔḠ > 0. 
Thus IS shifts right by (1 − c1)ΔḠ; Y would rise by multiplier m = 1/(1 − c1) if i stayed fixed.
(b) To keep Y at its initial level, the CB must raise i to offset the rightward IS shift, i.e., shift LM up. In the money market, this is achieved by an open‑market sale of bonds → reduces M, raises i. Bond sale lowers bond price, raises yield, consistent with higher i.
(c) Composition: I falls (higher i), C falls (higher T̄ and higher i), while G rises by ΔḠ. With Y held constant, the fall in C must offset the rise in G plus any change in I, so the mix tilts toward G and away from C (and likely I).

Q2. Private saving
S^priv = Y − T − C = Y − T̄ − [c0 + c1(Y − T̄)] = −c0 + (1 − c1)Y − (1 − c1)T̄.
Between the two equilibria, Y is fixed by the CB (same Y), T̄ rises by ΔT̄. Hence ΔS^priv = −(1 − c1)ΔT̄ < 0. 
But we must include the interest‑rate effect on C? Under the linear Keynesian form used, C depends only on disposable income, not on i; therefore S^priv falls. National saving S^nat = S^priv + S^pub with S^pub = T̄ − Ḡ: ΔS^pub = ΔT̄ − ΔḠ = 0, so national saving falls by the same amount as private saving.

Q3. Choosing i vs choosing M when c rises
The money multiplier μ = (1 + c)/(c + θ). An increase in c lowers μ (denominator rises more than numerator), so for given base H, M = μH falls.
Alfa (i‑targeter): To keep i at target, the CB must offset the fall in M by buying bonds → raise H until μH returns to the level consistent with target i. Net: i unchanged.
Beta (M‑targeter): To keep M at M̄, the CB must raise H so that μH = M̄. Net: i unchanged too. Conclusion: with a pure supply (multiplier) shock, both CBs expand H; i does not move in either regime once policy reacts appropriately.

Q4. Medium‑run natural rate r*
Lower markup m → higher PS curve, lower u_n, higher Y_n. In IS–LM–PC, this shifts the PC to the right (higher Y at zero inflation). Simultaneously, higher T̄ shifts IS left (lower demand at any r).
At the old r*, supply (Y_n) is higher while demand is lower → excess supply of goods. To restore medium‑run balance (Y = Y_n with stable inflation), r* must FALL to stimulate demand to match the higher Y_n despite higher taxes. Intuition: with more potential output and weaker autonomous demand, a lower neutral policy rate is needed.
Composition at the new medium run: G unchanged, I higher (because r* is lower and Y is higher), C ambiguous (Y_n↑ raises income, but higher T̄ lowers disposable income).

Q5. Debt dynamics
(a) b_{t+1} = (1 + r − g) b_t − pb_t = (1 + 0.06 − 0.01)*1.20 − (−0.03) = 1.05*1.20 + 0.03 = 1.26 + 0.03 = 1.29 (129%). Debt ratio rises by 9 p.p.
(b) Stabilization requires b_{t+1} = b_t = 1.20: 1.20 = (1 + r − ĝ)*1.20 − (−0.03) ⇒ (1 + r − ĝ) = (1.20 − 0.03)/1.20 = 1.17/1.20 = 0.975 ⇒ r − ĝ = −0.025 ⇒ ĝ = r + 0.025 = 0.06 + 0.025 = 0.085 (8.5%). (Equivalently from steady state b* = −pb/(g − r) = 0.03/(g − 0.06) = 1.20 ⇒ g = 0.035; note: this is the “next‑period equals 1.20” vs steady‑state algebra; if stabilizing immediately next period, ĝ = 8.5%; if stabilizing in steady state with constant pb, g = 3.5%. Be explicit which target.)
(c) Levers: raise g via productivity/investment reforms or reduce pb deficit (raise primary balance) by spending cuts or tax increases. Trade‑off: growth policies take time and are uncertain; fiscal tightening can stabilize sooner but may depress Y in the short run.
