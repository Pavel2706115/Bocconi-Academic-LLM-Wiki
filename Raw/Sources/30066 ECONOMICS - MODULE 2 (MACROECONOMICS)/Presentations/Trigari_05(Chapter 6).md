# Chapter 6 - Financial markets and the extended IS LM model

## What is this chapter about?

- Assumption so far: two financial assets - money and bonds - and one interest rate - the rate on bonds - determined by monetary policy

- We now relax this assumption

- Why? Because the financial system plays a major role in the economy, and we want to study its macroeconomics implications

## Outline

- Nominal versus real interest rates

- Risk and risk premiums

- Extending the IS-LM to think about financial shocks

## Nominal versus real interest rates

- **Nominal interest rate:** interest rate in terms of dollars/euros

- **Real interest rate:** interest rate in terms of a basket of goods

- To obtain the real interest rate, we must adjust the nominal interest rate for **expected inflation**

- When expected inflation equals zero, the nominal interest rate and the real interest rate are equal

- Because expected inflation is typically positive, the real interest rate is typically lower than the nominal interest rate

- For a given nominal interest rate, the higher expected inflation, the lower the real interest rate

- The **real interest rate** , _r = i − π(e)_ , is based on expected inflation, so it is sometimes called the **ex-ante real interest rate**

- The **realized real interest rate** , _r = i − π,_ is called the **ex-post interest rate**

## Nominal and real T-Bill rates, US, 1978-2014

The nominal interest rate has declined considerably since early 1980s, but since expected inflation has declined as well, the real rate has declined much less than the nominal rate

## Real or nominal interest rate in the IS?

- What interest rate should enter the IS relation? The **real rate** !

- Why? What matters for consumption and investment decisions is the **real interest rate**

## What are the implications for monetary policy?

- While the **CB sets the nominal interest rate** , it **cares bout the real interest rate** , as it is the one affecting spending decisions

- To set the real rate it wants, the CB has to account for expected inflation, and choose the nominal rate so that, given expected inflation, r = i - π(e)

- For example, if the CB wants r= 4% and π(e) =2, then it sets i=6%

- Note that the **zero lower bond of the nominal interest rate** implies that the real interest rate cannot be lower than the negative of inflation

- If π(e) =2%, the real interest rate cannot be lower than 0 - 2% = -2% If π(e) =-1%, real interest rate cannot be lower than 0 - (-1%) = 1%

## Risk and risk premiums

- **Some bonds are risky** , since there is a non negligible probability that the borrower will not be able or willing to repay

- To compensate for the risk, bond holders require a **risk premium**

- What factors determine risk premia?

**1. Probability of default** of the borrower

**2. Degree of risk aversion** of the bond holders

## 2. Risk aversion and risk premia

- Even if the expected return on risky bonds is the same than the return on riskless bonds, the risk itself may make bond holders reluctant to hold risky bonds

- Bond holders will require a higher risk premium, the higher is their degree of risk aversion, for given probability of default

## Financial shocks and output

- An increase in _x_ leads to a shift of the _IS_ curve to the left and a decrease in equilibrium output

- An increase in _x_ , given _r_ , increases the borrowing rate, reduces investment, demand and output

- A financial crisis becomes a macroeconomic crisis (that is, it leads to a recession)

## Financial shocks, monetary policy and output

- What can monetary policy do?

- If sufficiently large, a decrease in the policy rate can in principle offset the increase in the risk premium

- The _zero lower bound_ may however put a limit on the decrease in the real policy rate
