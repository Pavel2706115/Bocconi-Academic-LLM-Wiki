## OPENNESS

When economies are open it is possible to make international exchange of:

1. Goods/services

2. Factors of production

3. Financial assets

Advantages and disadvantages:

- More variety and opportunities

- More competition and lower prices

- Interdependencies: shocks/policies in a country may affect other countries

Economies are becoming more interconnected…

## ….which increases the risk of **contagion in the global economies**

A crisis in one country can quickly spread to others through trade, financial markets, and investment flows

- The 2007-2009 crisis started in the US, but then diffused at the global level

- Covid-19 has highlighted the importance of international connections

- - The war in Ukraine had an economic impact at the world level (energy prices, availability of goods, global value chains)

## OPENNESS IN REAL ECONOMY

- Countries exchange goods/services (final and intermediate)

- The production process is broken across countries: firms specialize in a specific task and do not produce the whole product

- Comparative advantage

- Economies of scale and specialization

- Countries may affect the amount of trade imposing:

- Tariffs/Quotas (Direct taxes or limits on imported goods)

- Non-tariff barriers (technical standards and regulations, bureaucratic procedures and customs delays, product labeling requirements, sanitary and phytosanitary measures (especially in food and agriculture)

- International cooperation to reduce barriers:

- WTO

- Bilateral and regional agreement (EU;
- NAFTA;
- TPP;
- Mercosur, …)

## BALANCE OF PAYMENTS

- A set of accounts that summarizes a country’s transactions with the rest of the world

- It is composed by three main accounts:

1. Current Account (CA): records payments to and from the rest of the world

2. Capital Account (KA): acquisition of non-financial assets by residents in the rest of the world, or by non-residents

- CA + KA = exposure of an economy to the rest of the world

3. Financial Account: net changes in assets

- FA = how it is financed

## How is the exchange rate set in the markets?

It depends on the exchange rate regime chosen by the country

_E_ can freely fluctuate over time based on demand and supply (purchases and sales) of the two currencies in the foreign exchange market

→ If the D of a currency ↑, its price ↑ with respect to the price of the other currency => E ↑

E is kept constant over time at a predetermined level (exchange rate parity), set by the monetary authorities

→ Demand and supply imbalances are corrected through interventions by the Central Bank (buying or selling foreign currency)

→ Instantaneous adjustments in the market as for € and $

→ No automatic adjustment → Adjustment happens through Central Bank actions, not by market forces

Argentina pegged the exchange rate at 1 Peso per 1 US Dollar

1) FLOATING EXCHANGE RATE: E is the relative price of the domestic currency

the value of a currency depends on how desirable it is in the market for carrying out transactions

→ At any moment in the foreign exchange market, currencies are being bought and sold

- → The relative demand for the domestic currency (compared to the foreign one) is therefore determined by these market transactions
