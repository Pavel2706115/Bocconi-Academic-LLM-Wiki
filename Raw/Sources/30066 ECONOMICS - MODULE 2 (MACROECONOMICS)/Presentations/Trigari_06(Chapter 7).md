- The labor market is in general very dynamic!

- In general, we observe large **flows** of people between employment, unemployment, and non-participation

- However, a given unemployment rate may reflect

- A **dynamic labor market** , with **many separations** (workers who are leaving for another job and workers losing their jobs) and **many hires** (workers newly employed by firms)

- A **sclerotic labor market** with **few separations** , **few hires** and a stagnant unemployment pool

## In general, there are **common forces** at work

1. Workers are typically paid a wage higher than their **reservation wage** , the wage that makes them indifferent between working or being unemployed

2. Wages typically depend on **labor market conditions** . The lower the unemployment rate, the higher the wages

How can we **explain these facts?** Two theories:

1. Bargaining

2. Efficiency wages

## 1. Bargaining

How much **bargaining power** a worker has depends on two factors

- How costly it would be for the firm to replace him

- How hard it would be for him to find another job

Bargaining power will then depend on

- **Skills**: highly skilled workers are more difficult to replace; they will have more bargaining power

- **Labor market conditions**: when the unemployment rate is low, it is easier for workers to find other jobs and more difficult for firms to find replacement workers; workers will have more bargaining power

## Wage bargaining set

Firms are willing to pay at most this Wage depends on Wage bargaining power! Reservation wage (below this worker prefer to remain unemployed)

## 2. Efficiency wages

Firms may want to pay higher wage than the reservation wage to **incentivize workers** to be productive (efficient) or to stay at the firm

- **Nature of the job**: firms (such as high-tech firms) that see employee morale and commitment as essential will pay more than firms in sectors where workers’ activities are more routine

- **Labor market conditions**: when unemployment is low, quits and turnover will be higher, and firms will pay higher wages to compensate and retain workers

## Annual turnover and layoff rates (%) at Ford, 1913–1915

- Turnover rate: ratio of separations to employment

- Layoff rate: ratio of layoffs to employment

- In 1914, Henry Ford, the builder of Model-T, announced that his company would pay all qualified employees a minimum of $5.00 a day for an eighthour day, compared to previously an average $2.30 for a nine-hour day

- The turnover rate plunged from 370% in 1913 to 16% in 1915

- The layoff rate collapsed from 62% to nearly 0%

## The expected price level

First ignore the difference between _P_ and _P(e)_

Both workers and firms care about **real wages** _(W/P),_ **not nominal wages** _(W)_

- Workers do not care about how many dollars they receive but about **how many goods they can buy** with those dollars. They care about _W/P_

- Firms do not care about the nominal wages they pay but about the nominal wages, _W_ , they pay **relative to the price of the goods they sell** , _P_ . They also care about _W/P_ ^atu360

Now, **why** do **wages depend on the expected price level** _**$$$P(e)$$$**_ as opposed to the actual price level _P_ ?

Because **wages are set in nominal terms** , and when they are set the price level is not yet known

- Think about union contracts setting nominal wages in advance for one or more years

- Even when wages are set by firms or by bargaining between workers and firms, nominal wages are typically set for one year and if the price level raises unexpectedly during the year, nominal wages are not readjusted

## The unemployment rate

- If we think of wages as being determined by bargaining, then higher unemployment weakens workers’ bargaining power, forcing them to accept lower wages

- If we think of wages as being determined by efficiency wages considerations, then higher unemployment allows firms to pay lower wages and still retain workers

## Short and medium run

- We have focused on the labor market, and we have assumed that the price level is equal to the expected price level

- In the **short run** , the **price level** may well turn out to be **different from what is expected** when nominal wages are set, so that unemployment is not necessarily equal to the natural rate or output equal to its natural level
*(See also: [[L12 Labor Market#^hcrdyo]])*

- In the short run output continues to be determined by factors such as fiscal and monetary policy

- Because **expectations are unlikely to be systematically wrong** , in the **medium run** , output tends to return to its natural level

- The next chapter will relax the assumption that the price level is equal to the expected price level and derive a relation between unemployment and inflation
