## MAIN DETERMINANTS OF AGGREGATE OUTPUT

The amount of goods and services an economy can produce is determined by different factors in different time horizons:

- **SHORT RUN** (few quarters/some years): The level of the demand

- **MEDIUM RUN** (a decade or more): Structural supply factors (population size, features of the labor market, productivity (human and physical, technology)

- **LONG RUN** (a century or more): Institutional factors (educational system, quality of political institutions, justice …)

**A SHORT-RUN MODEL** to explain income variations in the short run

## 3. ECONOMIC INTUITION

- Why does a change in G result in a more than proportional change in Y?

There are some direct and indirect effects

- Direct effect: ↑ G = ↑Y

- The increase in government expenditure raises demand

- As demand is higher, firms produce more

- Indirect effect:↑Y → ↑ C by c1 → ↑ Z → ↑ Y → ↑ C….

- More production = More income

- If income increases, consumption increases (but less than proportionally)

- Demand increases  production increases…

- … demand increases further more…

**The final effect (sum of direct and indirect effects) on output is more than proportional**

## THE MULTIPLIER

- The goods market multiplier captures the sum of all direct and indirect effects

- Given an initial change in the autonomous spending, in equilibrium, the production increases more than proportionally

- It measures by how much income changes if autonomous spending changes by a given amount

## CASE STUDY: THE COVID-19 AND THE GOODS MARKET

- COVID-19 led to a sudden economic downturn due to lockdowns

- Huge demand shocks impacted the economy

- We can use the model to answer two key questions

- How did aggregate demand change?

- How did policies respond?

## IMPACT ON AGGREGATE DEMAND COMPONENTS

- C↓ due to restrictions, lower income and uncertainty

- I↓ as firms postponed spending

- Stronger impact on some sectors

- X and IM ↓due to ddisruptions in global trade

- Aggregate demand ↓  Z = C + I + G + X – IM

- Fall in output and economic downturns

- Government intervention to stimulate demand and prevent deep recession. – Expansionary FP to stabilize Y with

- ↑ G: more investment in health, investment in infrastructures, spending in education and digitalization, finance vaccine research, production, and free distribution (US, EU, UK, France, Italy, China, India)

- ↓ T: unemployment benefits, deferred income tax, VAT, and payroll tax payments, cut Value Added Tax rates (Germany, UK, France, Italy)

- Rapid demand increase in 2021-2022

## CONCLUSIONS: LESSON FROM COVID-19

- Importance of government intervention in demand shocks

- Role of fiscal policy to ensure economic stability

- Need for balancing recovery with inflation control
