## **SKETCH OF ANSWER** 

_With risk averse investors, the yield on two-year bonds exceeds the average of the current and future expected yields on 1-year bonds by an amount that is larger the greater is the risk premium, x. It follows that, with risk aversion, a positively sloped yield curve does not necessarily signal expectations of a rise in short term rates (for instance, when x>0 the yield curve will be positively sloped even if investors expect future short rates to be equal to current short rates). Similarly, the fact that Beta’s yield curve is steeper than Alpha’s could be due only to the larger risk premium prevailing in Beta, rather than to the fact that the yield on one-year bonds is expected to rise more in Beta than in Alpha._

## **SKETCH OF ANSWER** 

- _Correct figure: B._

- _Since the central bank keeps the interest rate at the initial level, the economy reaches equilibrium 2, where income is lower (due to the fall in demand that has disturbed the initial equilibrium), I lower (because of the decrease in Y), G unchanged, C lower (both due to its decrease on impact, for given Y, mentioned in the second point of this answer, and because of the fall in equilibrium income). - _As the economy goes from 1 to 2, money demand decreases (i unchanged; Y lower); it follows that money supply will have to fall by the same amount money demand has gone down. The central bank will have to lower M to prevent the decrease in money demand from lowering interest rate below the level it has chosen (i*)._

## **SKETCH OF ANSWER** 

_Equilibrium income and net exports will vary in the same direction and by the same amount discussed when answering the previous question.  The effect of the changes in consideration in an economy under a flexible exchange rate in which the central bank has chosen to set the domestic interest rate equal to the foreign one is identical to that prevailing in an economy under a fixed exchange rate, but otherwise identical. The only difference between the two economies is that, in the second one, the central bank must set i= i* to keep the exchange rate fixed, while in the first economy a domestic interest rate equal to i* is just a choice of the central bank._

## **SKETCH OF ANSWER** 

- _Since the debt/GDP line has a positive vertical intercept, d>0 (the government runs a primary deficit); and since the slope of the same line is less than 1, r<g._ 

- _Absent policy interventions, the debt/GDP ratio would fall over time, approaching the steady state level b overbar in the figure.
