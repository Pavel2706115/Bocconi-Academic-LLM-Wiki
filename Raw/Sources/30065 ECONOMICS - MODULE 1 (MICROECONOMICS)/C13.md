# Econ 30065: Microeconomics Sara Negrelli Monopoly

## Topics

- Market power - Monopoly pricing - Welfare effects of monopoly pricing

## Market Power

- - **Market power:** when a firm can profitably charge a price that is above its marginal cost (P>MC)

- **Monopoly market:** a market with a single seller - **Oligopoly market:** a market with a few sellers

- In order for a firm to have market power, competition cannot be intense on that market - Determining if a market is a monopoly can be trickier than simple definitions might suggest

- Need to define the relevant market, which includes close substitutes

## How do firms become monopolists?

- Government awards and patents (cable TV companies in local communities, drug patents)

- Innovation (iPod)

- Erode over time unless continuous innovation

- Ownership of all of an essential input (De Beers diamond producer in the 80s) - Other firms do not find the market profitable (ex: economies of scale)

## Monopoly Pricing

- Monopolist will choose the price (and quantity) that maximizes its profit (total Revenue – total Cost), given the demand for its product - Optimality condition $$MR=MC$$

- If marginal revenue exceeded marginal cost the firm would be better off selling more - If marginal revenue were less than marginal cost the firm would be better off selling less - Marginal cost curve applies as usual

- But now firm is not price taker (as in perfect competition): MR ≠ P

- Need to examine the shape of the marginal revenue curve: additional revenue from selling one extra unit of the good

## Marginal Revenue

- Increase in sales quantity, from _Q –_ Δ _Q_ to _Q_ changes revenue in two ways - **Output expansion effect**: sell Δ _Q_ additional units, each at price of _P(Q)_ - **Price reduction effect**: increased sales quantity requires a Δ to reduction in price from _P(Q – Q) $P(Q)$_ for all the previous units

## Marginal Revenue

- In Perfect competition price reduction effect is zero (price takers) → $$MR=P$$ - In Monopoly it is negative → MR<P

## Monopolist profit maximization

- **Step 1: Quantity rule**

- Identify positive sales quantities at which $$MR=MC.$$ If more than one positive sales satisfies the conditions, identify the one that produces the highest profit.

- **Step 2: Shut-down rule**

- Check whether the most profitable positive sales quantity from Step 1 is greater profit than shutting down. If it is, that is the profit maximizing choice. If not, then selling nothing is the best option - TR ≥VariableCosts - p≥AVC(q) - p≥min AVC

## Markup: A Measure of Market Power

- A monopolist facing a downward sloping demand curve will set its price above marginal cost

## Markup: A Measure of Market Power

- Monopolist’s markup at its profit-maximizing price always equals the reciprocal of the elasticity of demand, times negative one

- The less elastic the demand curve, the greater the firm’s markup over its marginal cost

- When demand is less elastic, raising the price is more attractive because fewer sales are lost

## Welfare Effects of Monopoly Pricing

- By charging a price above marginal cost, the monopolist makes consumers worse off than under perfect competition

- Consumers who buy the product pay more for it

- Some who would have bought it under perfect competition will not buy it at the higher price - Welfare effects of monopoly pricing:

- Firm gains - Consumers lose

- Deadweight loss incurred

- Deadweight loss from monopoly pricing is the amount by which aggregate surplus falls short of its maximum possible level, which is attained in a competitive market
