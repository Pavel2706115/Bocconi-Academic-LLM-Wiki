# Econ 30065: Microeconomics Sara Negrelli

C5

## Preferences

- **Preferences** tell us about a consumer’s likes and dislikes

- (Assumptions) Rational consumers follow two basic decision-making principles:

- **Ranking principle**

- **Choice principle**

- **(Assumption) The more is better Principle**

## Principles of Decision making

- **Ranking principle**: a consumer can rank, in order of preference (though possibly with ties), all potentially available consumption bundles

## Implications

- Preferences are **complete**: between any pair of alternatives, a consumer either prefers one to the other or is indifferent between them.

- A consumer is **indifferent** between two alternatives if he likes (or dislikes) them equally

- Preferences are **transitive**: if the consumer prefers one alternative to a second, and prefers the second alternative to a third, then he also prefers the first alternative to the third.

## Principles of Decision Making

- **The choice principle**: among the available alternatives, the consumer selects the one that he ranks the highest:

X, Y, Z

- If Z is preferred to X, and - Z is preferred to Y, then - Z is the selected alternative

## Principles of Decision Making

- **The More-is-better Principle**: When one consumption bundle contains more of every good than a second bundle, a consumer prefers the first bundle to the second (free disposal)

## Substitution between goods

- The IC contains information about the rate at which a consumer can substitute one good for another so that she is indifferent between the two bundles

- The rate of substitution between 2 bundles is given by –ΔY/ΔX

## Marginal rate of substitution between goods

- The rate at which Madeline is willing to substitute small amounts of pizza with yogurt is called **marginal rate of substitution for yoghurt with pizza** - The marginal rate of substitution at bundle A equals the (absolute value) of the slope of the line drawn tangent to the indifference curve running through point A.
*(See also: [[C7#^3zq2c0]])*

## Substitution between goods

**Marginal Rate of Substitution for X with Y(at a given point)**: The amount of  good Y that a consumer is willing to give up for an extra unit of good X, such that the agent is on the same indifference curve as before.

## MRS characteristics

- Assume convex indifference curves (preference for variety)

- Within the same indifference curve, as pizza becomes more scarce and yogurt becomes more plentiful, the MRS for yogurt with pizza falls

## MRS characteristics

- Angie likes yogurt relative to pizza more than Marcus - Angie’s MRS for yogurt with pizza is higher than Marcus’ MRS
