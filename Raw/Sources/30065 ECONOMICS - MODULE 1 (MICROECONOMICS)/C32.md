# Microeconomics Sara Negrelli Asymmetric information-Adverse Selection

## Adverse Selection

- A firm hiring a worker may know less than the worker does about the worker's ability and hence about her productivity - The buyer of a used car may know less than the seller does about the quality of the car

- A bank financing an entrepreneur's project may know less than the entrepreneur does about the entrepreneur's skills and hence about the profitability of the project

- A company providing car insurance may know less than the driver does about the driver's driving skills

## Adverse Selection

- **Adverse selection** is the phenomenon under which the informed side of a transaction has information that the other side does not have about some aspect of product quality and this leads to a poor performance of the market.

- Not possible to separate low quality from high quality products: only one price

- This leads to:

- the disappearance of high quality products from the market - A higher price for low quality products than under symmetric information → inefficiency

## Adverse Selection

- The basic reason for inefficiency: we have several goods but only one price

- - In the examples above, all goods look the same before they are bought

- This prevents the creation of two separate markets, each with its own price

- No single price can simultaneously persuade higher-quality sellers to trade at that price and dissuade the lower-quality ones from doing so
