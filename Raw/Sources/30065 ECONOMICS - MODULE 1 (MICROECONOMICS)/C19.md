# Econ 30065: Microeconomics Sara Negrelli

## Market intervention

## Taxes

- Governments tax goods to raise the revenue needed to pay public expenditures

- Taxation alters the competitive equilibrium

- There are different kinds of taxes. We will focus on:

- **Specific tax:** a fixed dollar amount that must be paid on each unit bought or sold - Example: 3$ per unit

## Effects of a specific tax T on sellers

- Supply curve shifts left by T - Point A shows the market equilibrium - Point B shows the increase in the price paid by consumers due to the tax

- Point C shows the decrease in the price received by producers due to the tax

- Area Pb-Ps-B-C: total taxes collected.

## Effects of a specific tax T on sellers

- For any price paid by consumers, firms now receive less than when there is no tax

- Won’t be willing to supply as much as before

- Supply curve with the tax is a distance T above the original supply curve

- New equilibrium price paid by consumers is price at which the demand curve and new supply curve cross

- Amount bought and sold falls

- Price paid by consumers rises; price received by firms falls

## Incidence of a specific Tax

- **Incidence of a tax:** how much of the tax burden is borne by various market participants.

- From the graph we see that the burden of the tax is shared by consumers and producers

## Incidence of a specific Tax

- **Incidence of a tax:** how much of the tax burden is borne by various market participants. - It depends on elasticity of demand and supply curves - 4 cases:

- Perfectly elastic demand: sellers bear the entire burden of the tax - Perfectly inelastic supply: sellers bear the entire burden of the tax - Perfectly inelastic demand: buyers bear the entire burden of the tax - Perfectly elastic supply: buyers bear the entire burden of the tax
