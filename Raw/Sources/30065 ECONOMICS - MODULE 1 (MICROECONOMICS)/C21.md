## Externalities 

Ø Today, we see another reason for inefficiency: _externalities_ Ø One of the key assumption of the competitive market model we had was that 

Ø Each consumer’s utility depends only on her own consumption Ø Each consumer’s action affects only his own well-being Ø In reality this assumption is often violated: Ø A person delighting neighbors playing piano Ø A power generator polluting air, contributing to respiratory diseases Ø Article we saw at the beginning on gas production Ø The actions in these example create _externalities_ 

## Externalities 

Ø An action creates an _**externality**_ if it affects someone with whom the decision-maker has not engaged in a related market transaction 

Ø _**Negative externality**_ if it harms someone else ØPolluting causes **health problems in a community** Ø _**Positive externality**_ if it benefits someone else ØCarefully tended garden causes a **raise in the value of surrounding homes** 

## Negative Externalities and inefficiency in competitive markets 

Ø Presence of a negative externality in a competitive market will usually allocate economic resources inefficiently Ø With a negative externality there is an _**external cost**_ , the economic harm that a negative externality imposes on others: EC(Q)>0 ( MEC(Q)>0) 

- Ø Optimality condition in competitive markets: each firm produces up to the point at which **its** marginal cost curve equals its marginal revenue(market price): P=MC 

- Ø Since P=MB èMB=MC 

- Ø But for a level of production to be socially efficient it must be that marginal **social** cost and marginal **social** benefit are equal Ø Marginal social benefit is equal to marginal benefit (no positive externality) 

   - Ø Marginal social cost = marginal cost to the producer + marginal external cost 

      - MSC=MC+MEC 

- Ø Therefore the firm’s equilibrium production level is socially excessive and inefficient: MB=MSB<MSC
