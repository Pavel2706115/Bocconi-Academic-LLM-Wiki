# Econ 30065: Microeconomics Sara Negrelli

Externalities

## Externalities

- Today, we see another reason for inefficiency: _externalities_ - One of the key assumption of the competitive market model we had was that

- Each consumer’s utility depends only on her own consumption - Each consumer’s action affects only his own well-being - In reality this assumption is often violated: - A person delighting neighbors playing piano - A power generator polluting air, contributing to respiratory diseases - Article we saw at the beginning on gas production - The actions in these example create _externalities_
*(See also: [[C22#^uupmvf]])*

## Externalities

- An action creates an _**externality**_ if it affects someone with whom the decision-maker has not engaged in a related market transaction

- _**Negative externality**_ if it harms someone else - Polluting causes **health problems in a community** - _**Positive externality**_ if it benefits someone else - Carefully tended garden causes a **raise in the value of surrounding homes**

## Negative Externalities and inefficiency in competitive markets

- Presence of a negative externality in a competitive market will usually allocate economic resources inefficiently - With a negative externality there is an _**external cost**_ , the economic harm that a negative externality imposes on others: EC(Q)>0 ( MEC(Q)>0) ^jak6ko

- - Optimality condition in competitive markets: each firm produces up to the point at which **its** marginal cost curve equals its marginal revenue(market price): P=MC

- - Since P=MB èMB=MC

- - But for a level of production to be socially efficient it must be that marginal **social** cost and marginal **social** benefit are equal - Marginal social benefit is equal to marginal benefit (no positive externality) ^1p2qlj

- - Marginal social cost = marginal cost to the producer + marginal external cost

- MSC=MC+MEC

- - Therefore the firm’s equilibrium production level is socially excessive and inefficient: MB=MSB<MSC
