## Which goods should be taxed? 

ØTax goods for which the deadweight loss of taxation will be low 

ØDeadweight loss of taxation will be low if either the demand or the supply curve is very inelastic: 

ØIf supply or demand is perfectly inelastic, for example, there is no deadweight loss: The tax does not change the quantity bought and sold

## Subsidies 

Ø **Subsidy:** payment that reduces the amount that buyers pay for a good or increases the amount that sellers receive (example: subsidy of 3$ on each unit sold or bought) ØIn competitive markets, subsidies create: Øincrease sales of the affected goods Ø **deadweight loss**

## Effect of a subsidy T to firms 

Ø For any price paid by consumers, firms now receive more than when there is no subsidy 

Ø willing to supply more than before 

Ø Supply curve with the subsidy is a distance T below the original supply curve 

Ø New equilibrium price paid by consumers is price at which the demand curve and new supply curve cross 

Ø Amount bought and sold increases 

Ø Price paid by consumers falls; price received by firms increases 

Ø In a competitive market the benefit of a subsidy is shared by consumers and firms 

## Effect of a subsidy on buyers 

ØRegardless of who receives the subsidy by the government, consumers and producers obtain the same benefit. (like in taxes) ØThe share of the benefit received depends on the elasticity of the demand and supply curves 

## Welfare effects of a subsidy 

Ø Welfare analysis of a subsidy shows consumers and producers both gain Ø The sum of the reduction in price to consumers and the increase in price to firms exactly equals the size of the subsidy: T Ø Aggregate surplus falls (CS+PS+GR) Ø This is because the government incurs an expense, the per-unit subsidy times the number of units sold
