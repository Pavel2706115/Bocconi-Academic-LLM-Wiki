## Nash Equilibrium in One-Stage Games

- - Concept created by mathematician John Nash, published in 1950, awarded Nobel Prize

- - Has become one of the most central and important concepts in microeconomics

- - In a _**Nash equilibrium**_ , the action played by each individual is a best response to the actions played by everyone else - Everyone correctly anticipates what everyone else will do and then chooses the best available alternative

- - Combination of action in a Nash equilibrium is stable (people are doing their best, given the action of the opponents)

- - In other words, once players have seen action chosen by others, noone wants to unilaterally deviate from what he chose.

- - A game can have 1, 2 or more NE in pure strategies, or also NO Equilibria in pure strategies

- In practice:

1. for each action of the opponent, calculate the best responses for each player

2. Find the profiles of actions where both players are playing best responses

## Nash Equilibria in Games with Finely Divisible Choices

- Concept of Nash equilibrium also applies to strategic decisions that involve finely divisible quantities (infinite number of actions) - Determine each player’s best response function

- A _**best response function**_ shows the relationship between one player’s best response and other players’ choices - A pair of choices is a Nash equilibrium if they satisfy both best response functions simultaneously

## Example: Free Riding in Groups

- - Scott and Liz need to write a research report together. They have 24 hours to do the research. Their promotion depends on how good is this report.  Each conducts research simultaneously and independently

- - Their choice is how much time to spend on the report - They can’t observe each other’s choice.

- - **Free-riding problem:** each player would prefer to spend less time on the report if the other is spending enough time on it - the best response functions will be downward sloping. If Scott (Liz) knows Liz (Scott) will work hard, he (she) will work less.

- Intersection (N.E.) – the only point at which each player’s action is the best response to what the other player is doing.
