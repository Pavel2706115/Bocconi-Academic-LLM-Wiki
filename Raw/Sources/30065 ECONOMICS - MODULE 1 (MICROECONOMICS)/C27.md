# Econ 30065: Microeconomics Sara Negrelli

Choices under uncertainty

## Introduction

- Up until now, we assumed that individuals made decisions under _**perfect information**_ - Choices with certainty - In reality, many decision are made under uncertainty - A choice has many possible consequences - Example: a guy receives as an  inheritance the possibility of picking 2 paintings at his uncle house. There is a 1% probability the two paintings are stolen on his way back home, and a 18% probability that 1 painting is stolen.

- How do we make choices when certain variables are uncertain (making choices with risk)?

## Describing risk

- Risk exists whenever the consequences of a decision are uncertain. - In economics, we call any situation involving uncertainty a _**lottery**_ - _**A lottery**_ is a set of outcomes, each with an associate payoff, and each happening with some probability

- Consumers choose among lotteries→ we need to describe them.

- Example: Alberto is trying to decide whether to buy tickets to a baseball game. His enjoyment of the game depends on an uncertain event beyond his control: whether it rains. He will be happiest if he does not rain. èBuying tickets is a lottery! The possible outcomes are: it rains, it does not rain.  The payoff is higher if it does not rain. Probabilities are involved: there is a chance it rains.

- To describe lotteries we must know:

1. All the possible outcomes

2. The **probability** or likelihood that each outcome will occur

3. The **payoffs** of each outcome

## Describing risk

- An **outcome** is one possible way in which events relevant to a risky decision can unfold

- To analyze a risky decision, begin by describing every possible outcome

- _**Outcomes set**_ is the set of all possible outcomes

- Once the outcomes of the lottery is realized, the agent experiences only one consequence

- Each outcome rules out the others

## Describing risk

- Some outcomes are more likely than others - _**Probability**_ is a measure of the likelihood that a given outcome will occur

- A number between 0 and 1, or a percentage - Probability of 0 means an outcome is impossible, probability of 1 means it’s certain - Add the probabilities of two outcomes to obtain the probability that one of those two outcomes will occur - The probabilities of all outcomes always add to 1; it’s certain that something will happen - The _**probability distribution**_ of a set of outcomes gives the likelihood that each possible outcome will occur
*(See also: [[C28#^v2d93a]])*

## Describing risk

- Risky choices often have financial consequences, **payoffs** - Payoffs can be positive (gains) or negative (losses)

- Every outcome has an associate payoff. Since every outcome happens with some probability, that probability is also the probability of the associate payoff.

## Expected value

- To determine the average gain or loss from a lottery, we can calculate its expected value - _**Expected value**_ (or expected payoff) of a risky choice is a weighted average of all the possible payoffs, using the probability of each payoff as its weight

= _EV PV PV PV_ ... + _PV_ 1 1 + 2 2 + 3 3 + _n n_ where _Pi_ is probability of outcome _i_ occuring, _Vi_ is value of the outcome if state _i_ occurs

## Decision making under uncertainty

- Agents choose among lotteries - We know the expected value of a lottery - How do agents choose?

- Depends on the individual - Some may be willing to take risk with higher expected value

- Some will prefer less risk even with lower expected value èDecision making under uncertainty èExpected utility functions èThe agent will pick the one that gives him the highest expected utility

## Expected Utility Functions

- An _**expected utility function:**_

- Assigns a utility level to each payoff resulting from a possible outcome

- Then take the expected value of those utilities - It is a weighted average of all possible utility levels using the probability of each level as its weight

## Preferences toward risk

- We see empirically that people differ in their preference toward risk

- People can be _risk averse, risk neutral,_ or _risk loving_

## Certainty equivalent

- The _**certainty equivalent**_ of a risky bundle is the amount of money which, if provided with certainty, would make the consumer equally well off: $$$$U(CE)=E(U(lottery))$$$$

## Risk premium

- The _**risk premium**_ of a risky bundle is the difference between its expected value and the ’ consumer s certainty equivalent: risk premium=(EV-CE) - Amount by which a consumer is willing to reduce expected consumption to eliminate all risk ^mjxlup
