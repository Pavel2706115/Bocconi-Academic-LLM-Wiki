# Econ 30065: Microeconomics Sara Negrelli

Choices under uncertainty

## Risk aversion

- A person is _**risk averse**_ if, in comparing a riskless bundle to a risky bundle with the same expected value, he prefers the riskless bundle

- Concave utility function

- Degree of risk aversion may vary from person to person, or with age for the same person

- The greater the concavity, the greater the risk aversion - For a _**risk-averse**_ person, the certainty equivalent of a risky bundle is always less than expected value - Providing the same expected value with no risk would make the individual better off - For a _**risk-averse**_ person, the risk premium is positive
*(See also: [[C27#^mjxlup]])*

## Risk aversion example

- A person can have a $20,000 job with probability 1 and receives a utility level of 16 - The person could have a job with a 0.5 chance of earning $30,000 and a 0.5 chance of earning $10,000. - Utility at $30,000 is 18 - Utility at $10,000 is 10

## Risk aversion example

- Expected value of Risky Job EV=0.5 \times 30000+0.5 \times 10000=20000

- Expected Utility of Risky Job E(U)=0.5(18)+0.5 \times 10=14

- Expected value from both jobs is the same – risk averse may choose current job - Expected utility is greater for certain job - Would keep certain job ^v2d93a

## Risk neutrality

- A person is _**risk neutral**_ if, in comparing a riskless bundle to a risky bundle with the same expected value, he is indifferent between the two

- Linear utility function

- For a _**risk-neutral**_ person, the certainty equivalent of a risky bundle is always equal to the expected value - Providing the same expected value with no risk would make the individual indifferent

- For a _**risk-neutral**_ person, the risk premium is zero income

## Risk loving

- A person is _**risk lover**_ if, in comparing a riskless bundle to a risky bundle with the same expected value, he prefers the risky bundle

- Convex utility function

- The greater the convexity, the greater the risk loving - For a _**risk-loving**_ person, the certainty equivalent of a risky bundle is always more than expected value - Providing the same expected value with no risk would make the individual worse off - For a _**risk-loving**_ person, the risk premium is negative

## Risk loving example

A person can have a $20,000 job with probability 1 and receive a utility level of 8. The person could have a job with a 0.5 chance of earning $30,000 and a 0.5 chance of earning $10,000. Utility at $30,000 is 18 and Utility at $10,000 is 3.

## Risk loving example

- Expected value of Risky Job E(I)=0.5 \times 30000+0.5 \times 10000=20000

- Expected Utility of Risky Job E(U)=0.5(18)+0.5 \times 3=10.5

- Expected income from both jobs is the same - Expected utility is greater for uncertain job - Would take uncertain job
