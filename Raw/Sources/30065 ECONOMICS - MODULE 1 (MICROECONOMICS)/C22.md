## Remedies for Externalities:

## Private Sector

- Let’s focus on negative externalities (same holds for positive externalities)

- Whenever the allocation of resources is inefficient we now it is possible to arrange mutually beneficial trades - Private parties have incentives to identify inefficiencies and negotiate solutions: this will increase their surplus

## PRIVATE NEGOTIATION

## Example: Negotiation

- Factory earns $600,000 in profits and it pollutes a river; to abate pollution, it can buy a water purifier at cost C=100,000

- A farmer uses the water for irrigation - He makes $370,000 if water clean and $250,000 if water polluted

- Joint profits:

- - 850,000 if factory pollutes

- 970,000 – C if factory does not pollute

## Example: Negotiation

- It is efficient to invest in water purifier (total surplus would increase)

- If the factory has the right to pollute the river

- Then the farmer will pay for the water purifier, because that would increase his profits: 370,000-100,000>250,000. The factory has same profits as before: 600,000. - Pareto improvement! - No pollution

- If the farmer has the right, the company will pay for the purifier, and has lower profits of 500,000 - If abatement of pollution is efficient, market will negotiate to eliminate it!

## Property rights

- Who has the property rights does not influence the level of pollution but influences only the profits!

- If it is efficient not to have pollution, no matter who has the property rights, pollution disappears

- If it is efficient not to abate pollution, no matter who has the property rights, pollution does not disappear

- _**Coase theorem:**_ If bargaining is frictionless, regardless of how property rights are assigned, voluntary agreements will restore efficiency

## Limitations of Bargaining

- In our example the farmer and the factory are creating a new market: the market for exchanging a right (to pollute) - They remedy the externality by creating a new market - Every externality can be traced to missing markets

- - Private negotiations lead to transactions that the parties would have made if the required markets were not missing

- - Problem: many factors that cause externalities also make bargaining fail

- Bargaining can be impractical

- - Difficult logistics, substantial time and effort (e.g. air pollution caused by traffic)

- - Property rights may be ambiguous (who has the rights to the river,  the farmer or the factory?) → legal battles

- - Limited information can lead to an impasse (parties may have an incentive to over/under state the costs of the externality)

- - Contracts may be difficult to enforce

## Public sector intervention

- When private negotiations fail to address market failures associated with externalities, government policies can potentially improve **social efficiency** ^h4b3b4

- **Social efficiency:** the socially efficient outcome maximizes the total surplus, no matter whether one party will be worse off (with respect to perfect competition)

- - Socially efficient outcome is Pareto efficient, but it may not be a Pareto improvement over perfect competition outcome

1. Policies that support markets (e.g., establishing clear property rights)

2. Quantity controls (e.g., emissions standards) 3. Policies that correct private incentives (e.g., taxes, fees, subsidies)

## Policies that support a market

- In some situations, governments can address externalities by helping the private sector create the necessary markets

- Establish clear property rights, pass laws that protect those rights, and enforce contracts - **Property rights**: enforceable claim on a good or resource

- Sometimes market remedies are not possible

## Quantity Controls

- Government can attempt to address an externality by regulating the activity that produces it

- An _**emissions standard**_ is a legal limit on the amount of pollution that a person or company can produce when engaged in a particular activity

## Policies that Correct Private Incentives

- Some policies address externalities by forcing people to internalize external costs and benefits

- Impose taxes or fees, provide subsidies (Pigouvian remedies) - _**Pigouvian taxation**_ involves the use of taxes or fees to remedy negative externalities

- A Pigouvian tax forces a decision-maker to internalize the marginal external costs associated with her activity - Set tax equal to MEC(atQ efficient)èMC=MSC!

- Ideal Pigouvian tax reproduces the price that the good in question would command in an efficient competitive equilibrium
