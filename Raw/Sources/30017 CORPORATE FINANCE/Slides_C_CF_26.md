# Corporate Finance 30017 - Slides C

## Payout Policy
*   How a firm distributes its free cash flows back to shareholders.
*   **Dividends:** Cash payments (regular, special, or liquidating).
*   **Share Repurchases:** The firm buys back its own stock (Open market, Tender offer, Dutch auction, Private negotiation).
*   **Modigliani-Miller (MM) Irrelevance:** In perfect capital markets, payout policy is irrelevant. Firm value depends on its investment policy, not how it distributes cash.
*   **Market Imperfections:**
    *   *Taxes:* If dividends are taxed at a higher rate than capital gains, repurchases are preferred.
    *   *Information Content (Signaling):* Managers are reluctant to cut dividends. A dividend increase signals confidence in sustainable future earnings. A dividend cut is a strong negative signal.
    *   *Agency Costs:* Dividends constrain managers from wasting free cash flow.

## Capital Structure and MM Propositions
*   **MM Proposition I (No Taxes):** The market value of a firm is independent of its capital structure. $V_U = V_L$. 
    *   *Homemade Leverage:* Investors can replicate the firm's leverage by borrowing/lending on their own.
*   **MM Proposition II (No Taxes):** The expected return on equity increases proportionally with the Debt/Equity ratio. $r_E = r_A + (r_A - r_D)(D/E)$.
*   **MM Proposition I (With Corporate Taxes):** Debt provides a tax shield because interest payments are tax-deductible. 
    *   $V_L = V_U + PV(\text{Tax Shield})$
    *   $PV(\text{Tax Shield}) = T_c \times D$ (assuming permanent debt).
    *   Implies firms should be 100% debt-financed.
*   **Personal Taxes:** Can offset the corporate tax advantage if interest income is taxed heavier than equity income at the personal level.

## Financial Distress and Agency Costs of Debt
*   Why aren't firms 100% debt-financed? Because of the costs of financial distress.
*   $V_L = V_U + PV(\text{Tax Shield}) - PV(\text{Financial Distress Costs})$
*   **Direct Costs:** Legal fees, court costs in bankruptcy.
*   **Indirect Costs (Agency Frictions):**
    *   *Risk Shifting (Asset Substitution):* Shareholders of a distressed firm have an incentive to take on high-risk, negative-NPV projects because they get the upside while bondholders bear the downside.
    *   *Debt Overhang (Underinvestment):* Shareholders may refuse to fund safe, positive-NPV projects because the benefits will accrue entirely to bondholders.
    *   *Bait and Switch, Playing for Time, Cash In and Run.*

## Trade-off Theory
*   Firms balance the tax benefits of debt against the costs of financial distress.
*   **Optimal Capital Structure:** The debt level that maximizes firm value.
*   Explains why tech companies (high distress costs, intangible assets) use little debt, while utilities and airlines (tangible assets, lower distress costs) use high debt.

## Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC) and Valuation
*   $WACC = r_E \frac{E}{V} + r_D (1 - T_c) \frac{D}{V}$
*   The WACC implicitly accounts for the tax shield by using the after-tax cost of debt.
*   **WACC Pitfalls:** Can only be used if the project has the same risk as the firm AND will be financed with the same constant target debt ratio.
*   **Adjusting WACC for changed leverage:**
    1.  *Unlever:* Find the unlevered cost of capital ($r_A$).
    2.  *Relever:* Find the new cost of equity ($r_E$) at the new debt ratio.
    3.  *Recalculate WACC* using the new $r_E$ and debt weights.
