# The Efficient Market Hypothesis, the Financial Analysts Journal, and the Professional Status of Investment Management

This source note summarizes Stephen J. Brown's 2020 paper, which reviews the historical evolution of the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), its academic development, and its profound impact on the professional status and methods of investment management.

## Core Concepts & Historical Context
Brown traces how the idea of market efficiency evolved from a controversial academic concept in the 1960s and 1970s into an organizing principle for modern finance.

1.  **Fama's Formalization (1970)**: Eugene Fama defined an "efficient market" as one where prices always "fully reflect" available information. To test this hypothesis empirically, Fama categorized the information set into three levels:
    *   **Weak Form**: The information set includes only historical price and volume data. If weak-form efficiency holds, technical analysis cannot consistently generate abnormal returns.
    *   **Semi-Strong Form**: The information set includes all publicly available information (earnings announcements, corporate financial statements, macroeconomic indicators, etc.). If semi-strong efficiency holds, fundamental analysis cannot consistently beat the market.
    *   **Strong Form**: The information set includes all public and private (insider) information. If strong-form efficiency holds, even insiders cannot earn abnormal profits.

2.  **The Joint Hypothesis Problem**: Brown highlights a fundamental limitation of testing EMH: any empirical test of market efficiency must be conducted in conjunction with an asset pricing model (such as the CAPM). If the test rejects efficiency, it is impossible to determine whether the market is truly inefficient or if the underlying asset pricing model is misspecified.

3.  **Impact on Professional Investment Management**:
    *   **Rise of Quantitative and Index Products**: As academic evidence piled up showing that the vast majority of active mutual fund managers failed to outperform simple benchmarks, the investment industry shifted toward passive products (such as index funds and ETFs).
    *   **Redefining Professionalism**: Rather than focusing solely on "picking winners" (which EMH suggests is a random exercise), the role of the modern financial analyst shifted toward risk management, portfolio construction, asset allocation, and tax-efficient planning.
    *   **Big Data and Fintech Challenges**: In the 21st century, the speed of information processing and the availability of alternative data sources have raised new questions about the micro-structure of market efficiency and the limits of arbitrage.
