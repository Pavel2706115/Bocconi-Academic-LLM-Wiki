# The Rationality of Index Investing and Paradoxes of Indexation

This source note covers the theoretical framework and mathematical modeling presented by David Eagle et al. (2009) regarding the rationality of passive index investing and the microeconomic paradoxes that arise as passive indexation dominates financial markets.

## The Rationality of Passive Indexing
The authors review the core rationale behind passive indexation:
1.  **Low Cost Structure**: Index funds minimize operating expense ratios, transaction costs (due to extremely low portfolio turnover), and management fees.
2.  **Broad Diversification**: Passive indexing captures the entire market portfolio, eliminating idiosyncratic (unsystematic) firm-specific risk.
3.  **Tax Efficiency**: Low turnover leads to fewer capital gains distributions, allowing compounding to occur with maximum tax-deferred efficiency.
4.  **The Arithmetic of Active Management**: Sharpe's classic argument dictates that, in aggregate, before costs, the average active dollar must equal the return of the average passive dollar. After costs, the average active dollar must underperform.

## The Paradoxes of Indexation: Grossman-Stiglitz Paradox
The core of the paper addresses the microeconomic equilibrium of active vs. passive management:

1.  **The Information Acquisition Dilemma**:
    Active investors spend resources (time, money, advanced quantitative systems) to acquire and analyze proprietary information. If markets are perfectly efficient, all this information is immediately reflected in asset prices. If so, active investors cannot earn abnormal returns to offset the cost of information acquisition. Therefore, it is irrational for anyone to acquire information.

2.  **The Breakdown of Price Discovery**:
    If no one acquires information, prices will fail to reflect fundamental economic realities. In a world where $100\%$ of investors are passive indexers:
    *   No fundamental analysis or valuation is performed.
    *   Capital allocation is completely detached from corporate productivity or earnings quality.
    *   Markets become highly inefficient, creating substantial opportunities for active managers to trade against mispricings.

3.  **The Active-Passive Equilibrium**:
    Sanford Grossman and Joseph Stiglitz (1980) proved that a perfectly efficient market is an impossibility. Instead, the market must exist in an equilibrium state where:
    $$\theta^* = f(\text{cost of information}, \text{risk aversion}, \text{noise trader variance})$$
    Where $\theta^*$ represents the equilibrium proportion of active managers. 
    If the proportion of passive investors rises beyond this threshold, price discovery weakens, the return to active information acquisition increases, and active management becomes highly profitable again, pulling investors back into active strategies.
