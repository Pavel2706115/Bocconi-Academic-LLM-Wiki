# Value Investing: Essence and Ways of Finding Undervalued Assets

This source note summarizes the academic paper by Elitsa Petrova (2015), which explores the theoretical foundations, implementation methodologies, and empirical validity of the value investing strategy.

## Key Theoretical Pillars
Petrova identifies the active value strategy as a systematic effort to purchase securities at a price significantly below their intrinsic value. 

1.  **Intrinsic Value ($V$)**: The true economic value of a security, calculated by evaluating its fundamental factors (earnings, assets, dividends, and growth prospects). Unlike market price ($P$), which is subject to emotional swings, intrinsic value is relatively stable.
2.  **Margin of Safety**: The difference between a stock's intrinsic value and its market price:
    $$\text{Margin of Safety} = V - P$$
    A large margin of safety protects the investor from valuation errors, unexpected business disruptions, and market volatility.
3.  **Market Inefficiency**: The value strategy relies on the assumption that stock markets are not perfectly efficient. Behavioral biases (such as overreaction, herd behavior, and short-termism) cause prices to diverge from intrinsic values, creating mispriced buying opportunities.

## Quantitative Valuation Models & Filters
The paper highlights several historical valuation models pioneered by Benjamin Graham and David Dodd:

### 1. Net Current Asset Value (NCAV) / "Net-Net" Model
Graham's most conservative valuation filter focuses on buying companies priced below their liquidation value. A stock is considered a "net-net" buy candidate if:
$$P < \frac{2}{3} \times \text{NCAV}$$
Where Net Current Asset Value (NCAV) is defined as:
$$\text{NCAV} = \text{Current Assets} - \text{Total Liabilities} - \text{Preferred Stock}$$

### 2. Graham's Growth Formula
Graham proposed a simplified formula for valuing growth stocks, later revised in 1974:
$$V = \text{EPS} \times (8.5 + 2g)$$
Where:
*   $V$ = Intrinsic value
*   $\text{EPS}$ = Expected trailing 12-month Earnings Per Share
*   $8.5$ = The appropriate P/E ratio for a company with $0\%$ growth
*   $g$ = Expected long-term annual growth rate (expressed as a percentage)

### 3. Valuation Multipliers
Value investors use low ratios of:
*   Price-to-Earnings ($P/E$)
*   Price-to-Book ($P/B$)
*   Price-to-Sales ($P/S$)
*   Price-to-Cash Flow ($P/CF$)
These low ratios indicate that the market has undervalued the asset relative to its current operational fundamentals.
