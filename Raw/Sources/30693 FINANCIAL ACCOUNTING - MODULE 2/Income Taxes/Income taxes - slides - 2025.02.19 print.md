## **Introduction** 

"Income taxes" are a sub-category of taxes, which are of many different kinds (income tax, VAT, sales tax, registry tax, stamps…). 

**The procedure** to compute corporate income taxes, which is set according to procedures common to all countries in the world, is the object of study in the course 30427. 

**Income taxes are a cost to companies. The cost is debited, and a liability to the tax authority is credited.** 

**==> picture [37 x 143] intentionally omitted <==**

The cost and the debt arise due to the simple generation of a positive income. In other words, as the company has been able to earn money, it can contribute to public expenses by paying taxes. In Italy, the highest-ranking law governing taxes is Article 53 of the Constitution.

## **During the fiscal year: down payments** 

To simplify, we will assume when dealing with taxes that the accounting period coincides with the calendar year. 

During the year, **advanced down payments for taxes are required by law.** The size of down payments and payment dates vary from country to country. 

In Italy, broadly _(i.e. with many possible exceptions),_ these advance payments are due in two installments: 

- June 30: 40% of the gross tax liability of the preceding fiscal year 

- November 30: 60% of gross tax liability of the preceding fiscal year. 

At the date of each down payment, the journal entry is _(example: 30 June 2018)_ : 

Advance Tax Payment (or Prepaid Taxes) (+A) €XX Cash (-A) €XX 

**==> picture [37 x 143] intentionally omitted <==**

The «advance tax payment» is a current asset and, if no income tax payables are due at the end of the year, it may be either carried forward, or claimed back for a refund, or used to offset other tax payables (ex. VAT). 

An «advance payment» is a sort of «prepaid expense», the difference is that the «advance payment» can be claimed back if the conditions for which it was paid are not met (in the case of taxes, if no tax payments are due: see next slides)

## **Why does taxable income differ from GAAP?** 

As you have learned, the goal of financial accounting is to provide information to stakeholders so can make decisions about the company they good company. 

Income taxes are different. Tax authorities do not care about how useful taxable income is. Instead, there are two goals in calculating taxable income: (1) raising revenue for the government and (2) providing incentives for socially beneficial behaviors and disincentives for socially detrimental behaviors. 

As previously discussed, one primary reason why taxable income differs from GAAP income is that tax authorities need to limit manipulation that would affect the stability of governmental revenues. Many expenses we have covered require subjective estimates that, if used in calculating taxable income, would encourage managers to inflate their expenses so they pay less income taxes. 

**==> picture [37 x 143] intentionally omitted <==**

For this reason, taxable income calculations often disallow or limit expenses that require subjective judgments, like bad debt expense and goodwill impairments, and other times will require that an expense be completely recomputed based on strict formulas, as is common with depreciation expense.

## **Permanent or temporary differences** 

In the previous chapter we referred to the "variations" to be made to the Profit Before Taxes to calculate Taxable Income. 

There are two types of “variations”.

Permanent differences: when a cost or revenue is recorded in the general journal but the tax law does not recognize it in the financial year in which it is recorded or in any future year. In other words, for the tax authorities these kind of revenues or costs "do not ever exist". Temporary differences: when a cost or revenue is recorded in the but the tax law does not it in the same general journal recognize financial year in which it is recorded. Rather, tax law will recognize it in the future. These misalignments between the accrual period for accounting purposes and the accrual period for tax purposes generate: • **Deferred tax assets, and** 

- **Deferred tax liabilities**
