- **Corporate sustainability:** 

   - **The integration of environmental, social, and governance (ESG) considerations into a company's strategy, operations, and decisionmaking processes.**

## **ESG Ratings: Strengths & Weaknesses as a Tool** 

**==> picture [33 x 34] intentionally omitted <==**

## **Strengths** 

## **Standardisation & comparability** 

- •Allow investors to screen and compare companies across sectors and geographies on non-financial criteria 

- •Enable integration of ESG factors into portfolio construction, risk models, and index products 

## ✘ **Weaknesses / Limitations** 

## **Rating divergence** 

- •Different providers reach opposite conclusions on the same company 

- •Driven by scope (what to measure), weight (how much), and data (how to collect) differences 

## **Greenwashing & disclosure bias** 

•Ratings partly reward disclosure quality, not actual ESG performance — companies learn to “play the game” 

## **Backward-looking & static** 

- •Based on disclosed past data — do not capture future ESG trajectory or strategic commitment 

- •VW had high ESG ratings in 2014, one year before Dieselgate

## **Corporate voluntary ESG disclosure** 

- Today, most public companies and some small- and medium-sized companies publish ESG disclosures. 

   - ESG disclosures can take place in various corporate communication channels, such as annual reports, corporate websites, stand-alone ESG reports, and so on. 

- **In different markets, in absence of universal formal mandatory rules, ESG disclosures significantly vary** in: 

   - Form (presentation, communication channel, reporting frequency, etc.), and 

   - Content (scope, topic, text vs. number vs. graphs, quality, etc.). 

- Concerns cumulate about the consistency, comparability, truthfulness, and reliability of ESG disclosures.

## **ESG disclosure debate: Should it be mandated/regulated? – Cons (1/4)** 

- Even within a single industry, business activities and company cultures vary (sometimes very significantly). How **broadly** or specifically should regulations be written? 

   - Broad rules make accurate reporting impossible: 

      - Because managers would struggle to fully provide what’s required. 

      - Consequently, managers may not know exactly what data to include or how detailed it should be. Then, ESG disclosures end up incomplete, inconsistent, or difficult to verify. 

   - Broad rules also leave more room for managers to hide bad news. 

      - A key reason to require CSR disclosures would be to allow the public to see how companies and their activities compare with each other, even if this means some companies will have to release unfavorable information. 

      - But if managers think disclosing something is risky or not in a company’s best interest, they will have more freedom to avoid disclosing it.

## **ESG disclosure debate: Should it be mandated/regulated? - Cons (4/4)**

## **Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) – Foundations** 

- **Double materiality:** 

   - Identifying all potential negative and positive impacts on people and environment, from the company’s own operations and its value chain. Materiality is assessed from a financial and impact perspective. 

   - The CSRD incorporates the concept of ‘double materiality’. This means that **companies have to report not only on how sustainability issues might create financial risks for the company (financial materiality), but also on the company’s own impacts on people and the environment (impact materiality).** 

   - Investors are increasingly interested in both sides of this equation. This is partly as a result of the growing market for sustainable investment products. 

   - Other stakeholders, including civil society organisations and trade unions, expect a transparent and objective account of a company’s impact on issues such as biodiversity, climate change or human rights.

## **Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) – Foundations (cont.)** 

- Boundary: 

   - A company’s reporting boundary has expanded to include the entire value chain in measuring and reporting social and environmental impact. This covers all suppliers, both upstream and downstream. 

- Sustainability strategy and business model: 

   - Companies are required to set a strategy to respond to these matters, and disclose the business model’s resilience towards sustainability-related risks and climate scenarios. 

- Coverage Scope: 

   - Large EU companies & Non-EU companies with significant operations within the EU must comply with the CSRD.

## **European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS): Overview** 

- ESRS were developed by the EFRAG (European Financial Reporting Advisory Group), an independent body bringing together various different stakeholders. 

- The European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) are a set of mandatory sustainability reporting standards. 

   - These standards define how companies must disclose ESG information in a structured, comparable, and standardized manner. 

- The ESRS take a “double materiality” perspective: 

   - They oblige companies to **report both on their impacts on people and the environment, and on how social and environmental issues create financial risks and opportunities for the company.**

- **European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS): Detailed Requirements (cont.)** 

- ESRS 1 (“General Requirements”) sets **general principles** to be applied when reporting according to ESRS. 

- ESRS 2 (“General Disclosures”) specifies **essential information** to be disclosed irrespective of which sustainability matter is being considered. 

   - ESRS 2 is mandatory for all companies under the CSRD scope. 

- All the other standards and the individual disclosure requirements and datapoints within them are subject to a **materiality assessment.** 

   - In other words: Companies will report only relevant/significant (“material”) information and may omit the information in question that is not relevant/significant (“not material”) for its business model and activities.
