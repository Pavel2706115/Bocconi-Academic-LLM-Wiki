_Price is particularly important_ because it: 

- has a direct and immediate impact on _company profitability_ ; 

- is the _easiest and fastest_ variable to adjust within the [[Wiki/Topics/Marketing|marketing]] mix; 

- plays a key role in _defining value_ , as it summarizes the monetary sacrifice required to obtain the benefits of a given offer; 

- has strong _competitive potential_ , wheter in offensive, defensive, or even collusive straegies.

## **1. Price setting (2/13)** 

When setting the _price,_ three key aspects must be analyzed: 

1. the _total production costs_ of the goods, considering not only the variable costs of raw materials but also fixed costs (e.g. rent in the cost of ice cream); 

2. the _prices charged by competitors_ , ensuring that prices are either alligned with those of other competitors offering goods of similar quality, or appropriately differentiated based on the quality premium offered; 

3. the _value created for the customer,_ and consequently, the price the customer is willing to pay for the product.

## **1. Price setting (3/13)** 

Usually, the _full cost of a product_ is considered the lower limit of the price range; prices should not fall below this level (except temporarily, for promotional purposes): the cost-based price acts as the _floor_ of in the socalled _price room_ ; 

The _demand-oriented price_ represents the maximum amount a customer is willing to pay for a given product; this serves as the _ceiling_ of the imaginary price room. 

Finally, the _competition-oriented price_ serves as the intermediate reference point; it should be used as a benchmark when deciding whether to set prices above or below competitors; depending on the company's strategic [[Wiki/Concepts/Marketing-Segmentation-and-Positioning|positioning]].

## **1. Price setting (4/13)** 

- When setting the price, the first step typically involves assessing _the costs_ required to produce the product or deliver the service: the price must exceed these costs. 

- In calculating costs, both _variable_ and _fixed_ costs must be taken into account. 

- The pricing methods adopted usually involve adding a _margin_ to _the product’s base cost_ ; this margin should be sufficient to cover any additional costs not included in the base cost and to ensure a certain level of profit ( _surplus_ ). 

- Cost-oriented pricing methods are generally referred to as _cost-plus pricing_ , which involves applying a _mark-up_ or _margin_ on the base cost.

## **1. Price setting (7/13)** 

It is also possible to determine the so-called _break-even price_ – that is the price which, given a certain sales volume, allows the business to break even. Starting from the previous slide, we have: Q x p – Q x vc – FC = 0     (9) Q x p = Q x vc + FC         (10) p* = Q x vc/Q + FC/Q     (11) p*= vc + FC/Q (12) 

The _break-even price_ (p*) represents the unit variable unit plus the portion of fixed costs allocated to each unit – that is, the fixed costs divided by the target quantity (Q).

## **1. Price setting (8/13)** 

It is also possible to set a _price that covers total costs and generates a specific target profit_ . In this case, formula (5) will not be set equal to zero but instead will be represented by a value corresponding to _the target profit_ (P). Therefore, we will have: 

Q x p – Q x vc – FC = P     (13) 

Q x p = Q x vc + (FC + P)     (14) p* = Q x vc/Q + (FC + P)/Q     (15) p*= vc + (FC + P)/Q (16) 

In this case, the _price that allows fixed costs to be covered and achieves a target profit_ is equal to the variable unit costs plus the sum of the fixed costs and the target profit, divided by the expected quantities to be sold.

## **1. Price setting (9/13)** 

_Competition-oriented pricing_ is commonly used by companies operating in highly competitive market conditions. The most frequently adopted _methods_ include: 

- _the parity with competition method_ : the pricing policy is aligned with that of the main competitors; 

- _the discount price method_ : this involves setting a price lower than that of the competition, encouraging customers to choose the most affordable option; 

- _the premium price method_ : this involves setting a higher price than that of the main competitors, inviting customers to perceive a higher value in the product that justifies the price premium.

## **1. Price setting (10/13)** 

_Demand orientation_ is the predominant approach in [[Wiki/Topics/Marketing|marketing]]-oriented companies, as **it focuses on the value that customers attribute to the offer** . **based on the utility they derive from it** 

This value represents _**the maximum price**_ that can be charged in the market: if price exceeds this level, the product would be perceived as more expensive than the value it provides, giving customers no incentives to purchase it. 

One method used to determine demand-oriented pricing is to calculate **the** _**competitive differential**_ **between two products (e.g. by applying** _**Fishbein’s**_ **model for offer evaluation** ) and set a price proportional to that differential. **If the total perceived value is equal to that of the competitor** (i.e., a competitive ratio equal to 1), **prices should be aligned with those of the competition.**

## **1. Price setting (12/13)** 

- The company should regularly _verify the consistency_ of its pricing policy by referring to the following three guidelines, ensuring that: 

- the price (except during promotional periods) is _higher than the cost of production_ and includes an adequate mark-up to cover overheads and generate a satisfactory profit; 

- the price is _alligned with competitors’ offerings_ , and _parity_ , _discount,_ or _premium pricing_ policies are justified by the perceived differential value of the product compared with the competing alternatives; 

- the price is _consistent with the value perceived by customers,_ and with _the utility_ they derive from the product and/or service.

## **1. Price setting (13/13)** 

In many cases, it is not the manufacturing company that sets the product _the commercial distributor_ . price, but rather 

**The distributor sets the price of individual products in a way that supports the overall [[Wiki/Concepts/Pricing-Policies-and-Bundling|pricing strategy]] at the point of sale** , as its main goal is to promote the entire [[Wiki/Concepts/Product-Range-and-Branding|product range]]. To this end, it determines the prices of **individual brands** in such a way as to create an _**overall perception of value and affordability**_ at the retail location. 

_Branded products_ possess greater _signalling power_ than lesser-known brands, particularly in shaping consumer perceptions of affordability. This explains why certain branded products – referred to as _loss leaders_ – are used in ‘price wars’, often being sold below cost. 

As a result, distributors often introduce _private label_ or _commercial brands_ into their assortments, which are not directly comparable with the competition.

## **2. Price policy management (3/6)** 

## **2. Price differentiation and discrimination** 

_Price differentiation_ refers to offering customers different prices for different products, in line with the costs incurred to produce each product. _Price discrimination_ – on the other hand - occurs when two differentiated products are sold at the same price, or at different prices not justified by cost differences. 

Price discrimination is commonly used in service sector, particulary to manage: 

- Temporal fluctuations (e.g. _temporal_ discrimination such as peak vs offpeak pricing) 

- Geographical differences (e.g. _geographical discrimination_ , based on location-specific factors).

## **2. Price policy management (5/6)** 

## **4. Skimming or penetration** 

A _penetration pricing stragety_ aims to maximize sales volume through _aggressive pricing_ , typically setting prices below those of competitors in order to rapidly gain market shares. 

In constrast, _skimming pricing strategy_ focuses on maximizing profitability, emphasizing the margins generated by commercial activity. It involves setting prices above the market average, [[Wiki/Concepts/Marketing-Segmentation-and-Positioning|targeting]] customer segments that are particularly responsive to the product’s unique features and willing to pay a premium price.

## **5. Multi-channel pricing** 

A distinctive [[Wiki/Concepts/Pricing-Policies-and-Bundling|pricing strategy]] is required for companies that operate through _multiple distribution channels_ , including online platforms. 

Multi-channel firms typically aim to _differentiate their offerings_ across various channels and points of sales, implementing channel-specific pricing strategies. This may also involve _bundling_ (e.g., including value-added services such as delivery). 

The _differentiation of offerings and pricing_ plays a key role in preventing conflicts within the distribution system – namely: 

- _Horizontal conflicts_ , such as price competition between different retailers 

- _Vertical conflicts_ , between manifacturers and distributors operating within the same supply chain.
