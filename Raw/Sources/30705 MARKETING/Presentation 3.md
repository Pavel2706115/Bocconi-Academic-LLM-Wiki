### Slide 15: Quantification of Gaps

**Slide elements (verbatim/structured):**

- **Title:** Quantification of gaps: Non-consumer gap, occasion gap, dose gap
- **Text:**
    - **POTENTIAL GAP = ([[Wiki/Concepts/Demand-Estimation-and-Market-Share|market potential]] – current demand)**
    - Formula breakdown:
      - **Non-consumer GAP** (User GAP) = (potential users – current users) $\times$ Potential Occasions $\times$ Optimal Dose
      - **Occasion GAP** = Actual Users $\times$ (ideal occasions – actual occasions) $\times$ Optimal Dose
      - **Dose GAP** = Actual Users $\times$ Actual Occasions $\times$ (full quantity – actual quantity)
      - **POTENTIAL GAP** = sum of the 3 GAPs
- **Visuals (charts/tables/images):**
    - One picture [894 x 306] intentionally omitted. This picture was a flow diagram showing the decomposition of the total potential gap into its three components (Non-consumer gap, Occasion gap, and Dose gap).
- **Labels/footnotes:** None.

**Explanation of content:**

- **Text explanation:** This slide introduces a crucial analytical framework: the decomposition of the potential gap into three distinct drivers. Instead of treating the gap as a single number, marketers split it into:
  1. **Non-consumer (User) Gap**: Volume lost because some eligible individuals are non-users.
  2. **Occasion Gap**: Volume lost because actual users consume the product less frequently than the ideal rate.
  3. **Dose Gap**: Volume lost because actual users use less than the optimal amount per occasion.
  Decomposing the gap allows companies to target their marketing efforts at the specific source of volume loss.
- **Graph/table explanation:** The omitted flow diagram visualizes how the three sub-gaps sum up to equal the total potential gap.
- **Key takeaways from visuals:** Total gap closure requires distinct actions depending on whether volume is lost to non-consumers, low usage frequency, or under-dosing.

**What this slide is really saying (1–3 sentences):**

The total potential gap is the sum of three distinct components: non-users, users not consuming frequently enough, and users consuming too small a dose.

### Slide 20: Soft Drinks Gap Decomposition

**Slide elements (verbatim/structured):**

- **Title:** Estimation of potential market demand. Nondurable goods/services: example SOFT DRINKS (Italy – year t) - Gap calculations
- **Text:**

        - $52\% - 15.5\% = 36.5\%$ of the population do not consume soft drinks despite having no objective impediments to doing so (Total population: 60M. Excluded demographics: 15.5%. Total potential population: 84.5%. Actual consumers: 48%. Non-consumer gap: $84.5\% - 48\% = 36.5\%$).
        - Quantity of soft drinks lost to non-consumers:
          $$60,000,000 \times 36.5\% \times (3 \times 365) \times 0.33\text{ l} = 7,913,565,000\text{ liters}$$
    - **Occasion gap**:
        - $48\%$ of Italians consume soft drinks but do so on average only once a day. This means that two consumption occasions are lost per day:
          $$60,000,000 \times 48\% \times (2 \times 365) \times 0.33\text{ l} = 6,937,920,000\text{ liters}$$

### Slide 59: Market Share Antecedents: The Fundamental Market Share Theorem
