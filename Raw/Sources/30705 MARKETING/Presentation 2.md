**2. Environmental layers and significant trends (2/5) Territory and demographics** Italy is one of the _**most densely populated**_ countries in the EU, with 45% of the population living in highly urbanised areas and 39% in moderately urbanized areas. 

This second figure exceeds the European average by about 14 percentage points: the _**average city**_ is a typically Italian phenomenon. This influences _distribution policies_ and _sales networks_ , which must take into account the key role of provincial areas. 

In _**terms of demographics**_ , Italy is also one of the _**oldest**_ countries in Europe. Average life expectancy is 84 years for women and 80 years for men, with significant growth over the last 50 years. This opens up opportunities for senior targets.

## Impact and probability of events 

• It is advisable to intercept and analyse all environmental signals and first assess the likelihood of the phenomena detected occuring in the future, focusing only on those events that are most likely to take place and which, as such, require greater attention. For example, a new regulation, the speed with which Covid-19 vaccine booster doses are administered, or a new technology that could replace a previous one should be further classified according to the impact they are potentially capable of having on the company, focusing the analysis on those that are most likely and capable of generating a significant effect.

## **3. The competitive [[Wiki/Concepts/Marketing-Environment-and-Competition|micro-environment]] (2/3)** 

To measure the **level of industry concentration** , reference is usually made to the sum of the market shares of the top _n companies_ ( **Cn** ), generally taking into account the [[Wiki/Concepts/Demand-Estimation-and-Market-Share|market share]] held by the top four companies in the sector ( _C4 index_ ). 

Another useful element in understanding the structure of the industry is the presence of **barriers to entry** , which can be of various types, such as: 

- those relating to authorisations and regulatory constraints for operating in certain industries ( _institutional barriers_ ); 

- those relating to the capital required to start a business in a given industry ( _financial barriers_ ); 

- barriers linked to the difficulty of accessing the distribution system ( _distributive barriers_ ).

## **3. The competitive [[Wiki/Concepts/Marketing-Environment-and-Competition|micro-environment]] (3/3)** 

A final characteristic of the industry structure is the opportunity to **differentiate the offering** from that of competitors. 

The possibility of making a product unique in the customer’s view, differentiating it from that of competitors, allows for the development of a good level of **competitive isolation** from other companies in the sector, increasing brand recognition and the level of customer trust and loyalty.

## **3. The competitive [[Wiki/Concepts/Marketing-Environment-and-Competition|micro-environment]] (3/3)** 

- When there is little differentiation, the products offered by the various companies on the market are perceived as very similar to each other and therefore easily imitable by external parties ( _commodity_ products). 

- For commodity products, the comparison is based on price, given their high degree of substitutability. 

- With high differentiation, on the other hand, a _premium price_ approach is justified.

## **4. The expansion of industry boundaries (1/2)** 

The SCP paradigm overlooks the impact that _buyer characteristics_ and the _structure of the commercial sector_ have on the performance of industrial sectors. 

- In this regard, Porter has shown that the influence of the distribution system structure on industry performance depends mainly on two factors: 

- the _degree of concentration of the retail sector_ : the higher this is, the more the performance of the industry will be affected. 

- the ability of commercial enterprises themselves to influence

## **5. Customer perception: customercentric analysis** 

To understand how customers perceive the various alternatives available on the market, we generally use the attribute-based model ( _à la Fishbein_ ). This involves the following steps: 

- breaking down the company's offering into a set of **attributes** ; 

- understanding, through appropriate market research, how **important** these attributes are to customers; 

- • assessing how various competitors on the market are **perceived** in relation to these attributes.
