Consumers do not approach all purchases the same way. The level of mental effort, time, and care they invest scales directly with how psychologically involved they are with the purchase.

### Slide 11: Variables influencing psychological involvement

**Slide elements (verbatim/structured):**

- **Title:** Variables influencing [[Wiki/Concepts/Consumer-Behavior-Models|psychological involvement]]
- **Text:**
    - _1. Interest in the category_ : depends on _subjective elements_ (functional and socio-psychological values and emotional value).
    - _2. Perceived risk_ :
        - **functional risk** → inadequate performance;
        - **physical risk** → threats to consumer safety or health;
        - **economic-financial risk** → negative consequences in terms of assets and/or income;
        - **psychosocial risk** → possible negative impact of the product on the individual's self-esteem and/or social standing.
    - 3. The _social visibility_ of purchasing and consumption increases the socio-processes: perceived psychological risk.
    - 4. The _context of use._
- **Visuals (charts/tables/images):**

**Explanation of content:**

- **Text explanation:** This slide identifies the four fundamental variables that dictate a consumer's level of psychological involvement. First is their personal interest in the product category. Second is the perceived risk, which is broken down into four distinct dimensions: functional (will it work?), physical (is it safe?), economic (is it worth the money?), and psychosocial (how will others view me?). Third is the social visibility of the product (publicly consumed goods trigger higher psychosocial concern), and fourth is the specific context of use (e.g., buying wine for a casual night vs. an important business dinner).
- **Graph/table explanation:** Not applicable.
- **Key takeaways from visuals:** Perceived risk is the most powerful driver of consumer involvement. Marketers can lower barriers to purchase by offering warranties, free trials, or social proof to mitigate these perceived risks.
