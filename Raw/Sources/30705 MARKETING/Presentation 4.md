## **Classification of purchasing motivations (at the point of sale)*** 

**1. need for identification** : to be recognized as an individual, to assume a certain _status_ or social role, and to be identified with a particular lifestyle; 

**2. need for affiliation** : combating loneliness, seeking social contacts, being part of the clientele of an exclusive point of sale, sharing one's interests with others, or belonging to a community; 

**3. need for affirmation** : bargaining, acquiring a position of superiority over sales staff, and being able to optimize one's choice; 

**4. need for new stimuli** : escaping the monotony and routine of everyday life, keeping up with fashion trends, finding new sensory stimuli in stores, and engaging in physical activity. 

*examples of valid motivations particularly for shopping in the narrow sense (distributive perspective/shopper [[Wiki/Topics/Marketing|marketing]])

## **Classification of buyers based on shopping orientation** 

**1. recreational shoppers** : those who view the [[Wiki/Concepts/Consumer-Behavior-Models|purchasing process]] and the related gathering of information as an engaging experience through which they can satisfy a series of psychological and social needs; 

**2. functional shoppers** : those who view shopping from an exclusively functional perspective.

## Types of use: 

- **preparation activities** ( _sensing_ ): the consumer processes the relevant information in order to use the product or service (e.g. information booklet on how to use the product); 

- **interaction activities** ( _sharing_ ): the consumer interacts with a consumption context, participating in the construction of the context itself (e.g. a cooking course for a food product); 

- **performing activities** _(performing):_ the consumer puts into practice the activities that allow them to extract value from consumption (e.g. from the production of something that includes the good, to its customization and appreciation)
