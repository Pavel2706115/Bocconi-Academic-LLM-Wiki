## Return on total assets (ROTA)

EBIT Total assets

## EBIT

Is the amount remaining when total operating costs are deducted from total revenues

Total operating cost includes direct factory cost, administration, overheads, and depreciation and amortization

Total Assets Resources controlled by the firm as a result of past events and from which future economic benefits are expected to flow to the firm Current Assets + Fixed Assets

## Interpreting ROTA

- Rate of return earned by the total assets

- This ratio measures how well management uses all the assets to generate an opening surplus

- Key tool in directing management’s day-to-day activities.

- It provides a benchmark against which all operations can be measured

## Problems with ROTA

- Firms deal with several products, not just one:

hard to disentangle specific contributions from average components

- It differs widely across industry

- Depends heavily on asset valuations in the Balance Sheet:

- age of plants, depreciation etc. play an important role

## Decomposing ROTA

However, as a single figure it simply provides a target. To be useful in decisionmaking, it must first be broken down into its component parts

EBIT EBIT Total sales = X Total assets Total sales Total assets ROTA ROS Asset turnover Return on total assets Return on sales

## ROTA: interpretation

- ROTA tells you how much money is generated from each dollar invested into the firm

- It shows you the relationship between its resources (assets) and its income, providing a comparison term to evaluate if an organization is using its assets more or less effectively than in the past

- Comparisons should be made with companies **in the same industry** . When ROTA is .

- higher than industry average, a company is said to have a **competitive advantage**

## Return on sales (ROS)

EBIT Total sales

- Also named Net profit sales percent

- Identifies profit as a percentage of sales (how much profit is being produced per dollar of sales)

- Compare a company's ROS over time to look for trends

- An increasing ROS indicates the company is growing more efficient, while a decreasing ROS could signal troubles

- Compare it to other companies in the industry

## Asset turnover

## Total sales Total assets

- Not a percentage relationship → How many times sales exceed total assets

- The ability of a company to generate revenue from its assets

- It is less often emphasized in the assessment of company performance

- Its contribution to ROTA is just as powerful and important as the ROS

- Companies with low-profit margins may have high asset turnover

- Grocery stores have high/low asset turnover? Why?

## Asset turnover: interpretation

- Useless to compare Walmart and AT&T because they are in different sectors.

- In contrast, comparing AT&T and Verizon is useful and tells you that Verizon was more efficient at deploying its assets in generating revenues in 2016.

- However, remember to review for trends: AT&T’s asset turn drastically improved since 2006

## Return on equity (ROE)

Net income Owners’ funds

- Measures the absolute return delivered to shareholders

- A good ROE also makes it easy to attract new funds and enables the company to grow

## ROE: interpretation

- It should be high enough to compensate shareholders for the specific risk of investing in the company (higher than risk-free financial instruments).

- Riskier companies should earn higher ROE to convince shareholders to invest.

**LIQUIDITY RATIOS**

## Liquidity

- Short/Long-term solvency, which is the capacity for the company to pay all current liabilities

- Short-term liabilities are an important way of funding firm’s operations (e.g. payables)

- Profitability and liquidity are not the same concepts (It is possible to be profitable, having liquidity problems):

- Not all the payments are made at the same time (e.g. Delayed Payments)

- Not all inflows and outflows of cash are costs and revenues (e.g. Debt Reimbursement)

- Some costs are spread over many years (e.g. Depreciation)

## Cash flow cycle

- Cash Flow Cycle describes how the cash flows in and out of business

- Cash is a continuous circulation through the arteries of the business carrying value to the organs

- If this flow is stopped, or evenly seriously reduced for a time, then serious consequences result

Cash flow cycle

## Current ratio

Current assets Current liabilities

- A ratio greater than 1 tells us that the firm has enough near-cash assets to cover payments due in the immediate future

- However, as all indexes, the threshold value differs drastically depending on industry etc.

- https://www.readyratios.com/sec/ratio/current-ratio/

- It doesn’t distinguish between different types of current assets (some are more liquid than others)

## Quick ratio

Current assets – Inventories Current liabilities

- We exclude inventories because its may be difficult sometimes to turn them quickly into cash

- The numerator includes only current assets that can become cash soon (e.g. cash holdings, marketable securities)

- A value of 1 is very high, Around 0.8 is a good figure

- Widely used by lending institutions to assess companies’ liquidity levels

## Working capital to sales ratio

Current assets – Current liabilities X 100 Sales

- Includes items from both B/S and income statement

- It measures the relationship between the funds used to finance a company's operations and the revenues a company generates → It says how efficiently a company is using its working capital to support sales and growth

- Values between 10% and 20% are considered solid

- Not necessarily co-move with current and quick ratio

- Remember that the other formula to compute working capital is OF+LTD-FA

## Working capital days

Funding requirement (“Cash conversion cycle”) = (Inventory days + Accounts receivable days) – Accounts payable days. The shorter the better!

## Cash conversion cycle

- Short time (effective management of inventory and credit sales)

- The company needs to finance account receivables and inventory for a short period of time

- Long time

- Longer to sell company’s products

- Longer to receive payments from customers

- Too quick payment of bills

**FINANCIAL STRENGTH RATIOS**

## Financial strength

- The company’s ability to meet interest and principal payments in the long-run

- Financial strength ratios provide measures of this ability

- They are associated to the structure of capital:

- risk capital (equity)

- loan capital (bank and corporate debt)

## Interest coverage ratio

EBIT Interests

- It relates financial charges and profitability

- how many times a firm’s operating income exceeds interest expenses

- Three factors determine its value:

- the operating profit,

- the total amount borrowed

- the effective rate of interest

- The lower the ratio, the more the company is burdened by debt expense

## Interest coverage ratio

EBIT Interests

- Can the ratio be negative?

- What does it mean?

- <1 means bad financial situation

- Why?

- <1.5, the ability to meet interest expenses is questionable

- Worst situation: EBIT declines and interest increases over time

## Structure of capital

- Composition of debt and equity

- Where can we find the relevant items?

- What are the financial charges generated by debt?

- Interest

- Payment of the principal

- There is no uncertainty about these charges

Certain cash-out flows vs uncertain cash-in flows in a specific period

## Structure of capital

- High corporate risk means higher likelihood of going out of business

- If the firm is unable to meet debt payment: default

- A court may impose supervision on how to handle a default: bankruptcy (i.e. legal procedure to pay creditors)

- But why then do companies use debt?

- Couldn’t firms just use equity (which is risky capital, i.e. if the firm doesn't make profit, shareholders don't get anything and cannot complain

- Debt in usually cheaper than equity

- Less risky for investors, expected return must be lower than equity, and average realized return must be lower too (i.e. interest rate paid to the lenders must be less than the rate of return earned by the shareholders)

- Tax deductible

- Good for funding growth

## Debt to equity ratio (or LEVERAGE)

Total liabilities – Owners’ funds Owners’ funds

- The entire debt is considered here, i.e. total liabilities minus owners’ funds

- Values between 1 and 2 are typically good

- <1 indicate that the company is over-capitalized

- >2 may indicate excessive risk in the capital structure (high values common in capital intensive industries)

## Effect of financial leverage

## ROE = Net income/owners’ funds

- It tells us the value generated for shareholders

- We decompose ROE as:

## Effect of financial leverage

- An increase in debt/equity has two effects:

- It increases ROE

- It increases corporate risk

- It pays off to increase ROE by increasing debt/equity in good times

- But its very risky and not convenient to do so in bad times

## Example

Current capital structure Total Assets = $1 million;
- Equity = $1 million (10,000 shares);
- Debt = $0 New capital structure Total Assets = $1 million;
- Equity = $700,000 (7,000 shares);
- Debt = $300,000

EBIT next year will be either $50,000, $125,000, or $200,000 depending on macroeconomic conditions. The interest rate on debt is 10%. Ignore taxes — How would this new capital structure affect the firm’s ROE?

## Takeaway

- Use financial leverage with caution

- A high use of financial leverage to deliver high ROE may also entail greater corporate risk

- Particularly risky in times of high uncertainty

- Combine information from ROE with other indexes

- In particular, ROTA, which assesses the ability to generate profits from the entire asset base – not just equity)

- Also, use solvency indicators to better understand the capital structure of the company (too much debt?)
