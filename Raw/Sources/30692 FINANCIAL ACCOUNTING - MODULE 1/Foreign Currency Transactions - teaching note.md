## Foreign currency transactions

Many modern companies spread their activities outside the country of incorporation. Indeed, not all transactions take place among firms that use the same currency for reporting purposes and, when it is the case, the exchange rate between the two transacting parties has to be taken into account.

In particular, the “foreign currency” problem arises because a firm needs to record all the transactions using the same currency, according to the “homogeneity of account money” principle. Though the doctrine considered the possibility to record the transactions keeping multiple currencies, financial statements must use only one currency, which is typically the currency of the country where the firm is located. The problem of foreign currency translation arises whenever the relative strength of two economies of two countries with different currencies changes, thereby there is a fluctuation in the exchange rate of their currencies.

Given such problem and the need to keep the “homogeneity of account money” principle, it is necessary to convert (i.e., apply an exchange rate to) a foreign currency amount so that it is possible to express the monetary value of a transaction in the firm’s home country currency. The exchange rate is the price at which the foreign currency can be acquired. The rules of foreign currency transactions are very similar across different Accounting Standards. Thus, for example, in countries that have adopted IFRS, firms have to follow _IAS 21 The Effects of Changes in Foreign Exchange Rate_ .

If the contract is settled in a foreign currency, the accounting for exchange rate depends on the payment settlement date. Generally, there are three cases to distinguish:

## Case 1: Transaction is completed on the reporting date

When the invoice is paid at the transaction recognition date, the company should use the spot exchange rate of the transaction date.

_Example_: On 30 June 2021, the French Company A sells equipment to an American company and receives 1000$ in cash on the same day; the spot exchange rate on that day is 1$ = 0.84€.

Company A accounts for this transaction as follows:

0.84 € × 1000 $ ÷ 1 $ = 840 €

## Case 2: Transaction is settled by the end of the reporting period

In cases in which the transaction recognition date differs from the payment (or settlement) date (but the payment is settled prior to the end of the accounting period), the differences in exchange rates are accounted for as Realized Gains and Losses. Initially, the company records for the transaction using the spot exchange rate between the functional currency and the foreign currency at the date of the transaction; then, when the invoice is settled, the company accounts for it using the spot exchange rate at the settlement date. The difference which may arise is recorded as foreign exchange gain or loss. Let’s clarify with an example.

_Example_: On 14 January 2021, German company “X” purchases a machinery from American company “Y” for 500,000 $.  Company X’s fiscal year coincides with calendar year. The invoice is settled on 21 September 2021.

The spot exchange rates are as follows:

- 14/01/2021: 1€= 1.22$ (or 1$ = 0.82€);

- 21/09/2021: 1€ = 1.17$ (or 1$ = 0.85€).

The amount at 21/09/21 is computed as: 500,000/1.17. This gives rise to a foreign exchange loss of 500,000/1.22 - 500,000/1.17.

NOTE: the balance of the “Machinery” account (the “non monetary” item in the transaction) stays unchanged i.e. the change in the exchange rate has no impact on the balance of the purchased asset’s value in the books.

## Case 3: Transaction is settled after the end of the reporting period

In cases in which the transaction recognition date differs from the payment date and the payment is not settled by the end of the accounting period, the differences in exchange rates are accounted for as Unrealized Gains and Losses at the fiscal year end. Initially the company records for the transaction using the spot exchange rate between the functional currency and the foreign currency at the date of the transaction; then, at the fiscal year end, the company uses the spot exchange rate at the last day of its reporting period to record the unrealized foreign exchange gains or losses.
