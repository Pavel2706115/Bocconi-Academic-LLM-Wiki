## Operating Revenues

- Revenues are the **amounts earned** and recorded from a company’s day-to-day business activities.

- Revenue is recorded when a company sells products or provides services to customers or clients. These revenues are from the central focus of the business. _e.g., when Chipotle sells salad bowls, it has_ _**earned** revenue._

- Many companies earn revenue from multiple sources such as from providing financing to customers and franchise fees.

- If a customer pays in advance, for example a gift card, the company creates a liability account: Unearned (or Deferred) Revenue.

- This is the receipt of cash in exchange for a promise to provide a good or service in the future.

- Later, when the customer pays with the gift card, the revenue is recognized and the liability is eliminated.

## Operating Expenses

- Operating expenses are the **costs** of operating the business that are **incurred to generate revenues** during the period.

- _Incurred = the resources or services of others that are_ _**used**_

- Many expenses are incurred in making a sale or providing a service.  Without these expenses, the company cannot generate revenues.

- Expenses may be incurred before, after, or at the same time as they are paid in cash.

- Don’t confuse the terms expenditure and expense! An **expenditure** is an **outlay of cash for any purpose** , whether to buy equipment, pay off a bank loan, and pay for equipment repair. **Expenses** are cost incurred to generate revenue.

- Therefore, **not all cash expenditures are expenses!**

## Other Income Statement Items

Not all activities affecting the income statement are “central to ongoing operations”.

Revenues, expenses, gains, or losses that result from **other activities** that are NOT central to ongoing operations are categorized as **Other Items** .

These items are not included in the subtotal “Income from Operations”.

Other items typically include:

- ❖ Interest Revenue (also Investment Revenue, Investment Income, or Dividend Revenue)

- ❖ Interest Expense

- ❖ Losses (Gains) on Sale of Investments

## Income Tax Expense and Earnings per Share

Operating expenses are subtracted from operating revenues to determine Income before Income Taxes (also called Pretax Income).

From this subtotal, **Income Tax Expense** (also called **Provision for Income Taxes** ) is calculated by applying the tax rates of the federal, state, local, and foreign taxing authorities.

Corporations are required to disclose **earnings per share** (EPS) on the income statement or in the notes to the financial statements.

## Typical Income Statement Format

Operating Revenues Less Operating Expenses Income from Operations Add/Less: Other Items Income before Income Taxes _(or Pretax Income)_ Income Tax Expense _(or Provision for Income Taxes)_ Net Income

How Are Operating Activities Recognized and Measured? (1 of 2) Cash Basis Accounting

- Cash basis accounting is used by sole proprietors and small partnerships. It is simple and permitted for tax purposes. - Cash basis accounting records each cash payment as a cash outflow and each cash receipt as a cash inflow.

- This produces **net cash flow** information that is often quite adequate for organizations that do not need to report to external users.

**CAUTION**: Cash basis accounting may lead to an incorrect interpretation of future company performance. The financial statements are not very useful!

Revenues and expenses are often postponed or accelerated long before or after goods and service are delivered.

Other than cash, assets and liabilities of the company are not reported in financial statements.

**GAAP does not allow the cash basis of accounting.**

How Are Operating Activities Recognized and Measured? (2 of 2) Accrual Basis Accounting

In accrual basis accounting, **revenues are recognized when goods and services are provided to customers** (they are earned), **and expenses are recognized in the same period as the revenues to which they relate** (resources are used or debts are incurred to . generate revenues), **regardless of when cash is received or paid** The two basic accounting principles that determine when revenues and expenses are recorded under **accrual basis** accounting are: 1) the **revenue recognition principle** and 2) the **expense recognition principle** (also called the **matching principle** ).

## Revenue Recognition Principle

The revenue recognition principle specifies the timing and amount of revenue to be recognized during an accounting period.

**Revenue is recognized (1) when the company transfers promised goods or** . **services to customers (2) in the amount it expects to be entitled to receive The five steps to follow for recognizing revenue for complex contracts are:**

1. Identify the contract between the company and the customer.

2. Identify the seller’s performance obligations (promised goods and services).

3. Determine the transaction price.

4. Allocate the transaction price to the performance obligations.

5. Recognize revenue when each performance obligation is satisfied (or over time if a service is provided over time).

**The critical point for revenue recognition under the five-step model is when goods or services are delivered** , not when cash is received from customers.

## Expense Recognition Principle

The expense recognition principle (also called the **matching principle** ) requires that costs incurred to generate revenues be recognized in the same period—a matching of costs with benefits.

The costs of generating revenue include expenses incurred such as:



- Salaries and wages to employees who worked **during the period**

- Utilities for the electricity used **during the period.**

- Supplies used **during the period.**

- Facilities rented **during the period.**

- Equipment used **during the period.**

As with revenues and cash receipts, expenses are recorded as incurred, **regardless of when cash is paid** . An entry will be made on the date the expense is incurred and another on the date the cash is paid, if they occur at different times.

## Exhibit 3.3 Recording Expenses versus Cash Payments

Cash may be paid at any of these times:

_1. Before_ the expense is incurred to generate revenue.

_2. In the same period_ as the expense is incurred to generate revenue.

_3. After_ the cost is incurred to generate revenue.

Expense is always recorded **when the cost is incurred** , not when the cash is paid.

## Accounting for Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) as a Provider

The fastest-growing software sales model is software-as-a-service (SaaS). SaaS companies create, develop, host, and update software products through the Internet for customers who pay a fixed monthly or annual subscription fee for the service.  Top companies include Zoom, Salesforce, Google, Shopify, and Microsoft.

**How are the fees paid by customers accounted for as revenue?**

- If the customer pays with a recurring billing, usually monthly, revenue is recognized on the billing date and Accounts Receivable is recorded.

- If the customer is billed for the entire contract up front, then the liability Unearned (or Deferred) Revenue is recorded when the customer pays ahead of receiving services.  As time elapses, revenue is recorded monthly with a reduction of the liability.

- Training or set up fees are recorded as revenue when the performance obligation is completed.

## A Question of Ethics

**Managers are motivated to produce earnings results that meet or exceed investors’ expectations to bolster stock prices** . Greed may lead some managers to make unethical accounting and reporting decisions, often involving falsifying revenues and expenses. **Fraud** is a **criminal offense** for which managers may be sentenced to jail.

- In 2002, **WorldCom** , the second largest long-distance telephone company in the United States at the time, **violated the expense recognition principle** by recording $11 billion in expenses as assets, thus overstating assets and overstating net income. Bernie Ebbers, age 65 and former CEO of WorldCom, was convicted sentenced to 25 years in prison.

- **ZZZZ Best** was a carpet cleaning company started by teenager Barry Minkow who took the company public in 1986. Minkow created fictitious sales to boost earnings and the stock price. At the age of 23, Minkow was convicted and sentenced to 25 years in prison. The audit firm paid $35 million for failing to uncover the fraud. Investors and lenders reportedly lost $100 million.

Many others are affected by accounting fraud. Shareholders lose stock value, employees may lose their jobs and pension funds, and customers and suppliers may be wary of dealing with a company operating under the cloud of fraud.

## How do revenues and expenses affect the transaction analysis model?

When a revenue or expense is recorded, either an asset or a liability will be affected as well.

**Revenues increase net income.  Revenues therefore increase stockholders’ equity** through the account Retained Earnings. They have **credit** balances. Recording revenue requires either increasing an asset or decreasing a liability.

**Expenses decrease net income.  Expenses therefore decrease stockholders’ equity** through the account Retained Earnings. They have **debit** balances. Recording an expense requires either decreasing an asset or increasing a liability. ^abv39s

## Net Income v Net Loss

When **revenues exceed expenses** , the company reports **net income** , increasing Retained Earnings and stockholders’ equity.

However, when **expenses exceed revenues** , a **net loss** results that decreases Retained Earnings and thus stockholders’ equity.

## Steps for Analyzing Any Transaction

- Step 1: **Ask →** Was a revenue earned by delivering goods or services?

- If so, **debit the account for what was** received (+A, -L) and **credit the revenue account** (+R, +SE)

**OR** Was an expense incurred to generate a revenue in the current period? - If so, **debit the expense account** (+E, -SE) and **credit the account for what was** given (-A, +L)

**OR** If neither a revenue was earned or an expense was incurred, what was received and given?

**debit the account for what was** received (+A, -L) **credit the account for what was** given (-A, +L)

Step 2: **Verify →** Is the accounting equation in balance? (Does A = L + SE?) Do debits = credits?

## Effect of Operating Activities on Cash Flows (1 of 2)

Only transactions affecting cash are reported on the Statement of Cash Flows. Cash flows from operating activities are primarily cash received from customers and cash paid to suppliers and other involved in operations.

As discussed in Chapter 2, companies report cash inflows and outflows over a period of time in their **statement of cash flows** . This statement is divided into three categories:

- **O** — Operating activities include those primarily with customers and

- suppliers, and interest payments on debt and earnings on investments.

- **I** — Investing activities include buying and selling noncurrent assets and

- investments.

- **F** — Financing activities include borrowing and repaying debt, including

- short-term bank loans; issuing and repurchasing stock; and paying dividends.

**Study tip:  If you see Cash in a transaction, it will be reflected on the statement of cash flows** .
