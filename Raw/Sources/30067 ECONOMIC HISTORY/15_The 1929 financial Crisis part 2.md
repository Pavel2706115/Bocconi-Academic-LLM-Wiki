## USA: HOOVER REACTION

- In the months following the crash the gold standard forced central banks to pursue **restrictive** monetary policies.

- No one thought of implementing expansionary monetary policies .

- and everyone preferred to adopt **spending review measures**

- These defensive strategies produced liquidity crises and **multiple bank failures** (especially in the USA).

- The tight control on capital flows had **negative effects on international trade.**

## USA: HOOVER REACTION

President Hoover, always convinced that the government should not intervene with countercyclical policies, was forced to change his mind in the face of unemployment numbers, but the outcomes were not positive.

- In 1932 ( **Reconstruction Finance Corporation** ), he created a federal fund to be allocated to the largest banks, which were supposed to help the smaller ones in turn. The resources were inadequate. Large banks allowed the smaller ones to fail.

- The FED decided **to lower the discount rate** to expand credit, but banks used liquidity to shore up their own accounts rather than granting credit.

- Hoover also aimed for a **balanced budget** , which could be achieved by reducing service expenses or increasing taxes; both options would further depress the economy

## ECONOMIC ORTHODOXY

- **Balanced budgets** (Fiscal Policy):The core belief that governments must not spend more than they earn(i.e., avoid large, structural budget deficits). Running continuous deficits is seen as fiscally irresponsible and a direct cause of inflation or currency instability.

- **Sound money** (Monetary Policy):The commitment to a stable, non-inflationary currency. Historically, this meant adherence to the Gold Standard or a fixed exchange rate. In modern terms, it means giving the central bank independence with a clear mandate for price stability (low and stable inflation).

- **Fiscal prudence**: Maintaining low levels of public debt relative to national income(GDP).

## THE BEGINING OF THE WELFARE STATE

- The Crisis of 1929 had revealed imperfections in the functioning of market mechanisms.

- State intervention proved indispensable to correct market failures, giving rise to the concept of the welfare state.

-:

- According to the vision of **John Maynard Keynes, states should have adopted countercyclical policies** Governments would have made public spending the engine of economic recovery:

- wages, consumption, and profits would have mutually supported each other (Keynesian multiplier).

- In particular, Keynes criticized Say's Law, which posits that 'supply creates its own demand,' as, in his view, it does not function in a crisis context

## GOODBYE ECONOMIC ORTHODOXY

During the 1930s, more and more countries decided to disengage their currency from gold. In this way, central banks had the freedom to:

- Lower rates

- Print money to stimulate consumption, investment, and employment.

The great crisis challenged economic orthodoxy based on the idea of **market self-regulation** . We have to say that the **dogmatic adherence to the Gold Standard was abandoned more out of necessity than out of conviction**

Monetary policy began to be influenced by domestic policy objectives.

## THE NATIONA INDUSTRIAL RECOVERY ACT

- The National Industrial Recovery Act (NIRA), passed in June 1933, was one of the most ambitious and controversial pieces of legislation enacted during President Franklin D. Roosevelt’s First New Deal.

- The core of the NIRA was the creation of "Codes of Fair Competition" for every major industry.

- These codes, negotiated between business leaders, labor, and the government, were designed to eliminate "cutthroat" competition, which was driving down prices and wages.

- The codes established minimum standards for the entire industry, including:

- Minimum wages and maximum hours for workers.

- Fixed minimum prices and limits on production quotas (to stabilize prices and prevent inventory gluts).

## THE PUBLIC WORK ADMINISTRATION

- Title II of the NIRA established the Public Works Administration (PWA), which was given $3.3 billion to fund large-scale construction projects.

- The PWA's purpose was to create jobs (relief) while also building valuable public goods (recovery).

- It funded huge projects like bridges, dams, hospitals, and schools, providing long-term benefits to the nation's infrastructure.

- It relied on private contractors to carry out the projects, meaning it provided employment indirectly, unlike other New Deal programs that hired workers directly.

- The PWA was highly successful in improving the nation's infrastructure, and many of the buildings and facilities constructed in the 1930s still stand today.

## THE AAA

- The Agricultural Adjustment Act (AAA) passed in May 1933. It represented the federal government's most direct attempt to solve the devastating crisis facing American farmers during the Great Depression

- The fundamental problem was overproduction, which had driven commodity prices (like cotton and wheat) so low that many farmers could not afford to harvest their crops or pay their mortgages.

- The federal government provided subsidies (direct payments) to farmers who voluntarily agreed to reduce their production of "basic crops" (like corn, cotton, tobacco, rice, and wheat).

- The money for these subsidies came from a tax levied on the processors of the crops (e.g., flour millers, meatpackers). This cost was often passed on to consumers.

- In January 1936, the Supreme Court declared the AAA unconstitutional

## THE NEW DEAL PART 2: 1935-37

In 1935 a series of welfare measures and new labour market regulations brought America closer to a social democracy.

- **Social Security Act**: old age pension; disability and unemployment insurance; allowances for widows and children.

- **Revenue Act** of 1935 (the "Wealth Tax") to redistribute wealth by raising taxes on large incomes and corporations

- **National Labour Relations Act** (labour market regulation): obligation for companies to recognise trade unions; introduction of collective wage bargaining.

- **Work Progress Administration**: The goal was to provide immediate, meaningful employment and restore dignity through useful work.
