### Slide 14: APPENDIX: Deriving the formula for Deadweight Loss

**1) Slide elements (verbatim/structured):**

- **Title:** APPENDIX: Deriving the formula for Deadweight Loss
    
- **Text:**
    
    - Change in Q induced by the tax
        
    - Size of the tax
        
    - Tax induced change in quantity
        
- **Visuals (charts/tables/images):** Mathematical equations:
    
    - $DWL=-\frac{1}{2}\times\Delta Q\times\tau$
        
    - $\eta_{s}=\frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P}\times\frac{P}{Q}$
        
    - $\eta_{d}=\frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P+\tau}\times\frac{P}{Q}$
        
    - $\frac{\Delta Q}{Q}=\eta_{s}\times\frac{\Delta P}{P}$
        
    - $\Delta P=\frac{\eta_{d}}{(\eta_{s}-\eta_{d})}\times\tau$
        
    - $\Delta Q=\frac{\eta_{s}\eta_{d}}{(\eta_{s}-\eta_{d})}\times\tau\frac{Q}{P}$
        
- **Labels/footnotes:** None.
    

**2) Explanation of content:**

- **Text explanation:** This slide walks through the mathematical derivation of deadweight loss based on supply and demand elasticities ($\eta_{s}$ and $\eta_{d}$), starting from the basic area of a triangle ($1/2 \times base \times height$).
    
- **Graph/table explanation:** N/A
    
- **Key takeaways from visuals:**
    
    - DWL is fundamentally a function of the change in quantity ($\Delta Q$) and the tax rate ($\tau$).
        
    - The change in quantity is mathematically linked to how elastic supply and demand are.
        

**3) What this slide is really saying (1–3 sentences):**

> This slide provides the mathematical proof that deadweight loss depends on the elasticities of supply and demand, the size of the tax, and the initial price/quantity conditions.

### Slide 16: Determinants of Deadweight Loss

**1) Slide elements (verbatim/structured):**

- **Title:** Determinants of Deadweight Loss
    
- **Text:**
    
    - $DWL=-\frac{\eta_{s}\eta_{d}}{2(\eta_{s}-\eta_{d})}\times\tau^{2}\frac{Q}{P}$
        
    - $\eta_{s}$ and $\eta_{d}$ are the elasticity of supply and demand, $\tau$ is the tax rate, and Q and P are the quantity and price.
        
    - DWL rises with the elasticities of demand and supply
        
    - DWL rises with the square of the tax, so marginal DWL rises with the tax rate.
        
    - Marginal deadweight loss: The increase in deadweight loss per unit increase in the tax.
        
- **Visuals (charts/tables/images):** Bocconi logos.
    
- **Labels/footnotes:** None.
    

**2) Explanation of content:**

- **Text explanation:** This slide interprets the final formula derived on slide 15. The two main takeaways are: highly elastic markets create worse DWL, and raising taxes creates exponentially worse DWL (because of the square function).
    
- **Graph/table explanation:** N/A
    
- **Key takeaways from visuals:** N/A
    

**3) What this slide is really saying (1–3 sentences):**

> The math proves two essential rules for tax policy: taxing elastic goods causes huge inefficiencies, and doubling a tax will quadruple its economic damage. Therefore, small increases to an already high tax rate are incredibly harmful.

**1) Slide elements (verbatim/structured):**

- **Title:** Progressive Tax Systems Can Be Less Efficient: progressive
    
- **Text:**
    
    - When system is replaced with no tax on low-wage workers, there is no DWL for this worker, but the DWL for the high-wage worker increases by the trapezoid GEFI. ## Section: Optimal Commodity Taxation

### Slide 24: Ramsey Taxation: The Theory of Commodity Taxation

**1) Slide elements (verbatim/structured):**

- **Title:** Ramsey Taxation: The Theory of Commodity Taxation
    
- **Text:**
    
    - Optimal commodity taxation: Choosing the tax rates across goods to minimize deadweight loss for a given government revenue requirement.
        
    - Ramsey Rule: To minimize the DWL of a tax system while raising a fixed amount of revenue, taxes should be set across commodities so that the ratio of the marginal deadweight loss to marginal revenue raised is equal across commodities: $\frac{MDWL_{i}}{MR_{i}}=\lambda$
        
    - Value of additional government revenues: The value of having another dollar in the government's hands relative to its next best use in the private sector.
        
- **Visuals (charts/tables/images):** Bocconi logos.
    
- **Labels/footnotes:** None.
    

**2) Explanation of content:**

- **Text explanation:** Defines the Ramsey Rule for optimal taxation. The rule states that a government should set taxes such that the "pain per dollar raised" (the ratio of marginal DWL to marginal revenue) is exactly the same for every single good in the economy.
    
- **Graph/table explanation:** N/A
    
- **Key takeaways from visuals:** N/A
    

**3) What this slide is really saying (1–3 sentences):**

> To raise money efficiently, governments shouldn't just tax everything equally. Instead, they should balance their taxes so that the economic damage caused by squeezing the last dollar out of the coffee market is equal to the damage of squeezing the last dollar out of the car market.

## Section: Optimal Commodity Taxation

### Slide 25: Inverse Elasticity Rule

**1) Slide elements (verbatim/structured):**

- **Title:** Inverse Elasticity Rule
    
- **Text:**
    
    - If supply is infinitely elastic (as in perfect competition), the Ramsey Rule becomes: $\tau_{i}^{*}=-\frac{1}{\eta_{i}}\times\lambda$
        
    - $\tau_{i}^{*}$ is the optimal tax, $\eta_{i}$ is the elasticity of demand, and $\lambda$ is some constant.
        
    - Optimal taxation therefore balances two rules:
        
        - Elasticity rule: Lower taxes on goods with more elastic demand.
            
        - Broad base rule: Better to tax a wide variety of goods moderately than a few goods heavily.
            
- **Visuals (charts/tables/images):** Bocconi logos.
    
- **Labels/footnotes:** None.
    

**2) Explanation of content:**

- **Text explanation:** Simplifies the Ramsey rule to the "Inverse Elasticity Rule." Mathematically, the optimal tax on a good should be inversely proportional to how elastic its demand is. Thus, goods that are highly inelastic (necessities) should be taxed heavily, and elastic goods lightly. Furthermore, it's better to spread taxes across many items than to hit a few items hard.
    
- **Graph/table explanation:** N/A
    
- **Key takeaways from visuals:** N/A
    

**3) What this slide is really saying (1–3 sentences):**

> To maximize efficiency, governments should heavily tax goods that people are forced to buy regardless of price (like basic food or medicine) and lightly tax goods people can easily abandon. Additionally, spreading moderate taxes across many goods prevents the exponential damage caused by extremely high rates on just a few goods.
