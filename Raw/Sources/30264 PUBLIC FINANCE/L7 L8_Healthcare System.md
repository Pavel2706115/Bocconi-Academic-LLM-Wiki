### Topic 1: Healthcare Spending & Global Models
- **Slide 2: Graph - Health Care Spending in the OECD Nations (2022)**
	- **Graph Explanation:** A stacked bar chart comparing health expenditure as a percentage of GDP across OECD countries in 2022. The bars are split into "Government/Compulsory” and “Voluntary/Out-of-pocket” spending
	- **Key Points:**
		- The US spends the most on healthcare by a wide margin (16.6% of GDP), with a significantly larger share coming from voluntary/out-of-pocket spending compared to other nations. Italy sits lower at 9.0% of GDP, heavily reliant on government funding
- **Slide 3: Different models around the World**
	- **Summary:** Outlines the four primary methods which the countries use to structure and finance healthcare
	- **Key Points:**
		- **National health insurance:** Universal access provided by the government and funded by general taxation (e.g., UK, Italy)
		- **Social health insurance:** Universal access through insurance funds, financed by employer and employee payroll contributions, and delivered mostly by private providers (with government-related prices)
		- **Single-payer:** The government both funds (via taxes) and provides the health insurance
		- **Private insurance model:** The private sector funds and provides insurance (e.g., US)
### Topic 2: The US Health Insurance System
- **Slide 4: How Health Insurance Works: The Basics**
	- **Summary:** Explains the core mechanics and patient cost-sharing structures of health insurance
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Individuals (or their employers) pay monthly premiums to insurers, who pay medical providers for the services the individual uses. Patients share costs through Deductibles (a limit to what the individual pays before insurance kicks in), Copayments (a fixed fee per visit), and Coinsurance (a percentage of the total bill)</mark>
- **Slide 5: Table - US Population Across Health Insurance Types**
	- **Summary:** A table breaking down the health insurance coverage of the 324.6 million people in the US
	- **Key Points:** The majority (220.8 million) have private insurance, primarily employment-based (183 million) and the other 33.2 million have direct purchase based insurance. Public insurance covers 110.7 million people across Medicare (58.8 million), Medicaid (55.9 million), and military programs (3.2 million). Roughly 26.1 million remain uninsured
- **Slide 6: Private Insurance**
	- **Summary:** Details the private insurance market in the US
	- **Key points:** In 2019, 69.5% of the US population relied on private insurance. This is obtained either through employers or the “nongroup insurance market.” where families buy coverage directly
- **Slide 7: Why do Employers Provide Private Insurance? (Motive I: Risk Pooling)**
	- **Summary:** Explains how employer-provided insurance solves adverse selection
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Insurers want large “risk pools” to create a predictable distribution of medical risk using the law of large numbers. Pooling all employees together automatically prevents adverse selection (where only the sickest buy insurance)</mark>
- **Slide 8: Why do Employers Provide Private Insurance? (Motive II: The Tax Subsidy)**
	- **Summary:** Explains the financial incentive for employer-provided insurance
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Workers are taxed on standard wage compensation but are not taxed on compensation received as health insurance.</mark> The provided table shows that a worker (Kim) who takes part of their salary as health benefits ends up with more after-tax income compared to a worker (Nigel) who buys personal insurance out of their taxed wages
- **Slide 9: Why do Employers Provide Private Insurance? (The Tax Subsidy Continued)**
	- **Summary:** Clarifies misconceptions about the tax subsidy
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">The subsidy actually benefits the employee, not the employer (since both wages and health benefits are tax-deductible for the firm). To eliminate this tax subsidy, the government would have to count employer health spending as part of the employee's taxable income</mark>
- **Slide 10: The Other Alternative: Nongroup Insurance**
	- **Summary:** Discusses the flaws in the individual private market
	- **Key Points:** Historically, this market functioned poorly. Coverage was not always available, and the sickest individuals were often entirely unable to get coverage or faced incredibly high price due to adverse selection
- **Slide 11: Medicare**
	- **Summary:** Defines the Medicare program
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Medicare is a federal program financed by payroll taxes. It covers citizens over 65 (if they or their spouse worked 10 years in covered employment) and disabled persons under 65</mark>
- **Slide 12: Medicaid**
	- **Summary:** Defines the Medicaid program
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">A joint federal and state program targeting the poor. It covers cash welfare qualifiers, most low-income children and pregnant women, low-income elderly/disabled, and (in expansion states) all very low-income families</mark>
- **Slide 13: TRICARE and CHAMPVA**
	- **Summary:** Defines military health programs
	- **Key Points:** 
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">TRICARE is run by the Dept. of Defense for active/retired military and their families</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">CHAMPVA is for disabled dependents and survivors of veterans</mark>
- **Slide 14: The Uninsured**
	- **Summary:** Profiles the 26 million uninsured Americans and explains why they lack coverage
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Nearly 75% of the nonelderly uninsured are in families with full-time workers and 14.2% are children. They may be uninsured because insurance is too expensive, insurers fear adverse selection and deny them, they don't value the coverage, or they rely on “uncompensated care” (free emergency delivery)</mark>
- **Slide 15: Why Care About the Uninsured?**
	- **Summary:** Lists five reasons why uninsurance is a societal problem
	- **Key Points:** 
		- Reasons include: 
			- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Physical externalities (spread of communicable disease)</mark>
			- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Financial externalities (costs pushed onto the insured)</mark>
			- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Inappropriate care delivery</mark>
			- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Equity concerns</mark>
			- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">“Job lock”</mark>
		- **Job lock:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">when people refuse to switch to a more productive job out of fear they will lose their current health insurance</mark>
### Topic 3: Optimal Insurance & Managed Care
- **Slide 16: How Generous Should Insurance Be to Patients?**
	- **Summary:** Examines the flaws of highly generous insurance policies
	- Key Points: <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">“First-dollar coverage” pays for everything with zero patient cost. This provides very little consumption-smoothing benefit because people can easily self-insure minor risks, but it generates massive moral hazard costs (overuse of care)</mark>
- **Slide 17 & 18: Graphs - Moral Hazard Costs of Health Insurance**
	- **Graph Explanation:** Two supply and demand graphs plotting the price of a doctor's visit against the number of visits
	- **Key Points:** The graphs illustrate the core trade-off: consumption smoothing vs. medical overuse. When insurance artificially lowers the price the patient feels from $100 to $10, demand surges from $Q_1$ to $Q_2$. This overuse creates a “Deadweight loss” because the actual cost of providing the care is much higher than the value the patient receives from it
- **Slide 19: Optimal Health Insurance**
	- **Summary:** Concludes the ideal structure for health insurance for patients
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Optimal insurance balances moral hazard and risk protection. Individuals should bear a large share of their medical costs for minor, affordable issues, and only rely on insurance for catastrophic, unpredictable costs</mark>
- **Slide 20: How Generous Should Insurance Be to Medical Providers?**
	- **Summary:** Examines how providers are paid
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">“Retrospective reimbursement” pays doctors for costs after they have been incurred. This directly incentivizes doctors to overutilize care because they get paid mode for doing more, regardless of necessity</mark>
- **Slide 21: Managed Care**
	- **Summary:** Introduces systems designed to restrict provider supply and control costs
	- **Key Points:**
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Preferred provider organizations (PPOs) lower costs by aggressively shopping for providers on the patient's behalf</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Health maintenance organizations (HMOs) integrate the insurance and the delivery of care by paying doctors</mark>
- **Slide 22: Managed Care and Prospective Reimbursement**
	- **Summary:** Explains the payment structure of managed care
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Managed care uses “Prospective reimbursement,” paying providers a flat rate based on what a treatment should cost, rather than what they actually spend. Because the doctor pockets the difference if the care costs less, this creates an incentive for insufficient care</mark>
### Topic 4: Health Care Reform and the ACA
- **Slide 23 & 24: Health Care Reform (Single-Payer)**
	- **Summary:** Evaluates the pros and cons of moving to a single-payer system
	- **Key Points:**
		- **Pros:**
			- Guaranteed full coverage
			- Low administrative costs
			- Elimination of inequality
		- **Cons:**
			- Dramatically increased government spending
			- Blunt budgeting mechanisms that might restrict access to highly expensive but worthwhile technology
			- Immense political pushback
- **Slide 25: The Massachusetts Experiment**
	- **Summary:** Reviews the state-level reform that inspired Obamacare
	- **Key Points:** 
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">In 2006, MA used the “three-legged-stool” approach:</mark>
			1. <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Ban pre-existing condition exclusions</mark>
			2. <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Require an Individual Mandate (forcing everyone to buy insurance to avoid adverse selection)</mark>
			3. <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Provide subsidized/free insurance for the poor</mark>
		- It successfully dropped the uninsurance rate to 3%
- **Slide 26: The Affordable Care Act (ACA)**
	- **Summary:** Outlines the structure of the “Obamacare”
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Signed in 2010, the ACA scaled the MA model nationally. It mandated community pricing, required individuals to buy insurance, and massively expanded Medicaid to cover all families below 138% of the poverty line</mark>
- **Slide 27: Rising Health Care Costs and Cost Control in the ACA**
	- **Summary:** Explains the ACA's approach to cost containment
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Medical inflation has drastically outpaced regular inflation, largely due to new technology. The ACA attempts to control this by capping employer tax breaks, increasing competition, depoliticizing provider rates and sponsoring comparative effectiveness research</mark>
- **Slide 28-31: Early Evidence on the Effects of the ACA**
	- **Summary:** Analyzes the initial data following the ACA's implementation
	- **Key Points/Graphs:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">The ACA achieved its goal of mass coverage, dropping the uninsured rate precipitously from 18% in 2013 to 11% in 2016. The graph shows a massive spike in coverage for young adults in their early 20s. The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) correctly projected these gains and notes that the law would actually reduce the national deficit</mark>
- **Slide 32-33: Impacts and Trouble for the ACA**
	- **Summary:** Summarizes the successes and political failures of the ACA
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Despite insuring 20 million people, improving health access, and keeping cost growth historically low, the law was deeply unpopular. This unpopularity led to political turnover and allowed Trump administration to repeal  the individual mandate, critically weakening the system</mark>
### Topic 5: The Italian Healthcare System
- **Slide 34: The Italian healthcare system (SSN)**
	- **Summary:** Introduces the Sistema Sanitario Nazionale (SSN)
	- **Key Points:** 
		- Introduced in 1978, the SSN provides free, universal coverage funded through general taxation. It is highly ranked globally for its efficiency. 
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 107, 5, 0.2)">A 1992 reform introduced "quasi market" features, forcing competition between public and private providers</mark>
- **Slide 35: Four Levels of the SSN (Part 1)**
	- **Summary:** Breaks down the top two administrative tiers in SSN
	- **Key Points:** 
		- **Central government (Health Ministry):** Distributes tax revenue and defines the “essential level of care” (LEA) that must be guaranteed nationwide
		- **Regions:** Have significantly autonomy to organize their own systems and deliver care through local units
- **Slide 36: Four Levels of the SSN (Part 2)**
	- **Summary:** Breaks down the local administration and provider payment systems
	- **Key Points:** 
		- **ASL (Local Health Units):** Purchase and deliver actual care on the ground, funded by a fixed reimbursement per patient
		- **Providers:** Public hospitals are funded via prospective payments based on DRGs (Diagnosis Related Groups) - meaning they receive a pre-set fixed payment based on the patient's diagnosis regardless of actual costs. General practitioners (GPs) are paid via capitation (a fixed amount per patient)
