### Topic 1: Introduction to Public Finance & Government Size
- **Slide 1: Why Study Public Finance?**
	- **Summary:** Defines the purpose of the field
	- **Text:** The government plays a central role in modern economies. <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Taxes and spending directly impact economic growth, inequality, and incentives. The ultimate goal of public finance is to understand the proper role of the government in the economy</mark>
- **Slide 2: Why Study Public Finance Today?**
	- **Summary:** Lists contemporary global issues that make public finance highly relevant today
		- Tax reform
		- Inequality
		- Climate policy
		- Aging populations
- **Slide 3: Governments Are Economically Large**
	- **Summary:** Provides context on the sheer scale of government operations
	- **Text:** In advanced economies, public spending accounts for roughly 30% to 55% of the GDP. There are large differences across countries, which reflect their unique institutional and political choices
- **Slide 4: Tax-to-GDP ratios in 2023 and 2024 (as % of GDP)**
	- **Graph Explanation**: A scatter/bar plot ranking countries by their tax revenue as a percentage of GDP, which serves as a proxy for the size of the government
	- **Key Points**: Denmark and France have the highest ratios (near 45%), while countries like Mexico are at the bottom (under 20%). Italy is highlighted, sitting relatively high on the list at over 40%
- **Slide 5: Growth of Government Over Time**
	- **Summary:** Explains how historical trends in government size
	- **Text:** Government size has increased dramatically since the year 1900. This growth is primarily driven by expansions in social insurance, education, and health care
- **Slide 6 & 7: General government expenditures (as % of GDP & per capita)**
	- **Graph Explanation:** Two charts showing public spending across the OECD countries for 2019, 2023, and 2024
	- **Key Points:** Slide 6 shows expenditures as a percentage of GDP, with France, Finland, and Italy (highlighted) leading the pack at or above 50%. Slide 7 adjusts this to show expenditure per capita (in USD PPP), where Luxembourg tops the list at nearly $70,000 per person, while Italy sits closer to the OECD average
### Topic 2: Government Functions & Composition of Spending
- **Slide 8: What Does the Government Do?**
	- **Summary:** Categorize the four main economic activities of the government
	- **Text:** 
		1. Public goods (e.g, Infrastructure, defense)
		2. Redistribution (e.g., Taxes & transfers)
		3. Regulation (e.g., Market rules)
		4. Stabilization (e.g., Managing business cycles)
- **Slide 9: Composition of Public Spending**
	- **Graph Explanation:** A pie chart showing the OECD average government spending by function as a percentage of GDP in 2023
	- **Key Points:** “Social protection” is by far the largest slice at 31.3%, followed by Health (19.6%), Public services (14.0%), and Education (11.4%)
- **Slide 10: Government spending by function as a share of GDP, 2023**
	- **Graph Explanation:** A stacked bar chart comparing the breakdown of government spending across various countries
	- **Key Points:** France spends the most overall (57% of GDP). Italy is highlighted at 54% of GDP, with very large portions dedicated to Social Protection (pensions) and Health, but a visibly smaller portion dedicated to Education compared to peers
### Topic 3: The Four Questions of Public Finance
- **Slide 11: The Four Questions of Public Finance**
	- **Summary:** Introduces the four main core questions that frame the study of public finance
	- **Text:** 
		1. <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">When should the government intervene?</mark>
		2. <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">How should it intervene?</mark>
		3. <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">What are the effects of intervention?</mark>
		4. <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Why do governments intervene the way they do?</mark>
- **Slide 12: 1. When Should the Government Intervene?**
	- **Summary:** Answers the first main question by establishing the two main rationales for intervention
	- **Text:**
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">I. Market Failures:</mark> The market cannot always achieve allocative efficiency (due to public goods, externalities, or asymmetric information). Government intervention can enhance efficiency
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">II. Redistribution:</mark> Even if a market is efficient, the outcome may not be socially desirable or equitable. The government intervenes to shift resources from some groups to others based on equity principles
- **Slide 13: Rising Inequality**
	- **Graph Explanation:** A bar chart showing the “Top 1% income share” across OECD countries
	- **Key Points:** Income inequality has grown, especially at the very top of the distribution. The US has the highest concentration of wealth (the top 1% holds over 20% of the income), while Italy is highlighted near the OECD average at around 11-12%
- **Slide 14: Percentage of total population earning less than 50% of median income**
	- **Graph Explanation:** A chart showing relative poverty rates before taxes/transfers (triangles) and after taxes/transfers (diamonds) for 2021
	- **Key Points:** Taxes and transfers significantly reduce poverty. In Italy (highlighted), the poverty rate before government intervention is around 35%, but drops to roughly 13% after taxes and transfers are applied
- **Slide 15: Household income inequality before and after taxes and transfers**
	- **Graph Explanation:** A chart mapping the Gini coefficient (a measure of inequality where 0 is perfect equality and 1 is maximal inequality) before and after government intervention
	- **Key Points:** Government redistribution drastically lowers the Gini coefficient in every country. Italy is highlighted, showing a drop from roughly 0.48 to 0.35
- **Slide 16 & 17: 2. How Should the Government Intervene?**
	- **Summary:** Answers the second question by listing policy tools
	- **Text:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">The government can use: </mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Taxes & Subsidies</mark> (altering prices to encourage/discourage use)
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Regulation/Mandates</mark> (quotas restrict private sale of goods that are overproduced or mandating purchases of underproduced goods)
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Public Provision</mark> (the government directly provides the goods)
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Public Financing of Private Provision</mark> (the government pays private companies to produce the good)
- **Slide 18: The regulatory role of the government**
	- **Summary:** Provides real-world examples of government regulation
	- **Text:** 
		- The Food and Drug Association (FDA) (US) and European Food Safety Authority (EFSA/EMA) (Europe) regulate food, cosmetics, and drugs
		- The Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) (US) and European Environment Agency (EEA) (Europe) regulate environmental pollution
- **Slide 19 & 20: 3. What are the Effects of the Intervention?**
	- **Summary:** Differentiates between the two types of effects caused by policies
	- **Text:** 
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Direct (Mechanical) Effects:</mark> The <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">predicted impacts</mark> if people *did not* change their behavior (e.g., if there are 20 million uninsured people, universal healthcare mathematically covers 20 million people)
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Indirect (Behavioral) Effects:</mark> The <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">unpredictable impacts</mark> that arise because individuals change their behavior (e.g., if universal healthcare is offered, people might drop their private insurance, meaning the public plan actually has to cover far more than 20 million people)
		- *Empirical public finance heavily focuses on the indirect effects*
- **Slide 21: 4. Why the governments intervene the way they do?**
	- **Summary:** Introduces “Political Economy”
	- **Text:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Governments don't always choose the most efficient or socially desirable outcomes because figuring out what citizens want and matching policies to those desires is incredibly difficult. Political Economy is the theory of how the political process itself produces decisions that affect the economy</mark>
- **Slide 22: General government revenues as a % if GDP**
	- **Graph Explanation:** A bar chart ranking countries by their government revenues
	- **Key Points:** Norway and Finland lead with over 50% revenue to GDP. Italy is highlighted near the top tier, collecting roughly 48% of its GDP in revenues
### Topic 4: Government Finances & Takeaways
- **Slide 23: Structure of general government revenues**
	- **Summary:** A stacked bar chart showing where government money comes from:
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Taxes</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Net social contributions</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Sales</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Grants/Other revenues</mark>
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">In almost all countries, direct Taxes form the largest share.</mark> Italy is highlighted, showing a massive reliance on Taxes and a very significant chunk of revenue coming from Net Social Contributions (which funds its large pension system)
- **Slide 24: General government gross debt as a % of GDP**
	- **Graph Explanation:** A chart showing national debt levels
	- **Key Points & Text:** First, it defines the math:
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Deficit = Spending - Revenues and Debt</mark> = the accumulation of past deficits. The graph highlights Japan and Greece at the extreme top (over 175% and 200% debt-to-GDP respectively). Italy is also highlighted as having the third-highest debt burden, hovering around 140%-150% of its GDP.
- **Slide 25: Government investment as a % of GDP**
	- **Graph Explanation:** A bar chart showing public investment levels (like infrastructure_
	- **Key Points:** Eastern European countries like Estonia and Latvia lead in public investment (over 5-6% of GDP). Italy is highlighted further down the list, investing just over 3% of its GDP back into the economy
- **Slide 26: Key Takeaways**
	- **Summary:** A concluding summary of the entire lecture
	- **Text:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">The government plays a central role in modern economies. There is an ongoing debate about the appropriate size and scope of this activity. Public finance is the analytical framework used to study the costs, benefits, and consequences of these government interventions</mark>
