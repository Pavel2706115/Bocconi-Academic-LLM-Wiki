### Slide 6: Intertemporal choice model (1)

**Slide elements (verbatim/structured):**

- **Title:** Intertemporal choice model (1)
    
- **Text:**
    
    - Suppose Minh lives for two periods.
        
    - Period 1 is his working life, during which she earns income Y.
        
    - Period 2 is her retirement, during which she earns nothing.
        
    - She chooses how much to consume while working $(c^W)$ and while retired $(c^R)$.
        
    - Savings is the difference between income and first-period consumption: $S = Y - c^W$.
        
    - Savings earn interest, and Minh consumes her net savings: $(1 + r)S = c^R = (1 + r)(Y - c^W)$.

The equations show that her retirement consumption is entirely dependent on what she doesn't spend while working, multiplied by the interest rate that money earns over time.

### Slide 9: Substitution and Income Effects of Taxes on Savings

**Slide elements (verbatim/structured):**

- **Title:** Substitution and Income Effects of Taxes on Savings
    
- **Text:**
    
    - Taxes and other price changes affect savings in two ways:
        
    - Substitution effect: Lower after-tax interest rates cause first-period consumption to rise, reducing savings.
        
    - Income effect: Lower after-tax interest rates reduce the lifetime value of income, reducing first-period consumption and increasing savings.
        
    - Substitution effects may seem more natural, but a "target savings" model generates complete income effects.
        
- **Visuals (charts/tables/images):**
    
    - Bocconi University logo.
        
- **Labels/footnotes:** Slide number 9.
    

**Explanation of content:**

- **Text explanation:** This slide defines the two opposing forces triggered by a tax on savings. The substitution effect means that because saving pays less, people decide to just spend their money now. The income effect means that because the tax makes people poorer over their lifetime, they realize they must cut back on current spending and save more just to get by in the future. The slide notes that if someone has a strict goal (a "target") for retirement, the income effect dominates because they are forced to save more to reach that exact target.

- **Key takeaways from visuals:** Not applicable.
    

**What this slide is really saying (1–3 sentences):**

This slide defines the internal conflict caused by taxes on savings. People might save less because the reward is worse (substitution), or they might save more out of fear that they won't have enough money in the future due to the tax penalty (income).

- **Text:**

Conversely, the income effect highlights the reality that people have lifetime needs; feeling poorer across the board forces them to tighten their belts today, which mathematically means putting more money away.
