### Topic 1: Introduction to Externalities and Market Failures
- **Slide 1 & 2: Title and Outline**
	- **Summary:** Introduces the lecture on Externalities and Public Goods, starting with externality theory and private-sector solutions
- **Slide 3: Introduction to Externalities: Global Warming**
	- **Graph Explanation:** A line graph titled “Global average temperature” showing a steady upward trend in global average temperature from 1880 to 2020 (rising from roughly 56.8 degrees Fahrenheit (13.78 °C) to over 58.2 degrees Fahrenheit (14.56 °C)
	- **Key Points:** 
		- 2015, Paris, France - representatives from 195 nations met to negotiate an international pact to limit global avg. temp. rise
		- Carbon dioxide emissions drive Global Warming. It highlights the immense cost required to reduce fossil fuel use to safe, preindustrial levels
- **Slide 4: Externalities: Problems and Solutions**
	- **Summary:** Defines the core terminology of the chapter
	- **Key Points:** An **externality** occurs when one party's actions affect another party (positively or negatively), but the first party neither bears the cost nor receives the benefits. This causes a market failure, meaning the market economy fails to deliver perfectly efficient outcome
- **Slide 5: The Four Types of Externalities**
	- **Summary:** A matrix categorizing externalities into four types
	- **Key Points:** 
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Positive Production Externality:</mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Social Marginal Cost (SMC) is below Private Marginal Cost (PMC) (e.g., R&D investment)</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Positive Consumption Externality: </mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Social Marginal Benefit (SMB) is above Private Marginal Benefit (PMB)(e.g., vaccines)</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Negative Production Externality: </mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Social Marginal Cost (SMC) is above Private Marginal Cost (PMC) (e.g., steel production)</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Negative Consumption Externality: </mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Social Marginal Benefit (SMB) is above Private Marginal Benefit (SMB)(e.g., smoking)</mark>
- **Slide 6: An example: Negative production externality**
	- **Graph Explanation:** A supply and demand graph for steel. The private supply curve ($S=PMC$) intersects the demand curve ($D = PMB = SMB$) at point A($Q_1$). However, the true Social Marginal Cost curve ($SMC = PMC + MD$) sits higher up. The socially optimal intersection is at point C($Q_2$)
	- **Key Points:** Because the factory does not pay for its pollution (Marginal Damage, MD), it overproduces steel by the amount $Q_1 - Q_2$. This overproduction creates a **Deadweight Loss** to society, represented by the shaded triangle BCA

### Topic 2: Private-Sector Solutions to Externalities
- **Slide 7: The Solution**
	- **Summary:** Introduces <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">the concept of “internalizing the externality”</mark>
	- **Key Points:** This happens <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">when private negotiations or government actions force a party to fully bear the external costs or benefits of their actions</mark> (e.g., the steel producer directly paying fishers for the damage caused to the river)
- **Slide 8:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">The Coase Theorem</mark>
	- **Summary:** Explains Ronald Coase's theory on private market solutions
	- **Key Points:** 
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Part I: If property rights are well-defined and bargaining is costless, private parties can negotiate to reach the socially optimal quantity without government intervention</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Part II: The efficient solution will be reached regardless of which party is initially assigned the property rights</mark>
- **Slide 9: The Solution: Coasian Payments**
	- **Graph Explanation:** Updates the steel graph from Slide 6. It shows the private supply curve ($PMC_1$) shifting upward by the exact amount of the Coasian payment to become $PMC_2$. This perfectly aligns private costs with social costs ($SMC$), moving the market equilibrium from the inefficient Point A to the efficient Point B
	 ![[Pasted image 20260316121555.png]]
- **Slide 10 & 11: The Problem with Coasian Solutions 1 & 2**
	- **Summary:** Outlines <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">four reasons why Coasian bargaining often fails in the real world</mark>
	- **Key Points:** 
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Assignment problem: </mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">It is difficult to assign blame and determine who pays whom</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Holdout problem: </mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">If property rights are shared, any single owner has veto power and can demand enormous payouts</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Free rider problem: </mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Individuals will underinvest their own money if they can still enjoy the common benefits of others' investments</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Transaction/Negotiating costs: </mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">It is nearly impossible to coordinate negotiations when billions of parties are involved (like with global warming)</mark>
- **Slide 12: Bottom Line**
	- **Summary:** Concludes the Coase Theorem's utility
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">While brilliant in theory, it only practically applies to small-scale, localized externalities (like a dispute between two neighbors). It fails to solve large-scale, global externalities</mark>
### Topic 3: Public Goods Taxonomy and Optimal Provision
- **Slide 13 & 14: Public Goods Taxonomy**
	- **Summary:** Classifies goods based on two properties: Excludability and Rivalry
	- **Key Points:** 

|                         | Rival good                            | Non-Rival good                      |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| **Excludable good**     | Private good (ice cream)              | Impure public good (TV streaming)   |
| **Non-excludable good** | Impure public good (crowded sidewalk) | Pure public good (national defense) |

- **Slide 15-17: Optimal Provision & Summation Graphs**
	- **Summary:** Explains how to find total market demand for private vs. public goods
	- **Key Points & Graphs:** 
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Horizontal Summation (Slide 16):</mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)"> Used for private goods (like ice cream).</mark> Because goods are rival, you <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">sum the quantities (Q) (x-axis)</mark> demanded by each individual at a given price to find the total market demand
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Vertical Summation (Slide 17):</mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)"> Used for public goods (like missiles).</mark> Because goods are non-rival (everyone enjoys the exact same missile), you <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">sum the willingness to pay (the P) (y-axis)</mark> of each individual at a given quantity to find the total Social Marginal Benefit (SMB)
### Topic 4: Private and Public Provision of Public Goods
- **Slide 18 & 19: Private-Sector Underprovision**
	- **Summary:** Explains why markets fail to provide public goods
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">The “free rider problem” causes individuals to underinvest because they can reap the benefits of others' investments without paying. Private providers can sometimes overcome this by charging the user fees proportional to a person's valuation of the good (Lindahl's pricing model)</mark>
- **Slide 20 & 21: Altruism, Social Capital, and Warm Glow**
	- **Summary:** Explores psychological reasons why people privately fund public goods
	- **Key Points:** 
		- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">The free-rider problem won't lead to a complete absence of private provision of public goods because of three major behavioral mechanisms:</mark>
			- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Altruism</mark>
			- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Social Capital</mark>
			- <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Warm Glow</mark>
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Altruistic:</mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)"> When individuals value the benefits and costs to others in making their consumption choices</mark>
			- Many laboratory experiments provide evidence for altruism and show that people contribute to public goods
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Social Capital:</mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)"> The value of altruistic and communal behavior in society</mark>
			- The quantity of social capital depends on how much people of the same community affected by the public good can “trust” each other and are therefore willing to chance their personal investment of time and effort into paying for the public good without any formal guarantee of reciprocity from other community members
		- <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Warm Glow Model:</mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)"> A model of the public goods provision in which individuals care about the total amount of the public good and their particular contributions as well</mark>
- **Slide 22-26: Public Provision and the Crowd-out Problem**
	- **Summary:** Examines the government's role and its side effects
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Government provision solves the “noncontributor” problem via taxes.</mark> However, this can lead to **Crowd-out**: as the government spends more on a public good, private citizens donate less. Full crowd-out is rare; partial crowd-out is common because the act of forcing noncontributors to pay increases total resources, and the “warm glow” effect that keeps contributors donating which accounts for the part of the private citizens' donations. Studies (like Kingma's public radio study) show mixed results on the exact size of this crowd-out
- **Slide 27-29: The Right Mix & Measuring Costs/Benefits**
	- **Summary:** Discusses implementation and evaluation challenges
	- **Key Points:** Governments often use <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Contracting out (hiring private firms to build public goods)</mark>, but this risks misaligned incentives and noncompetitive bidding. Furthermore, conducting Cost-Benefit Analysis is highly complex. (e.g., Should wages for building a highway be counted as a strict cost, or as a social benefit if the workers were previously unemployed? How do you put a monetary value on the lives saved by a safer road?)
### Topic 5: Lindahl Pricing and Unanimous Consent
- **Slide 30 & 31: Lindahl Pricing Mechanics**
	- **Summary:** Introduces a theoretical model for funding public goods
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">Under Lindahl pricing, the government announces tax prices, individuals state how much they want, and the government aggregates this to find where total Marginal Willingness to Pay equals Marginal Cost. Each person is then charged exactly their state willingness to pay</mark>
- **Slide 32 & 33: Graphical Representation & Why It Works?**
	- **Graph Explanation:** Three graphs showing Ava's willingness to pay (a), Jack's willingness to pay (b), and their combined vertical summation (c). The optimal quantity of fireworks is there their combined willingness to pay curve intersects the flat Marginal Cost curve (MC)
	- **Key Points:** <mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)">It works perfectly in theory because it acts as a for of Benefit Taxation— people are taxed exactly according to the benefit they receive</mark>
- **Slide 34: Problems with Lindahl Pricing**
	- **Summary:** Explains why this is never used in reality
	- **Key Points:** 
		1. <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Preference revelation:</mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)"> People will lie and understate their willingness to pay a lower tax bill</mark>
		2. <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Preference knowledge:</mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)"> People often don't actually know their exact monetary valuation of a public good</mark>
		3. <mark style="background:rgba(5, 117, 197, 0.2)">Preference aggregation:</mark><mark style="background:rgba(240, 200, 0, 0.2)"> It's practically impossible to combine millions of individual preferences mathematically</mark>
