## Section: Taxation and Risk Taking

### Slide 6: Basic Financial Investment Model

#### 1) Slide elements (verbatim/structured)

- **Title:** Basic Financial Investment Model
    
- **Text:** How do taxes on risk taking affect behavior? Taxing gains and deducting losses increase risk taking because any tax on returns can be completely undone by taking more risk. Consider a $100 investment opportunity that pays out $120 half the time and $80 half the time. This has an expected return of zero. Expected return: The return to a successful investment times the odds of success, plus the return to an unsuccessful investment times the odds of failure.
    
- **Visuals:** None.
    
- **Labels/footnotes:** Università Commerciale Luigi Bocconi.
    

#### 2) Explanation of content

- **Text explanation:** This slide introduces a counter-intuitive economic theory: taxing investment gains while allowing deductions for losses actually encourages risk-taking. It provides a baseline example of a $100 investment with a 50/50 chance to make or lose $20, resulting in an expected return of zero.
    
- **Graph/table explanation:** N/A
    
- **Key takeaways from visuals:** N/A
    

#### 3) What this slide is really saying

If the government shares in your investment losses (through tax deductions) as well as your gains, it effectively acts as a silent partner, encouraging you to take bigger risks to achieve your desired payout.

### Slide 7: Some Taxes on Risk Taking Can Be Undone

#### 1) Slide elements (verbatim/structured)

- **Title:** Some Taxes on Risk Taking Can Be Undone
    
- **Text:** In row 1, Betsey makes a $100 investment that yields $20 if the investment is successful and -$20 if the investment fails. In row 2, the government imposes a 50% tax on her investment, so that Betsey now earns only $10 if successful and loses $10 if the investment fails. Betsey can undo the government's actions, as in row 3, by doubling her investment to $200, leaving her with the same returns as in row 1, so that taxation has increased risk taking.
    
- **Visuals (Table 23-1):**
    
    - **(1) No tax:** Inv $100 | Win Payoff $20 | Lose Payoff -$20 | Tax 0 | Ded. 0 | After-Tax Win $20 | After-Tax Loss -$20
        
    - **(2) Tax:** Inv $100 | Win Payoff $20 | Lose Payoff -$20 | Tax 50% | Ded. 50% | After-Tax Win $10 | After-Tax Loss -$10
        
    - **(3) Loss offset:** Inv $200 | Win Payoff $40 | Lose Payoff -$40 | Tax 50% | Ded. 50% | After-Tax Win $20 | After-Tax Loss -$20
        
- **Labels/footnotes:** Università Commerciale Luigi Bocconi.
    

#### 2) Explanation of content

- **Text explanation:** The text narrates the table. If a 50% tax is applied symmetrically to gains and losses, an investor's net exposure is halved. To maintain the original potential $20 upside, the investor simply doubles the amount invested to $200, thereby increasing overall risk-taking.
    
- **Graph/table explanation:** The table numerically proves the text. Row 3 shows that doubling the gross investment (and thus gross risk) restores the after-tax payoff and loss profile of Row 1.
    
- **Key takeaways from visuals:**
    
    - Symmetrical taxation of gains and losses acts like government insurance.
        
    - Investors can easily neutralize proportional taxes if full loss offsets exist.
        

#### 3) What this slide is really saying

Under a perfectly symmetrical tax system with full loss deductions, taxing investments paradoxically drives people to invest more money to reach their original profit goals.

## Section: Capital Gains Taxation

### Slide 16: What Are Capital Gains?

#### 1) Slide elements (verbatim/structured)

- **Title:** What Are Capital Gains?
    
- **Text:** Capital gain: Difference between an asset's purchase price and its sale price. Example: Buy stock at €100, Sell at €140 → Capital gain = €40. Key distinction: Interest income is taxed on accrual. Capital gains are taxed on realization (when the asset is sold).
    
- **Visuals:** None.
    
- **Labels/footnotes:** Università Commerciale Luigi Bocconi.
    

#### 2) Explanation of content

- **Text explanation:** Defines a capital gain as the profit from selling an asset (Sale Price - Purchase Price). It introduces a critical mechanical difference in tax law: standard interest is taxed as it grows (accrual), but capital gains are only taxed when the asset is physically sold (realization).
    
- **Graph/table explanation:** N/A
    
- **Key takeaways from visuals:** N/A
    

#### 3) What this slide is really saying

A capital gain is simply the profit made from selling an asset, but the crucial detail is that the government only taxes you when you actually sell, not while the asset is sitting in your portfolio gaining value.

### Slide 17: Accrual vs. Realization

#### 1) Slide elements (verbatim/structured)

- **Title:** Accrual vs. Realization
    
- **Text:** Taxation on accrual: Pay taxes each period on the return earned in that period. → clean, theoretically efficient, but harder to implement. Taxation on realization (current system): Pay taxes only when selling the asset. → creates timing incentives and strategic behavior.
    
- **Visuals:** None.
    
- **Labels/footnotes:** Università Commerciale Luigi Bocconi.
    

#### 2) Explanation of content

- **Text explanation:** Contrasts the two tax timing methods. Accrual taxation is economically sound but practically difficult (e.g., taxing a house's value increase every year without a sale). Realization taxation is easier to manage but incentivizes people to strategically delay selling to defer paying taxes.
    
- **Graph/table explanation:** N/A
    
- **Key takeaways from visuals:** N/A
    

#### 3) What this slide is really saying

Because the government chooses the easier administrative route of taxing assets only when they are sold (realization), it accidentally encourages investors to play games with when they buy and sell to minimize taxes.

### Slide 18: Lock-In Effect

#### 1) Slide elements (verbatim/structured)

- **Title:** Lock-In Effect
    
- **Text:** Capital gains taxes create an incentive to delay selling appreciated assets. Why? Tax is paid upon realization, so delaying payment increases the present value of after-tax returns. Consequences: Reduced market liquidity, Inefficient allocation (people hold assets too long), Tax preferences distort portfolio decisions.
    
- **Visuals:** None.
    
- **Labels/footnotes:** Università Commerciale Luigi Bocconi.
    

#### 2) Explanation of content

- **Text explanation:** Defines the "Lock-In Effect": investors refuse to sell profitable assets to avoid triggering a tax bill. Delaying the tax keeps more capital working for the investor. This harms the broader economy by freezing markets (reduced liquidity) and preventing money from flowing to newer, more productive investments.
    
- **Graph/table explanation:** N/A
    
- **Key takeaways from visuals:** N/A
    

#### 3) What this slide is really saying

Taxing capital gains only at the time of sale traps money in old investments, as people hold onto assets just to avoid paying the tax, preventing capital from flowing efficiently through the economy.

### Slide 23: Evidence on Taxation and Capital Gains 3

#### 1) Slide elements (verbatim/structured)

- **Title:** Evidence on Taxation and Capital Gains 3
    
- **Text:** The figure (Agersnap and Zidar, 2020) shows the relationship between the "event time," which is set at zero at the point at which states change their capital gains taxation, and the "net of tax" rate (one minus the tax rate). The figure demonstrates that capital gains realizations respond to tax changes. However, their calculations suggest a long run elasticity of capital gains with respect to the tax rate of -0.3 to -0.5, fairly inelastic. The authors conclude that increasing the capital gains tax rate would lead to net increases in revenue.
    
- **Visuals (Figure 23-1):** An event study line chart showing "Log real capital gains relizations by state".
    
    - **Y-axis:** Ranges from -6 to 10.
        
    - **X-axis:** "Event time" from -10 to 10.

- **Labels/footnotes:** Worth Publishers. Università Commerciale Luigi Bocconi.
    

#### 2) Explanation of content

- **Text explanation:** Explains an economic study measuring how sensitive investors are to state-level tax changes. "Inelastic" (-0.3 to -0.5) means that while people react to the tax change, the reaction isn't large enough to offset the new tax rate. Therefore, raising the rate actually generates more total revenue for the government.
    
- **Graph/table explanation:** The event-study chart isolates the effect of a tax change at "Time 0". It visually proves that realizations jump immediately following the event, establishing a causal relationship between tax policy and investor behavior.
    
- **Key takeaways from visuals:**
    
    - Tax changes cause immediate and measurable shifts in investor selling behavior.
        

#### 3) What this slide is really saying
