## ✓ Other arbitrage examples

- a) same asset trades with different prices on two different exchanges b) two assets with identical cash flows & risk but different prices c) an asset with a known price in the future does not trade today at the price discounted by the risk-free interest rate

## Arbitrage (3/4)

## Short selling and arbitrage

EX: you know with certainty (private information) that an asset is overpriced, and price will fall in the future

- ✓ _Strategy: sell something you don’t have, betting on expected price change_

1. you borrow the asset from someone for a rental fee at _t=0_ 2. sell it at _t=0 (say S0)_ 3. buy it again in the future ( _t=1_ ) when it has a lower price (say: _S1 <S0_: _this is your bet!_ )

4. return the asset ( _t=1_ ) to its rightful owner and pay rental fee! `o` profit = _S0 – S1 –_ rental fee

✓ _Arbitrage by short-selling has corrective effect on the price_ `o` selling of the asset by short-sellers reduces its price `o` soon enough, the arbitrage opportunity will have been eliminated

## Arbitrage (4/4)

- ✓ _Short-selling is also used for risky speculation_

`o` quite risky w.r.t to buying a risky asset and resting on its limited liability `o` essentially your bet on a price change in both cases, but with short selling. earn/lose a lot: quite risky! Indeed:

- potential **gain**: limited to the spot price of the asset (in case the asset becomes entirely worthless: S=0)

- **loss** un

- potential limited: you have to buy back and return the asset, no matter what it costs at that point ( _S1 >>S0 !)_

## Arbitrage and EMH

- ✓ Arbitrage opportunities cannot survive for long in a competitive market

- there are other investors who will see a profit and take advantage of it

- if you found one opportunity: very lucky!

- ✓ … The fact that there are few arbitrage opportunities

- … tells you that there are few securities whose price does not reflect all publicly available info (otherwise there would be an easy arbitrage opportunity)

## This is the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)

## EMH definition

# Definition of **EMH (semi-strong form)** ✓ “The security’s price reflects **all available public information** at all times”

✓ this hypothesis implies that the price of a stock in the future is not predictable from any information that is public knowledge (else, there would be arbitrage opportunities) ✓ it does **NOT** say that you cannot profit from private information: that is not incorporated in the stock price

## 1 Random Walk of Stock Prices

- ✓ If EMH is right, prices should be a random walk ✓ That is, **future changes in stock prices should be unpredictable**

- ✓ Random walk technically means that the best predictor for tomorrow’s price is **today’s price**

- why is this “random”? Because if today is your best guess for tomorrow then you really have no idea about tomorrow

- 𝑃𝑡+1 = 𝑃𝑡 + 𝑒𝑡 where: 𝑒𝑡 is random error with zero mean and finite variance, it reflects the new information (unpredictable) arriving between _t+1_ and _t_ .

- Δ𝑃 = 𝑃 −𝑃 = 𝑒 𝑡+1 𝑡+1 𝑡 𝑡 . In other words, changes in stock prices have the same Δ𝑃

- distribution, are independent of each other (4 ex.: 𝑡+1 is independent of past Δ𝑃 or Δ𝑃

- price changes, say 𝑡 𝑡−2 , etc…)

- ✓ Many studies find evidence on random walk behavior of stocks using stock market data

## 2 Un/Anticipated Announcements

- ✓ A positive (negative) but **anticipated** announcement about a company* will **not** affect (raise  or decrease) the price of its stock

- ✓ Why? because this information was already reflected in the stock price (it was anticipated  … already priced in)

- ✓ **Un** _anticipated_ announcements will **affect** the stock price, but there should be a full immediate adjustment to the new price under EMH

## Can’t beat the market

# ✓ If EMH is right, investment analysts and mutual funds should not be able to consistently **beat the market**

- ✓ It means that buying the shares they suggest should not be better than just buying a basket of the market as whole

## EMH Implications

## EMH does **not** imply that:

1.: … prices **do not change**

`o` as new information comes in, securities are re-evaluated

2. … a **rational** investor always gets the price right:

`o` only that his/her **expectations** are correct (“on average” he/she is right)

3. … a zero average market returns:

`o` earn a “fair” return in equilibrium, based on the riskiness of the security

## Evidence Against EMH

**A. January effect** ✓ Stock prices fall in December and rise in January `o` **Abnormal positive return** in the month of January that is predictable and, hence, inconsistent with random-walk behavior

## Evidence Against EMH

## B. Small-firm effect

✓ Many empirical studies have shown that **small firms** have earned **abnormally high returns** over long periods of time, even when the greater risk for these firms has been considered `o` this is a challenge for EMH because no stock should consistently exhibit excess returns

## Evidence Against EMH

- **C. Market overreaction** and **momentum:**

- ✓ sometimes stock prices **overreact to news announcements** ( _overshooting_ )

- ✓ sometimes prices _continue to rise_ for quite some time after a positive news ( **momentum** )

- ✓ Momentum strategies: sell stocks that have had bad -

- returns and buy those with high returns in the last 3 12 months

## Evidence Against EMH

- **D. Excessive volatility:**

- ✓ EMH implies that prices basically reflect all and only **market fundamentals** (PV of future dividends)

- ✓ However, stocks appear to have **excessive volatility** compared to volatility of fundamentals

- ✓ _4 ex._: Shiller found that fluctuations in the S&P 500 could not be justified by the subsequent fluctuations in the dividends of the stocks making up this index

- ✓ Shiller concluded that there must be factors other than fundamentals affecting stock prices

## Behavioral Finance

- ✓ _New_ field that includes concepts from psychology, sociology, and other social sciences to understand investors and patterns of securities prices

- Shiller seminal works, Richard Thaler won the 2017 Nobel prize

## ✓ _Loss aversion_

- loss aversion ( _feel worse when you lose than better when you gain_ ) may explain why so few people ever go short on a stock: normally when going short (borrow to sell), you first see losses on your position which doesn’t make you feel good about it

## ✓ _Overconfidence_

- people tend to be overconfident in their own decisions (almost everybody thinks of himself as an above-average investor). Investors overconfidence may perpetuate stock price bubbles

## ✓ _Herd behavior_

## Behavioral Finance: Evidence

- ✓ La Porta (1996): expectations of **stock market analysts** have predictive power for future stock returns

- ✓ Result: companies whose analysts are **most optimistic** about earn **poor returns** relative to alternative companies

## ✓: Interpretation

- analysts (and investors who follow them) are **too optimistic** about stocks with **rapidly growing earnings**

- **over**

- they estimate the probability that the firm is the **next Google** based on recent data (but “real” Googles are actually very rare)

## Outside of Academia

## ✓ Warren Buffett does not believe in EMH. Why?

- because he proved he can beat the market! 19.7% per year for the last 49 years (9.8% for S&P)
