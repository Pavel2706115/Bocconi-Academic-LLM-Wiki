## Government Safety Net: Deposit Insurance

Why does **DI** prevent bank failures? Because it prevents **bank runs** !

- ✓ A _bank run_ is a systemic event where many depositors withdraw their money at the same time

`o` we saw in Lect. 15 that banks can sustain some deposit outflows if they have _ample reserves_ `o` once reserves are depleted, they need to sell other assets to raise money (or borrow from others) `o` but many assets are illiquid (loans)!

- ✓ A perfectly solvent bank may go bankrupt because of a **liquidity** problem created by massive withdrawals

## Government Safety Net: Deposit Insurance

- ✓ Bank runs at **both** bad and good banks:

✓ failure of one hastens failures at other banks ( _**contagion** effect_ ) ✓ “ _First-come, first-served_ ” model gives incentives to withdraw first

## Government Safety Net: Deposit Insurance Implementation

✓ _Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation_ ( _FDIC_ ) `o` Government agency in the US that guarantees deposits up to $25k `o` $100k before 2008, but _unlimited_ in case of Signature Bank and SVB in 2023

`o` insurance fund paid by banks themselves (like an insurance premium). Similar system in other countries (EX: Italy <=€100k) `o` if money runs out (there’s not enough to pay all deposits at all banks, of course), the government pays

✓ DI can stop bank failures with no need for paying $: `o` it prevents runs by acting on _**expectations**_

✓ DI stops `o` not only _individual_ risk of loss in case the bank fails `o` but also _aggregate_ risk: _bank runs_ from happening in the first place!

## Deposit Insurance and Moral Hazard

✓ DI is great! It can stop bank failures by stopping runs!

✓ Well…it does have a cost: lack of market discipline

- ✓ Problem:

`o` if depositors’ money is insured by the government …. `o` they will not worry about bank solvency

✓ Banks covered by DI face less scrutiny from creditors. They _gamble_ with depositors’ money and take on more risks: **moral hazard**

- ✓ Logic:

- _all gains to private owners_

## Deposit Insurance and Moral Hazard

- ✓ Suppose a bank invests in very risky assets

- ✓ Moral hazard is **why** _deposit insurance_ is _limited_ in scope (<$250k or <$100k, etc…)

- ✓ Another problem with the government safety net is the creation of the “ **too big to fail** ” problem:

`o` some FIs become so large … too big for the gov.t to let them fail (cost would be tremendous!)

`o` markets may think that the government will always bail out (rescue) such a bank, at any cost to the taxpayer ... `o` FI know this and take advantage: they grow in size and take on excessive risks (moral hazard)

- ✓ **Too-connected-to-fail**

- similar issue arises if the bank is _too tightly connected_ to other FI’s

- `o` contagion after a bank failure would have disastrous effects

## Question motivating capital requirements

✓ Suppose you have to invest all your (and your parents’) wealth in two assets:

1. Asset A has a return of 5% for sure

2. Asset B has a return of +20% with 80% probability, and a return of -80% with 20% probability

## **Q**: Which asset would you choose?

**A**: Asset B has an expected return of

E(RB) = 20% × 0.8 + (−80%) × 0.2 = 0

while asset A has a return E(RA) = 5%

So, if you had to buy the asset with your own money, you would probably prefer asset A over asset B

## Capital Requirements: History

✓ How much capital is enough? Hard to know in practice

✓ Through most of the 1980’s, regulators used a simple minimum **leverage ratio**: E leverage ratio= A

`o` (changed notation: E=NW)

- ✓ Simple scale depending on the leverage ratio:

– > 5%: **well** -capitalized

- > 4%: **adequately** capitalized

– < 4%: **under** capitalized

- < 3%: **significantly** undercapitalized

- < 2%: **critically** under-capitalized (to be shut down)

✓ If bank capital fell short of any of these thresholds, there would be restrictions to what the bank could do

## Capital Requirements: Basel Accord

✓ What is Basel all about?

`o` _**risk weights**_ applied to _all_ assets and _off-balance sheet_ activities `o` these weights reflect the degree of risk and are used to calculate **risk-weighted assets** ( **RWA** ) ^4k3ntt

- ✓ RWA are calculated by multiplying each asset i by its risk weight and then adding them up:

N RW × A i i RWA= \sum i=

✓ Bank **capital requirements** ( CapReq ) are then specified as a percentage of its RWA, rather than total assets:

E CapReq= RWA(≥8%)

✓ CapReq are what matters to determine bank level of risk

## How are the risk weights calculated?

- ✓ **Basel I** just assigned a RW to each category

- ➢ 0% (no capital needed) on reserves and government debt of advanced economies

- ➢ 20% on securities from _corporates_ rated ≥ AA-

- ➢ 50% on municipal bonds, residential mortgages

- ➢ 100% on loans to consumers and corporations

Simple rule, but 2 problems: ✓ Risk weights for each asset class: too coarse and ad-hoc ✓: Banks engaged in _**regulatory arbitrage**_

`o` invest in the riskiest asset within the same risk weight class - Ex: would an Italian bank in 2012 buy German bond yielding 1% or Italian govt. bonds yielding +7%? They were assigned the same 0% risk-weight anyways!!

## Capital Requirements: Basel Accord

- ✓ 2007–2009 crisis revealed further problems of Basel II

`o` it did _not_ require _enough capital_ to weather the Financial Crisis (many banks were insolvent!)

`o` it relied on _credit ratings_ for weights — which proved unreliable during the crisis

- _procyclical_ requirement: since the RWs increase during a bad time, it requires more capital from the bank exactly when it is most short (due to write-offs)

- ✓ **Basel III** is again trying to fix these issues

- Emphasis on quality of capital (on numerator rather than denominator)

- Less procyclical: _countercyclical buffers_ to be filled in good times when credit grows excessively
