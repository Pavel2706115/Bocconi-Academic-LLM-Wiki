## Underwriting Stocks and Bonds: 3. Asymmetric Information

The IB also help solve an _**asymmetric information**_ problem when a firm issues stocks/bonds:

- ✓ investors don’t know the true value of the firm …

- they could _research_ the company, but it is costly

- they do not trust the company itself to reveal honest and accurate information

- ✓ … but they _trust_ the IB (reputation)

- ✓ the firm contacts the IB about the _initial interest_ in the issue of a particular security

- ✓ if the IB says yes, it is implicitly guaranteeing the quality of the issue to the public.

## Underwriting Stocks and Bonds: 3. Asymmetric Information

So IBs have **reputational capital** at stake

- ✓ It is a _repeat business_ , so their income also depends on customer returning and on investors’ trusting the IB judgement

- ✓ The IB is incentivized to do _**due diligence**_ on the issues they distribute to the public

- ✓ If IB lose investors’ confindence, their ability to make deals is _severely impaired_

## The Underwriting Process

## Two main types of underwriting

## **1. Traditional (Firm Commitment) Underwriting** _IB buys full issue at a pre-determined price and then distributes the issue_

`o` the firm is _guaranteed_ the price for its shares

`o` the IB profit is the difference between

- the price paid to the issuer ( **purchase price** _)_ - and the price received in the secondary market ( **offer price** _)_

`o` since this is quite risky for IB, usually IB ask other IBs to join and form a **syndicate** - EX. for Facebook IPO the syndicate was: MS, GS, BofA ML, JP Morgan, Allen & Company

## The Underwriting Process

**2. Best-effort underwriting no** price guarantee, only distribution

- the firm is not guaranteed a price because the IB does not purchase nor

- the entire issue ( guaranteed that the issue can be sold entirely)

- the IB gets a commission for selling securities - lower than fees with firm commitment

- the IB has less incentives to do due diligence because it is not putting its reputation at risk

- in best-effort underwriting the IB is no longer certifying the issuance quality

- not good for firms with substantial amount of asymmetric information (better choose traditional underwriting)

**The Underwriting Process** Traditional (Firm Commitment) Vs Best-effort

**Q1**: best-effort underwriting is mostly used for IPO or SEOs? **A1**: SEO. There is a price already on the secondary market and Asy Info less of an issue than with IPO

**Q2**: which method do you think is better for small or less known firms?

**A2**: traditional underwriting is better: with this method IB certifies the issuance quality, and thus reduces Asy Info for firms where these problems are more relevant

**Q3**: which method creates more conflict of interest over price? **A3**: IB does not want shares to be overpriced, especially with traditional method, because the risk of not reselling at appropriate offer price is on IB

## The Underwriting Process

- ✓ The goal of underwriting is to **fully subscribe** (all of the shares have been spoken for)

- ✓ Both cases below are not good outcomes for the issuing company and for the IB

**1. Undersubscribed**: underwriting syndicate unable to generate interest in all of the available shares

- IBs can lose huge amounts of money: need resell at a lower price …

**2. Oversubscribed**: interest in more shares than are available

- not good either: IB should have set a higher price

- issuing firm may feel uneasy about having received less than its fair value

## Conflicts of Interest

**Conflict of interest** is a type of _moral hazard_ that arises when there are multiple interests, and serving one interest is detrimental to the other

`o` the company benefits from an _optimistic review_

Serving one (say, investors) damages the other (company)

## Pricing the IPO

## ✓ Usually, IPOs are “underpriced”

- prices **rise** significantly right after the stocks start trading in the secondary market

- by about 17% on average in the US and UK

- there are exceptions, like Facebook

- Look (optional): “On May 18, 2012 Facebook opened for trading at 11:30 a.m. slightly above the offer price of $38 per share. During the first sixty seconds of trading, over 158 million shares changed hands and the price ranged from a low of $42 to a high of $45. The high price for the day of $45 happened within the first minute of trading.”

- ✓ This means that the issuer potentially leaves significant amounts of money “on the table”

- ✓ Markets (and issuing companies) have come to like underpriced IPOs as a _signal of “quality”_ of IPO
